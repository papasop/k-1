\title{\textbf{Realizability and the Origin of Causality}}

\begin{abstract}
Zeno's paradox implicitly assumes that spatial displacements carry irreducible positive
cost at every scale. We negate this: if spatial displacement sequences exist whose
cost-to-magnitude ratio vanishes (Assumption~R), while pure temporal displacements
retain positive cost (Assumption~T), then in $(1{+}1)$ dimensions any quadratic
leading-order expansion of the cost function is indefinite---provided the quadratic
form is nondegenerate, an explicit condition that is separately required.
After normalisation,
\[
Q(\delta t,\delta r) = \delta t^{2} - c_{\max}^{-2}\,\delta r^{2}.
\]
Lorentzian signature therefore emerges from the stated asymmetry assumptions
together with nondegeneracy, rather than from any a priori metric or causal cone.
Among nondegenerate quadratic forms, Lorentzian signature is the unique possibility
under the stated assumptions: positive-definite is excluded by Assumption~R together
with the Lemma, and negative-definite by Assumption~T. In $(1{+}3)$~dimensions, under
a strengthened three-directional realizability condition together with a 3D nonpositive
coherence condition and nondegeneracy of~$Q$, the only possible signature is
$\mathrm{Sig}(Q)=(1,3)$.

The present derivation differs from the EPS axiomatisation~\cite{EPS} in its starting
point: no metric, causal cone, particles, or light rays are presupposed; only a scalar
cost function together with temporal positivity and spatial realizability (strengthened
in 4D to a coherence condition). This provides an alternative axiomatic route, at a
more formal level than kinematic approaches, to the Lorentzian signature presupposed
by the thermodynamic derivation of Einstein's equations~\cite{Jacobson}.
\end{abstract}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Lorentzian signature $\cdot$ Causality $\cdot$ Cost function $\cdot$ Quadratic form $\cdot$ Constructive axiomatics

\section{Introduction}

We show that in $(1{+}1)$ dimensions a Lorentzian causal structure follows from
a minimal asymmetry between temporal and spatial displacement costs together with
a quadratic leading-order expansion; in $(1{+}3)$ dimensions an additional spatial
coherence hypothesis is required. This asymmetry forces the associated quadratic
form to be indefinite, yielding Lorentzian causal structure without assuming any
metric or \textit{a priori} causal cone. The closest precursor is the EPS axiomatisation~\cite{EPS}; unlike EPS,
no particles, light signals, or worldlines appear as primitives---only a scalar
cost function together with temporal positivity (Assumption~T) and spatial
realizability (Assumption~R; in 4D strengthened to a coherence condition, Assumption~R4).\footnote{This paper distinguishes \emph{kinematic} approaches
(which take free-fall, light propagation, or causal structure as primitive) from
\emph{cost-based} approaches (which derive both the metric and its signature from
a scalar cost function). Entropic gravity approaches~\cite{Verlinde2011} presuppose
Lorentzian spacetime and derive gravitational dynamics; the present work operates
at the prior level of deriving the Lorentzian signature itself.}
Whereas Jacobson's derivation~\cite{Jacobson} presupposes a Lorentzian
spacetime, the present work operates at the prior level of deriving the
Lorentzian signature pointwise; the integration of this pointwise structure
into the global causal geometry Jacobson presupposes lies outside the present scope.

The title warrants a brief clarification. By \textit{origin of causality} we mean
only the algebraic origin of Lorentzian causal signature --- a logical rather than
a physical origin, and specifically not the origin of global causal structure,
causal stability, light-cone propagation, or worldline realisability. We identify
a minimal set of conditions on a cost function from which Lorentzian signature
follows as the unique algebraic consequence: Assumptions~R, E, T, together with
nondegeneracy of~$Q$. The physical origin of the cost asymmetry itself lies
outside the scope of the present paper.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Main Theorem (informal).}
Under Assumptions~R, E, T, and nondegeneracy of~$Q$, the leading quadratic
form~$Q$ is Lorentzian in $(1{+}1)$ dimensions:
\[
Q(\delta t,\delta r) = \delta t^{2} - c_{\max}^{-2}\,\delta r^{2}, \qquad c_{\max}>0,
\]
up to normalisation and an invertible linear change of variables. In $(1{+}3)$
dimensions, under R4, E4, T, and nondegeneracy of~$Q$, the only possible signature
is $\mathrm{Sig}(Q)=(1,3)$.

The precise setup, statements, and proofs are given in Sections~2--4.

\section{Setup}

\noindent\textbf{Notation.}
$d(x;\delta x)\ge 0$: displacement cost at~$x$ for displacement~$\delta x$.
$Q$: the leading-order quadratic form of~$d^{2}$ (Assumption~E).
$\mathrm{Sig}(Q)=(p,q)$: inertia of~$Q$ ($p$~positive, $q$~negative eigenvalues).
$(u)_{+}:=\max\{u,0\}$: positive part.
$c_{\max}>0$: maximal propagation speed encoded in~$Q$.
$c_{t}>0$: lower bound on temporal cost per unit displacement (Assumption~T).
$\|\cdot\|$: any norm on the ambient space ($\mathbb{R}^{2}$ in Section~3,
$\mathbb{R}^{4}$ in Section~4); all conclusions are norm-independent.
$S^{1}=\{u\in\mathbb{R}^{2}:\|u\|=1\}$: unit circle (used in Section~3).

\medskip
Let $U\subset\mathbb{R}^{2}$ be open with chart $(t,r)$, and fix $x\in U$. For small
$\delta x=(\delta t,\delta r)$ with $\delta t>0$, the cost $d(x;\delta x)\ge 0$ is
defined. The chart variables $t$ and $r$ are mere labels: $t$ labels a direction
that will be required to carry positive cost (Assumption~T below), and $r$ labels
its complement. No metric or causal structure on~$U$ is assumed at this stage;
in particular, the conclusion that $t$ plays the role of a timelike direction in
the resulting Lorentzian form is a \emph{consequence} of the framework, not an input.

\begin{definition}[R --- Zero-threshold realizability]\label{def:R}
There exists a sequence $\delta x_{n}=(\delta t_{n},\delta r_{n})\to 0$ with
$\delta t_{n}>0$, $\delta r_{n}\neq 0$ such that
\[
\frac{d(x;\delta x_{n})}{\|\delta x_{n}\|}\longrightarrow 0,
\]
and there exists a subsequence $v_{n_{k}}\to v=(v_{t},v_{r})$ with $v_{t}>0$ and
$v_{r}\neq 0$, where $v_{n_{k}}:=\delta x_{n_{k}}/\|\delta x_{n_{k}}\|$.
\end{definition}

The condition $\delta t_{n}>0$ restricts attention to forward-in-time displacements.
Requiring the same condition for $\delta t_{n}<0$ would strengthen the assumption
without changing the conclusion: the proof uses only one null direction, which
the single-sided condition already provides. The sign choice itself, however,
is a choice of time orientation rather than a loss of generality: the symmetric
$\delta t_{n}<0$ version yields a time-reversed statement with the resulting
null vector on the opposite leaf of the null cone. We fix $\delta t_{n}>0$
as a convention throughout.

Assumption~E formalises the leading-order structure of the cost function within which Assumption~R operates.

\begin{definition}[E --- Quadratic expansion]\label{def:E}
There is a quadratic form $Q(\delta t,\delta r)=a\,\delta t^{2}+2\kappa\,\delta t\,\delta r+b\,\delta r^{2}$
on~$\mathbb{R}^{2}$ such that
\[
d(x;\delta x)^{2} = \bigl(Q(\delta x)\bigr)_{+} + o\!\left(\|\delta x\|^{2}\right) \quad\text{as } \delta x\to 0 \text{ with } \delta t>0,
\]
where $(u)_{+}:=\max\{u,0\}$.
Here $Q$ is an algebraic object on the full linear space~$\mathbb{R}^{2}$, determined
by its three coefficients; Assumption~E only controls the asymptotic behaviour of
$d^{2}$ on the half-plane $\{\delta t>0\}$, whereas the inertia $\mathrm{Sig}(Q)$
analysed in Sections~3--4 is a property of~$Q$ as a quadratic form on all
of~$\mathbb{R}^{2}$.
The positive part ensures compatibility with $d\ge0$: the quadratic form~$Q$ may take
negative values along directions where $Q<0$, but the squared cost~$d^{2}$ cannot.
The form~$Q$ is uniquely determined: on the nonempty open set
$\{\delta t>0,\,Q>0\}$ the truncation is inactive, and a quadratic form is
determined by its values on any nonempty open subset of~$\mathbb{R}^{2}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[T --- Temporal cost]\label{def:T}
There exists $c_{t}>0$ such that for all sufficiently small $\Delta t>0$,
\[
d\bigl(x;(\Delta t,0)\bigr)\ge c_{t}\,\Delta t.
\]
\end{definition}

\noindent The three assumptions are summarised as follows:
Definition~\ref{def:R} (Assumption~R) provides a zero-cost spatial direction;
Definition~\ref{def:E} (Assumption~E) establishes the quadratic leading-order structure;
Definition~\ref{def:T} (Assumption~T) fixes the positive cost of pure temporal displacement.

\section{Main result in $(1{+}1)$ dimensions}

\begin{lemma}[Null--Sign equivalence]\label{lem:null}
Assume~E. If $\delta x_{n}\to 0$ with $\delta t_{n}>0$,
$d(x;\delta x_{n})/\|\delta x_{n}\|\to 0$, and a subsequence of
$v_{n}:=\delta x_{n}/\|\delta x_{n}\|$ converges to $v\in S^{1}$, then $Q(v)\le 0$.
Conversely, if $Q(v)\le 0$ and $\delta x_{n}\to 0$ satisfies $\delta t_{n}>0$ and
$\delta x_{n}/\|\delta x_{n}\|\to v$, then $d(x;\delta x_{n})/\|\delta x_{n}\|\to 0$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
\textit{Forward:} By~E,
$(d(x;\delta x_{n_{k}})/\|\delta x_{n_{k}}\|)^{2}=(Q(v_{n_{k}}))_{+}+o(1)\to 0$,
so $(Q(v))_{+}=0$.

\textit{Converse:} Let $v_{n}:=\delta x_{n}/\|\delta x_{n}\|$, so $v_{n}\to v$.
Since $Q$ is continuous and $Q(v)\le 0$, we have $(Q(v_{n}))_{+}\to 0$.
By homogeneity of~$Q$,
\[
\bigl(Q(\delta x_{n})\bigr)_{+} = \|\delta x_{n}\|^{2}\bigl(Q(v_{n})\bigr)_{+} = o\!\left(\|\delta x_{n}\|^{2}\right).
\]
Hence Assumption~E gives $d(x;\delta x_{n})^{2}=o(\|\delta x_{n}\|^{2})$,
so $d(x;\delta x_{n})/\|\delta x_{n}\|\to 0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Lorentzian signature from realizability]\label{thm:lorentz}
Assume R, E, T, and that $Q$ is nondegenerate.
Then $Q$ is indefinite, and in suitable linear coordinates (obtained by rescaling
and an invertible linear change of variables so that $Q(1,0)=1$),
\[
Q(\delta t,\delta r) = \delta t^{2} - c_{\max}^{-2}\,\delta r^{2}
\]
for some $c_{\max}>0$. The null set consists of exactly two directions,
$\delta r/\delta t=\pm c_{\max}$, in these coordinates.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\textbf{Step~1 (Null direction from~R).}
By~R, there exists a subsequence $v_{n_{k}}\to v\in S^{1}$ with
$v_{t}>0$, $v_{r}\neq 0$. By Lemma~\ref{lem:null}, $Q(v)\le 0$.

\textbf{Step~2 (Temporal direction positive).}
If $Q(1,0)\le 0$ then~E gives $d(x;(\Delta t,0))=o(\Delta t)$,
contradicting~T. Hence $Q(1,0)>0$.

\textbf{Step~3 (Null vector by IVT).}
Define
\[
\gamma(s):=\frac{(1-s)(1,0)+s\,v}{\|(1-s)(1,0)+s\,v\|}, \qquad s\in[0,1].
\]
Since $v_{r}\neq 0$ (Assumption~R), the denominator is nonzero for all $s\in[0,1]$,
so $\gamma$ is well-defined. Since $v_{t}>0$, the numerator has strictly positive
$t$-component throughout, so $\gamma$ is a continuous path in
$S^{1}\cap\{u_{t}>0\}$. Then $Q(\gamma(0))=Q(1,0)>0$ and $Q(\gamma(1))=Q(v)\le 0$,
so by the intermediate value theorem there exists~$s^{*}$ with $Q(\gamma(s^{*}))=0$.
Set $w:=\gamma(s^{*})$; then $w_{t}>0$. Since $Q(w)=0$ but $Q(1,0)>0$,
$w\neq(1,0)$, so $w_{r}\neq 0$.

\textbf{Step~4 (Signature and normalisation).}
By Step~2, $Q$ takes a positive value; by Step~3, $Q$ vanishes on a nonzero
vector~$w$ with $w_{t}>0$. Since $Q$ is nondegenerate (hypothesis), a nondegenerate
quadratic form on~$\mathbb{R}^{2}$ that takes both a positive value and zero on a
nonzero vector cannot be definite; hence $Q$ is indefinite, and in two dimensions
$\mathrm{Sig}(Q)=(1,1)$. Rescaling~$t$ so that $Q(1,0)=1$ and applying an
invertible linear change of variables gives
\[
Q(\delta t,\delta r) = \delta t^{2} - c_{\max}^{-2}\,\delta r^{2}.\qedhere
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[The speed $c_{\max}$]
The constant $c_{\max}$ is not an independent postulate but arises from the cost
structure. It represents the maximal propagation speed, determined by the relative
coefficients of~$Q$. $c_{\max}$ may vary from point to point; the present result
is local. A global constant $c_{\max}$ would follow from additional homogeneity
assumptions on the cost function, which are not required here.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Physical interpretation of $d$]
The cost function $d(x;\delta x)$ is treated here as an abstract primitive.
Its physical identification---whether as an information-theoretic cost, a
thermodynamic work function, or otherwise---lies outside the present scope.
The derivation requires only the three structural properties R, E, and~T
together with nondegeneracy of~$Q$, not a specific physical origin.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Why nondegeneracy is an independent hypothesis]\label{rem:nondeg}
Both Theorem~\ref{thm:lorentz} and Theorem~\ref{thm:4d} take nondegeneracy
of~$Q$ as an explicit hypothesis. In $(1{+}1)$ dimensions, ruling out degeneracy
from Assumptions~R, E, T alone is not possible: the degenerate form
$Q(\delta t,\delta r)=(\delta t-\lambda\,\delta r)^{2}$ satisfies all three
assumptions for any $\lambda\neq 0$.
(Its kernel is spanned by $u=(\lambda,1)$ with $u_{t}=\lambda\neq 0$, so~$u$
itself serves as the null realizer required by~R, while $Q(e_{t})=1>0$ supplies~T.)

One might attempt to rule out degeneracy by the following argument: degeneracy
together with $Q(e_{t})>0$ should imply, in suitable coordinates,
that $Q(\delta t,\delta r)=a\,\delta t^{2}$,
forcing any null vector to have $t$-component zero and thereby contradicting the
$w_{t}>0$ conclusion of Step~3. This argument fails because the reduction
$Q(\delta t,\delta r)=a\,\delta t^{2}$ holds only when the kernel vector $u$ satisfies $u_{t}=0$; the
example above shows that the kernel can have $u_{t}\neq 0$, in which case no
contradiction arises. Nondegeneracy is therefore a genuinely independent
condition, not derivable from R, E, T.
\end{remark}

\section{Extension to $(1{+}3)$ dimensions}

For the four-dimensional extension, let $U\subset\mathbb{R}^{4}$ be open, fix
$x\in U$, and consider the chart $(t,x^{1},x^{2},x^{3})$; write
$e_{t}=(1,0,0,0)$ for the temporal basis vector. The two-dimensional case is the
minimal realisation: a single spatial direction suffices. To address the
$(3{+}1)$-dimensional case of physical interest, we impose a three-directional
realizability condition together with a nonpositive spatial coherence condition.
The paper does not explain why physical spacetime should supply exactly such a
3D nonpositive sector; it only shows what follows if it does.

Definition~\ref{def:R4} (Assumption~R4) strengthens the two-dimensional
Assumption~R to three independent spatial directions.
Definition~\ref{def:E4} (Assumption~E4) extends the quadratic expansion to~$\mathbb{R}^{4}$.

\begin{definition}[R4 --- Three-directional zero-threshold realizability]\label{def:R4}
For each $i\in\{1,2,3\}$ there exists a sequence $\delta x_{n}^{(i)}\to 0$ with
$\delta t_{n}^{(i)}>0$ and $\delta x_{n}^{(i),i}\neq 0$, such that
$d(x;\delta x_{n}^{(i)})/\|\delta x_{n}^{(i)}\|\to 0$, and a subsequence of
normalised directions converges to $v^{(i)}$ with $v_{t}^{(i)}>0$ and
$v_{i}^{(i)}\neq 0$. The three limiting directions $\{v^{(1)},v^{(2)},v^{(3)}\}$
are required to span a three-dimensional subspace~$W$, and the restriction
$Q|_{W}$ is required to be negative semidefinite:
\[
Q(w)\le 0 \quad\text{for all } w\in W.
\]
Here $\delta x_{n}^{(i),i}$ denotes the $i$-th spatial component of the
displacement~$\delta x_{n}^{(i)}$.
\end{definition}

The negative-semidefinite condition on~$W$ strengthens the spanning requirement:
it guarantees a three-dimensional subspace on which $Q$ is negative semidefinite,
thereby ruling out signatures such as $(2,2)$. This condition is strictly stronger
than pointwise vanishing at the three directions~$v^{(i)}$: it requires $Q\le 0$
on all linear combinations of those directions, a coherence condition on the
spatial sector as a whole. In $(1{+}1)$ dimensions, Assumption~R alone suffices;
the negative-semidefinite condition is the additional structural input required
for $(1{+}3)$. Why the spatial dimension is three, and why the target structure
supplies such a 3D nonpositive sector, are independent questions outside the scope here.

\begin{definition}[E4 --- Quadratic expansion in 4D]\label{def:E4}
There is a quadratic form $Q$ on~$\mathbb{R}^{4}$ such that
\[
d(x;\delta x)^{2} = \bigl(Q(\delta x)\bigr)_{+} + o\!\left(\|\delta x\|^{2}\right) \quad\text{as } \delta x\to 0 \text{ with } \delta t>0.
\]
The algebraic-object caveat of Definition~\ref{def:E} applies verbatim, with
$\mathbb{R}^{2}$ replaced by $\mathbb{R}^{4}$: E4 controls only $\{\delta t>0\}$,
while $\mathrm{Sig}(Q)$ is a property of~$Q$ on all of~$\mathbb{R}^{4}$.
\end{definition}

Assumption~T carries over unchanged.

\begin{theorem}[Lorentzian signature --- $(1{+}3)$D]\label{thm:4d}
Assume R4, E4, and~T, and suppose that $Q$ is nondegenerate.
Then $\mathrm{Sig}(Q)=(1,3)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\textbf{Step~1 (Temporal direction positive).}
Assumption~T implies $Q(e_{t})>0$ by the same argument as Step~2 of
Theorem~\ref{thm:lorentz}: if $Q(e_{t})\le 0$ then
$d(x;(\Delta t,0,0,0))=o(\Delta t)$, contradicting~T. Hence the positive
inertia index of~$Q$ is at least~1.

\textbf{Step~2 (Negative inertia index at least~3).}
By Definition~\ref{def:R4}, the limiting directions
$\{v^{(1)},v^{(2)},v^{(3)}\}$ span a three-dimensional subspace $W\subset\mathbb{R}^{4}$
on which $Q(w)\le 0$ for all $w\in W$. We claim that the negative inertia index
of~$Q$ is at least~$\dim W=3$.

Indeed, let $\mathrm{Sig}(Q)=(p,q)$ with $p+q=4$ (by nondegeneracy of~$Q$), and
let $V_{+}\subset\mathbb{R}^{4}$ be a maximal positive-definite subspace of~$Q$,
so $\dim V_{+}=p$. Any $w\in W\cap V_{+}$ satisfies both $Q(w)\le 0$
(since $w\in W$) and $Q(w)\ge 0$ with equality only at~$0$ (since $V_{+}$ is
positive definite); hence $W\cap V_{+}=\{0\}$. By the dimension formula,
\[
\dim W + \dim V_{+} = \dim(W+V_{+}) + \dim(W\cap V_{+}) \le 4 + 0,
\]
so $3 + p \le 4$, giving $p\le 1$ and therefore $q = 4-p \ge 3$.

\textbf{Step~3 (Inertia count).}
Since $Q$ is nondegenerate on~$\mathbb{R}^{4}$, the positive and negative inertia
indices sum to~4. The positive index is at least~1 (Step~1) and the negative
index is at least~3 (Step~2); hence the only possibility is
$\mathrm{Sig}(Q)=(1,3)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Nondegeneracy in the $(1{+}3)$D theorem]
Theorem~\ref{thm:4d} assumes nondegeneracy of~$Q$ as a hypothesis, matching
Theorem~\ref{thm:lorentz}. In four dimensions, ruling out degeneracy
from Assumptions~R4, E4, T alone requires additional input (e.g.\ realizability
along a fourth independent direction). The nondegeneracy hypothesis in
Theorem~\ref{thm:4d} is therefore an explicit scope condition, not a hidden
assumption. See Remark~\ref{rem:nondeg} for the $(1{+}1)$-dimensional analogue
and the explicit degenerate example $Q(\delta t,\delta r)=(\delta t-\lambda\,\delta r)^{2}$ illustrating why
this hypothesis cannot be dispensed with.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Exclusion of non-Lorentzian signatures]
Under the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:lorentz} in $(1{+}1)$ dimensions, and of
Theorem~\ref{thm:4d} in $(1{+}3)$ dimensions, Lorentzian signature is the unique
possibility among nondegenerate quadratic forms.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{In $(1{+}1)$ dimensions}, within the class of nondegenerate forms
the two remaining non-Lorentzian alternatives are excluded as summarised in the
abstract: positive-definite by Assumption~R together with Lemma~\ref{lem:null},
and negative-definite by Assumption~T (Step~2 of Theorem~\ref{thm:lorentz}).
Degenerate forms lie outside this class by hypothesis, an independent condition
not derivable from R, E, T (Remark~\ref{rem:nondeg}).

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{In $(1{+}3)$ dimensions} (Theorem~\ref{thm:4d}), the additional case
requiring attention is the split signature $(2,2)$: it is excluded by the
negative-semidefinite subspace condition of~R4, which together with nondegeneracy
forces the negative inertia index of~$Q$ to be at least~3 (Step~2 of
Theorem~\ref{thm:4d}), incompatible with $(2,2)$. The remaining non-Lorentzian
signatures $(4,0)$, $(0,4)$, and $(3,1)$ are excluded by the inertia count of
Steps~1--3.

\smallskip
Within the stated scope, Lorentzian signature is therefore not merely compatible
with the cost asymmetry --- it is the unique outcome.
\end{remark}
