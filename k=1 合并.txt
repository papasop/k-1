\begin{abstract}
From Newton's absolute time through proper time in general relativity, time enters physical theory as a primitive. Redefining it as the cost of structural change and requiring that the cost structure admit persistent configurations while one direction retains positive cost forces a $2\times 2$ metric $G$ to be indefinite; the sign of $\det G$ then simultaneously determines Lorentzian signature, real symplectic spectrum, and a nontrivial stability boundary $d_{c}>0$ (Theorem~4). The metric arises as the Hessian of an information time $dt_{\mathrm{info}}=d\Phi/H$; the quadratic form $K(x)=x^{\top}Gx$ measures local temporal cost, and the condition $K=1$ --- the metric reproducing its own normalisation --- requires that this cost equal unity. The evolution law $\dot{x}=(J_{G}-D)\nabla V$ is unique (Theorem~5), and at critical damping the restoring rate is $\kappa_{K}=4d_{c}$ for a normalised $G$-eigenvector (Theorem~6).

Under local thermal equilibrium, the Clausius relation is equivalent to $\sigma^{2}=2d_{c}\cdot T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ (Proposition~7). The $\{K=1\}$ manifold carries two classes of fluctuation: thermodynamic (Class~A, perpendicular to $\{K=1\}$) and gauge (Class~B, tangential boost diffusion). Within Class~A the Clausius relation $\delta Q=T_{\mathrm{eff}}\,\delta S$ follows algebraically; the local noise temperature is position-dependent, determined by the metric through $\|Gx\|$. The sole external input is the identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$.

Promoting $K{=}1$ to the field level, we prove that for static spherically symmetric spacetimes with symplectic eigenvalues $\sigma_1=r\sqrt{f}$ and $\sigma_2=r$, the eigenvalue self-consistency principle
$\sigma_2^2\,\Box\ln\sigma_i=1$ for each~$\sigma_i$
is algebraically equivalent to the vacuum Einstein equations $R_{\mu\nu}=0$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:field}), without thermodynamic input. Axiom~T forces $\sigma_1>0$ (Proposition~\ref{prop:sigma1}), excluding singularities from the theory's domain; the OU variance condition yields an internal resolution bound $\sigma_1\gtrsim\ell_P$ (Proposition~\ref{prop:domain}).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction: time as a derived quantity}

In Newton's mechanics, time is a uniform background coordinate; in general relativity, it is the proper time defined by a Lorentzian metric whose signature is prescribed. The Jacobson derivation~\cite{Jacobson} obtains the Einstein equations from thermodynamics on local Rindler horizons, but Lorentzian signature is still assumed. The EPS axiomatisation~\cite{EPS} derives causal structure from free-fall and light propagation, but requires kinematic primitives. Padmanabhan~\cite{Padmanabhan} and Verlinde~\cite{Verlinde} proposed related thermodynamic and entropic approaches. In every approach to Riemann's open problem --- the origin of the metric --- either the signature is prescribed or the derivation requires kinematic primitives.

Here time is neither coordinate nor metric primitive: it is the cost of structural change, and the metric --- including its Lorentzian signature --- is a consequence. This redefinition resolves the question in the $2\times 2$ setting natural to a single local Rindler horizon: an information time $dt_{\mathrm{info}}:=d\Phi/H$ defines a metric $G=\nabla^{2}(dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2})$, and the requirement that physical configurations persist forces $\det G<0$ (\S2; see also~\cite{Realizability}); Lorentzian signature, symplectic structure, and $d_{c}>0$ are then algebraic consequences of a single sign. The quadratic form $K(x)=x^{\top}Gx$ measures local temporal cost; departures from $K{=}1$ generate a restoring dynamics (Law~III), making this self-referential condition a dynamical attractor. Geometrically, $\{K{=}1\}$ is the unit hyperboloid of~$G$.

The framework reaches the Einstein equations by two routes:
\begin{itemize}
\item[\textbf{A.}] \emph{Thermodynamic bridge} (\S\S2--7): $K{=}1\to\mathrm{OU}\to\mathrm{Clausius}$; combined with $S\propto A$ and the Jacobson argument~\cite{Jacobson}, this yields $G_{\mu\nu}=8\pi T_{\mu\nu}$ for general metrics.
\item[\textbf{B.}] \emph{Direct algebraic route} (\S\ref{sec:field}): the eigenvalue self-consistency principle $\sigma_2^2\Box\ln\sigma_i=1$ gives $R_{\mu\nu}=0$ for spherically symmetric metrics, with no thermodynamic input.
\end{itemize}
Route~A is general but requires $S\propto A$; Route~B is independent but currently limited to spherical symmetry. The singularity structure (\S\ref{sec:singularity}) and scope (\S\ref{sec:discussion}) are discussed at the end.

The main results are: (i)~$d_{c}>0$ iff $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$ (Theorem~4); (ii)~unique evolution law (Theorem~5); (iii)~$\kappa_{K}=4d_{c}$ at critical damping (Theorem~6); (iv)~Clausius relation from stochastic dynamics within Class~A (Proposition~7); (v)~$\sigma_2^2\Box\ln\sigma_i=1\Leftrightarrow R_{\mu\nu}=0$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:field}); (vi)~$\sigma_1>0$ from Axiom~T (Proposition~\ref{prop:sigma1}).

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Notation.} $\mathrm{spec}(A)$: spectrum of $A$. $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(p,q)$: inertia ($p$ positive, $q$ negative eigenvalues). $J=\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}0&1\\-1&0\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$: canonical symplectic matrix. $\|\cdot\|$: Euclidean norm on~$\mathbb{R}^{2}$. $\nabla$: Euclidean gradient on~$\mathbb{R}^{2}$.

\section{Metric from information time (Law~I)}

\noindent\textbf{Law~I.} \textit{The metric $G$ is a non-degenerate symmetric bilinear form measuring the local cost of temporal displacement, where `temporal' denotes the direction distinguished by positive cost \textup{(Assumption~T of~\cite{Realizability})}.}

\medskip

The information time $dt_{\mathrm{info}}:=d\Phi/H$, where $\Phi$ parametrises structural variation and $H>0$ is an entropic resistance, provides the metric through its Hessian: the local quadratic approximation $dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2}\approx \delta x^{\top}G\,\delta x$ defines $G=\nabla^{2}(dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2})$; the condition $K(x):=x^{\top}Gx=1$ then identifies the self-consistent locus. The redefinition enters the theorems through $G$ alone: $dt_{\mathrm{info}}$ serves as the motivating construction for~$G$, which enters the theorems as the leading-order form~$Q$ of~\cite{Realizability}. The Hessian $G=\nabla^{2}(dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2})$ has the structure of an information metric --- it measures the local cost of displacement, as the Fisher metric measures the local cost of statistical distinguishability~\cite{Amari} --- but with Lorentzian rather than Riemannian signature. The specific nature of~$\Phi$ is not required by the theorems; what is required is that some directions carry vanishing cost --- equivalently, that the cost structure not be definite (Theorem~3). The theorems follow from the asymmetry, not from the identity of the varying structure.

The cost structure of $dt_{\mathrm{info}}$ satisfies the three realizability conditions of~\cite{Realizability}. Assumption~E (quadratic expansion) holds because $G=\nabla^{2}(dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2})$ exists by the smoothness of~$\Phi$ and~$H$. Assumption~T (positive temporal cost) holds by construction: the temporal direction is defined as the direction along which $dt_{\mathrm{info}}>0$, so $G(e_{t},e_{t})>0$. For Assumption~R (zero-threshold spatial directions), suppose instead that $G$ were positive definite; then $K(x)=x^{\top}Gx>0$ for all $x\neq 0$, and the gradient flow $\dot{x}=-\nabla K$ drives every configuration to $x=0$: no nontrivial structure persists. Since the framework requires persistent configurations, $G$ cannot be positive definite. Combined with $G(e_{t},e_{t})>0$, the only possibility for a non-degenerate $2\times 2$ form is $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$. A rigorous derivation from the three conditions alone, independent of the information-time construction, is given in~\cite{Realizability}.

Lorentzian signature therefore emerges from the cost structure: $G$ is indefinite, with light cones (the null set $\{x:x^{\top}Gx=0\}$) and a causal structure determined by the sign of~$K$. In general relativity, the Lorentzian metric is prescribed on a pre-existing differentiable manifold; here $G$ and its causal structure are derived.

\section{Symplectic structure from $G$}

\begin{theorem}[Structural Uniqueness]
Let $G$ be a non-degenerate symmetric $2\times 2$ real matrix. Among all matrices $M$ such that
\textup{(i)}~$GM$ is skew-symmetric and \textup{(ii)}~$M|_{G=I}=\alpha J$,
the unique solution is $J_{G}=\alpha G^{-1}J$.
\end{theorem}

Here $\alpha>0$ is a normalisation constant fixed by condition~(ii); all subsequent results are homogeneous in $\alpha$, and $\alpha=1$ is adopted wherever $d_{c}$ is compared with thermal quantities. Since $G$ is dimensionless in the information-time parametrisation, $J_{G}=\alpha G^{-1}J$ is also dimensionless.

\begin{proof}
$\dim(\mathrm{Skew}_{2})=1$, so condition~(i) gives $GM=\beta J$, hence $M=\beta G^{-1}J$; condition~(ii) fixes $\beta=\alpha$.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Non-degeneracy vs.\ signature]
Theorem~1 requires only $\det G\neq 0$; it holds for any signature. The Lorentzian condition enters decisively only in Theorem~2, through the reality of the symplectic spectrum. Since $GJ_{G}=\alpha J$ is skew-symmetric, $J_{G}$ lies in the Lie algebra $\mathfrak{sp}(2,\mathbb{R})$; the Lorentzian condition forces $J_{G}$ to be a hyperbolic element, whose real eigenvalues yield $d_{c}>0$.
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}[Spectral Survival]
Let $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$. The characteristic polynomial of $G^{-1}J$ is $\lambda^{2}+1/\det G=0$. Since $\det G<0$, the roots are real: $\lambda=\pm\sqrt{-1/\det G}$. The stability boundary is
\begin{equation}
d_{c}(\alpha)=\alpha\sqrt{-1/\det G}>0.\label{eq:dc}
\end{equation}
The dominant eigenvalue satisfies $\lambda_{\max}(d)=-(d-d_{c})$ and $\tau\sim(d-d_{c})^{-1}$ near threshold.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$\mathrm{tr}(G^{-1}J)=0$ and $\det(G^{-1}J)=1/\det G$. For $H_{*}=I$, $\max\mathrm{Re}\,\mathrm{spec}(J_{G}-dI)=0$ at $d=d_{c}$.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Necessity of Lorentzian Signature]
If $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(2,0)$ or $(0,2)$, then $\det G>0$, so $\lambda^{2}+1/\det G=0$ has purely imaginary roots and $d_{c}=0$.
\end{theorem}

\section{The stability boundary}

\begin{theorem}[Non-Separability]
For non-degenerate symmetric $2\times 2$ real $G$, under the isotropic linearisation $H_{*}=I$, the following are equivalent:
\begin{itemize}
\item[\textup{(a)}] The metric admits a nontrivial stability boundary $d_{c}>0$.
\item[\textup{(b)}] $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$ \textup{(Lorentzian)}, equivalently $\det G<0$.
\end{itemize}
The equivalence fails for general $H_{*}$ \textup{(Remark~\ref{rem:Hstar_dep})}: $d_{c}$ is a signature invariant only within the isotropic class.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$d_{c}>0 \Leftrightarrow -1/\det G>0 \Leftrightarrow \det G<0 \Leftrightarrow \mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$, via Theorems~2 and~3.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Dependence on $H_{*}$]\label{rem:Hstar_dep}
With $G=\mathrm{diag}(0.1,-10)$ and $H_{*}=\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}1&0.9\\0.9&1\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$, $d_{c}^{\mathrm{iso}}=1$ while $d_{c}(H_{*})=4.55$, confirming that $d_{c}$ may depend on $H_{*}$ outside the isotropic class.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Isotropic scaling]
$H_{*}$ denotes the cost-function weight matrix in $d(\delta x)/dt=(J_{G}-dI)H_{*}\,\delta x$; $H_{*}=I$ is the default. For $H_{*}=cI$ ($c>0$), $d_{c}$ is invariant. For general $H_{*}>0$, the threshold may shift.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Isotropy of $H_{*}$ for the Rindler metric]
For $G=\mathrm{diag}(-(\kappa\ell)^{2},1)$ and diagonal $H_{*}$, one has $\mathrm{tr}(J_{G}H_{*})=0$ identically, so $d_{c}=\alpha/(\kappa\ell)$ independently of the diagonal entries. The thermodynamic interpretation of \S\ref{sec:thermo} assumes $H_{*}$ diagonal; this is a necessary scope condition.
\end{remark}

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Numerical verification.} Among 2000 random symmetric $2\times 2$ matrices: all 1420 with $\mathrm{Sig}=(1,1)$ had $d_{c}>0$; all 580 with definite signature had $d_{c}=0$.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Scope.} The closed-form $d_{c}=\alpha\sqrt{-1/\det G}$ relies on $\dim(\mathrm{Skew}_{2})=1$; higher-dimensional extensions require additional structure and are not part of the present paper.

\section{Unique dynamics (Law~II)}

\noindent\textbf{Law~II.} \textit{The dynamics on $\{K=1\}$ take the unique form $\dot{x}=(J_{G}-D)\nabla V$, compatible with $GJ_{G}$ skew-symmetric and $D\ge 0$.}

\begin{theorem}[Law~II Canonical Decomposition]
Let $G$ be non-degenerate and let $J_{G}=\alpha G^{-1}J$ \textup{(Theorem~1)}. Suppose the generator $A$ splits as $GA = \alpha J - GD$, $D\ge 0$. Then $\dot{x} = (J_{G}-D)\,\nabla V$ uniquely.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$A=G^{-1}(\alpha J-GD)=J_{G}-D$. Uniqueness is immediate: $J_{G}$ is fixed by Theorem~1 and $D\ge 0$ is prescribed.\qed
\end{proof}

The generator decomposes as $J_{G}=A_{\mathrm{skew}}+S$ where $S=(J_{G}+J_{G}^{\top})/2$. For Lorentzian $G$: $\mathrm{spec}(J_{G})\subset\mathbb{R}$, $S\neq 0$, $\mathrm{tr}\,S=0$. For definite $G$: $\mathrm{spec}(J_{G})\subset i\mathbb{R}$ and $S=0$ iff $G\propto I$.

The GENERIC formalism~\cite{GENERIC} requires Euclidean antisymmetry $L^{\top}=-L$. For $G=cI$, $J_{G}$ is antisymmetric and Law~II reduces to isothermal GENERIC. For Lorentzian $G$, $J_{G}$ is $G$-antisymmetric but not Euclidean-antisymmetric, so Law~II falls outside GENERIC.

\section{Critical damping and fluctuation structure (Law~III)}\label{sec:lawIII}

\noindent\textbf{Law~III.} \textit{$K=1$ is selected by the stochastic dynamics as the unique self-consistent locus of the metric.}

\medskip

The auxiliary linear system $\dot{x}=(J_{G}-d_{c}I)x$ has eigenvalues $0$ and $-2d_{c}$ at critical damping. Theorem~6 concerns the nonlinear system $\dot{x}=(J_{G}-d_{c}I)\nabla V$, $V=\tfrac{1}{2}(K{-}1)^2$, where departure from $K{=}1$ incurs a cost~$V$. On $\{K{=}1\}$ one has $\nabla V=0$, so $x_*$ is an equilibrium; the Jacobian at $x_*$ is $(J_{G}-d_{c}I)\nabla^{2}V(x_*)$, which differs from $J_{G}-d_{c}I$ because $\nabla^{2}V(x_*)$ is rank-one.

\begin{theorem}[Local Effective Restoring Rate]
Let $G$ be a $2\times 2$ Lorentzian metric, $D=d_{c}I$. Define $K(x)=x^{\top}Gx$, $V=\tfrac{1}{2}(K-1)^{2}$, with drift $\dot{x}=(J_{G}-d_{c}I)\nabla V$. Then for all $x\in\mathbb{R}^{2}$:
\begin{equation}
\dot{K} = 4(K-1)\bigl[\alpha\,x^{\top}(J{\cdot}G)\,x - d_{c}\,x^{\top}G^{2}x\bigr].\label{eq:Kdot}
\end{equation}
At any $x_{*}\in\{K=1\}$ with $Gx_{*}=\lambda x_{*}$ and $x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}=1$:
\begin{equation}
\dot{K} = -\kappa_{K}(K-1)+o(K-1),\qquad \kappa_{K}=4\,d_{c}.\label{eq:kappa}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$\dot{K}=2x^{\top}G\dot{x}=4(K-1)\,x^{\top}G(J_{G}-d_{c}I)Gx$. Since $GJ_{G}=\alpha J$, right-multiplying by $G$ gives $GJ_{G}G=\alpha(JG)$, yielding~\eqref{eq:Kdot}. At a $G$-eigenvector, $x_{*}^{\top}JGx_{*}=\lambda\,x_{*}^{\top}Jx_{*}=0$ by skew-symmetry.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Base-point dependence]
The local effective restoring rate is $\kappa_{K}(x_{*})=4d_{c}\cdot x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}$; it depends on the base point. For the Rindler metric, $x_{*}=(0,1)$ is the unique $G$-eigenvector on $\{K{=}1\}$ with $x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}=1$. The factor~$4=2\times 2$ reflects two sources: the quadratic observable $K$ and the quadratic penalty~$V$; each contributes a factor of~$2$ upon differentiation.
\end{remark}

Adding isotropic white noise gives $dx=(J_{G}-D)\nabla V\,dt+\sigma\,dW_{t}$. Two classes arise:
\textit{Class~A} (thermodynamic): fluctuations of $K$ perpendicular to $\{K=1\}$, variance $\sigma^{2}/(2d_{c})$, verified numerically for $\sigma\in\{0.3,0.6,1.0\}$.
\textit{Class~B} (gauge): free diffusion along $\{K=1\}$ in the tangential (boost) direction, generating Lorentz boosts; the Hessian of $V$ restricted to $\{K=1\}$ is rank-one with null space containing this tangent, confirming that Class~B carries no restoring force and no heat.

Mixing the two classes conflates thermodynamic and kinematic degrees of freedom and yields a non-Boltzmann marginal $K$-distribution; this is an artifact of marginalising over the gauge direction.

\section{Spacetime thermodynamics (Route~A)}\label{sec:thermo}

This section connects the geometric structures of \S\S2--6 to spacetime thermodynamics; every connection requires at least one external identification, marked explicitly.

For the Rindler metric $G=\mathrm{diag}(-(\kappa\ell)^{2},1)$, where $\kappa$ is the surface gravity and $\ell$ the proper distance from the horizon, eq.~\eqref{eq:dc} gives $d_{c}=\alpha/(\kappa\ell)=\alpha/\sqrt{|g_{00}|}$. With $T_{H}=\hbar\kappa/(2\pi)$~\cite{Hawking},
\begin{equation}
d_{c}\cdot T_{H} = \frac{\alpha}{\kappa\ell}\cdot\frac{\hbar\kappa}{2\pi} = \frac{\alpha\hbar}{2\pi\ell},\label{eq:nearhorizon}
\end{equation}
exhibiting exact $\kappa$-cancellation. Hence the Tolman temperature~\cite{Tolman} $T_{\mathrm{tol}}=d_{c}\cdot T_{H}=\alpha\hbar/(2\pi\ell)$.

\subsection*{Clausius relation as FDT consequence within Class~A}

Within Class~A (fixed inertial observer), the $K$-dynamics near $K=1$ is a one-dimensional Ornstein--Uhlenbeck process with stationary variance
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Var}(K)\approx\frac{\sigma^{2}}{2d_{c}}\label{eq:var}
\end{equation}
and effective temperature $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=\sigma^{2}/(2d_{c})$. The fluctuation-dissipation relation $\sigma_{K}^{2}=2\kappa_{K}T_{\mathrm{eff}}$ follows algebraically from Law~II and Theorem~6. The Fokker--Planck equation yields a Boltzmann stationary distribution $\rho\propto\exp(-V/T_{\mathrm{eff}})$; the Clausius relation $\delta Q=T_{\mathrm{eff}}\,dS$ then follows for the OU process, with
\[
\delta Q := dV + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma_{K}^{2}\,dt,\qquad \sigma_{K}=2\sigma\|Gx_{*}\|.
\]
The only external input is the identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}(x_{*})=T_{\mathrm{tol}}(x_{*})$; everything else is derived internally.

\begin{proposition}[Local Clausius Equivalence]\label{prop:clausius}
Under local thermal equilibrium and the identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$, the Clausius relation $\delta Q=T\,\delta S$ is equivalent to
\begin{equation}
\sigma^{2} = 2\,d_{c}\cdot T_{\mathrm{tol}} = 2\,d_{c}^{2}\cdot T_{H}.\label{eq:noise}
\end{equation}
Conversely, $\sigma^{2}=2d_{c}\cdot T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ implies $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Setting $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ gives $\sigma^{2}/(2d_{c})=d_{c}\cdot T_{H}$, hence $\sigma^{2}=2d_{c}^{2}\cdot T_{H}$.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Status of the approximation]
Three logically distinct inputs: (i)~the OU approximation, valid when $|K-1|\ll 1$ and Class~B diffusion is slow compared to Class~A relaxation; (ii)~the identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$, the sole true external axiom; (iii)~the Tolman identity $T_{\mathrm{tol}}=d_{c}T_{H}$, which follows from~\eqref{eq:nearhorizon} and is independently verifiable.
\end{remark}

\subsection*{It\^{o} correction and local temperature}

Applying It\^{o}'s formula to $K=x^{\top}Gx$ gives an additional drift $\sigma^{2}\,\mathrm{tr}(G)\,dt$. This shifts the mean of $K$ but not its variance: at any $G$-eigenvector base point, the FDT ratio $\sigma_{K}^{2}/(2\kappa_{K})$ cancels to $\sigma^{2}/(2d_{c})$ regardless of $x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}$. Hence~\eqref{eq:var} and $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=\sigma^{2}/(2d_{c})$ hold for any Lorentzian $G$.

The $K$-space noise amplitude $\sigma_{K}(x)=2\sigma\|Gx\|$ varies along $\{K=1\}$, yielding a position-dependent temperature:
\begin{equation}
T_{\mathrm{loc}}(x) := \frac{\sigma_{K}(x)^{2}}{2\kappa_{K}(x_{*})} = \frac{\sigma^{2}\,x^{\top}G^{2}x}{2d_{c}\,x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}}.\label{eq:Tloc}
\end{equation}
For $\kappa\ell=1$ and $x(t)=(\sinh t,\cosh t)$: $T_{\mathrm{loc}}(t)=\sigma^{2}\cosh(2t)/(2d_{c})$, which is position-dependent and determined by the metric. For the Rindler metric, $\sigma^{2}\,\mathrm{tr}(G)=\sigma^{2}(1-(\kappa\ell)^{2})$; the OU approximation is exact at $\kappa\ell=1$ and accurate when $|(\kappa\ell)^{2}-1|\ll 1$.

\begin{remark}[Stability constraint]
For macroscopic black holes, $T_{\mathrm{tol}}\ll d_{c}$; setting $D=T_{\mathrm{tol}}I$ renders $K=1$ unstable. Stability requires $D\ge d_{c}I$; critical damping $D=d_{c}I$ is the unique marginally stable choice.
\end{remark}

Combined with the entropy--area proportionality $S\propto A$ and the Jacobson argument~\cite{Jacobson}, Route~A yields $G_{\mu\nu}=8\pi T_{\mu\nu}$ for general metrics. The sole external input beyond R+E+T is $S\propto A$.

\section{Eigenvalue self-consistency (Route~B)}\label{sec:field}

Route~A reaches the Einstein equations via thermodynamics.
We now show that a direct algebraic route exists in the
spherically symmetric sector, using no thermodynamic input.

Consider the static spherically symmetric metric
\begin{equation}\label{eq:metric}
  ds^2=-f(r)\,dt^2+f(r)^{-1}dr^2+r^2\,d\Omega^2,
\end{equation}
with symplectic eigenvalues $\sigma_1=r\sqrt{f}$, $\sigma_2=r$,
and d'Alembertian
$\Box\phi=(r^2f)^{-1}\partial_r(r^2f\,\partial_r\phi)$.

\begin{definition}[Eigenvalue self-consistency]\label{def:esc}
\begin{align}
  K_1 &:= \sigma_2^2\,\Box\ln\sigma_1 = 1,\label{eq:K1def}\\
  K_2 &:= \sigma_2^2\,\Box\ln\sigma_2 = 1.\label{eq:K2def}
\end{align}
\end{definition}

\noindent
Both conditions have the form
$\sigma_2^2\,\Box\ln\sigma=1$:
the d'Alembertian of each log-eigenvalue equals the angular
curvature cost $1/\sigma_2^2$.
Every eigenvalue must propagate at the rate set by the angular
sector---a field-level analogue of the point-level balance $K{=}1$.

Expanding:
$K_1 = f+2rf'+\frac{r^2}{2}f''$,\;
$K_2 = rf'+f = d(rf)/dr$.
(For $K_2$: $\Box\ln r = (1/r^2)d/dr[r^2f/r]=(rf'+f)/r^2$,
so $r^2\Box\ln r = rf'+f$.)

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:R}
$R=-2\,\Box\ln\sigma_1+2/r^2$ for~\eqref{eq:metric}.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
$R=-f''-4f'/r+2(1{-}f)/r^2$.
$\Box\ln\sigma_1=f/r^2+2f'/r+f''/2$.
Then $-2\Box\ln\sigma_1+2/r^2=R$.\qed
\end{proof}

\noindent
Hence $K_1{=}1\Leftrightarrow R{=}0$ and
$K_2{=}1\Leftrightarrow R_{\theta\theta}{=}0$
(since $R_{\theta\theta}=1{-}f{-}rf'=1{-}K_2$).
Equivalently, $K_2{=}1$ states that the Misner--Sharp mass
$m=\frac{r}{2}(1{-}f)$ is constant: $dm/dr=(1{-}K_2)/2=0$.

\begin{theorem}[Field-Level Equivalence]\label{thm:field}
For~\eqref{eq:metric} with $f\in C^2(0,\infty)$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:equiv}
  \sigma_2^2\,\Box\ln\sigma_i=1\;\;\text{for }i=1,2
  \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad R_{\mu\nu}=0.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$(\Rightarrow)$\;
$K_2{=}1$ gives $R_{\theta\theta}{=}0$.
$K_1{-}K_2=rf'+\frac{r^2}{2}f''=0$ gives $f''=-2f'/r$, hence $R_{tt}{=}0$.

$(\Leftarrow)$\;
$R_{\theta\theta}{=}0$ gives $K_2{=}1$.
$R_{tt}{=}0$ gives $f''=-2f'/r$; substituting:
$K_1=f+2rf'-rf'=f+rf'=K_2=1$.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:solutions}
$K_1{=}1$ alone gives $R{=}0$ with general solution
$f=1-C_1/r-C_2/r^2$; $K_2{=}1$ forces $C_2{=}0$,
recovering Schwarzschild uniquely (Birkhoff).
In the weak field ($f=1+\varepsilon\varphi$),
$K_2{=}1$ gives $\varphi=C/r$ (Newton) in one line;
$K_1{=}1$ is automatically satisfied at linear order,
so its independent content is purely nonlinear (strong-field).
\end{corollary}

\begin{remark}[Non-vacuum and $\Lambda$]
From the Einstein $G_{tt}$ component,
$\rho = (1{-}K_2)/(8\pi r^2)$:
matter is the angular eigenvalue's failure to self-consistently
propagate, measured per unit area.
For Schwarzschild--de~Sitter, $K_2=1{-}\Lambda r^2$ and
$K_1=1{-}2\Lambda r^2$.
Both relations \emph{use} the Einstein equations;
they are cost rewritings, not independent derivations
(in particular, $8\pi$ is inherited from GR).
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[General metrics and polarization]
For a general metric, one applies $\sigma_2^2\Box\ln\sigma_i=1$ on
each null direction.
The \emph{polarization identity} guarantees that if
$R_{\mu\nu}v^\mu v^\nu=0$ for every null~$v^\mu$ and $R=0$,
then $R_{\mu\nu}=0$.
(Proof: in an orthonormal frame, taking $v=e_0\pm e_k$ gives
$R_{0k}=0$, $R_{00}+R_{kk}=0$; mixed spatial components vanish
from $v=(e_0+\frac{e_i+e_j}{\sqrt{2}})$; the trace $R=0$ then
forces $R_{00}=0$.)
The step connecting the eigenvalue condition to
$R_{\mu\nu}v^\mu v^\nu=0$ on each null plane requires the
Gauss--Codazzi relations for null hypersurfaces and is not yet completed.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Route A vs.\ Route B]
Route~A (Proposition~\ref{prop:clausius} $+$ Jacobson) applies to all
metrics but requires $S\propto A$.
Route~B (Theorem~\ref{thm:field}) requires no thermodynamic input
but is proved only for spherical symmetry.
The general extension requires null-surface Gauss--Codazzi and shares
Jacobson's null-direction patching architecture;
in the spherically symmetric sector, however, the derivation is
entirely independent.
\end{remark}

\section{Singularity structure}\label{sec:singularity}

\begin{proposition}[$\sigma_1>0$ from Axiom~T]\label{prop:sigma1}
Axiom~T implies $\sigma_1>0$ wherever $K{=}1$ is imposed.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The cost metric has $G_{00}=\sigma_1^2$. If $\sigma_1=0$ then
temporal displacement has zero cost, violating Axiom~T.
When $\sigma_1=0$, $K=-x_1^2\le 0$
and $K{=}1$ has no solutions.\qed
\end{proof}

\noindent
The Schwarzschild singularity ($\sigma_1\to 0$ as $r\to 0$) lies
outside the domain of the theory.

\begin{proposition}[Self-consistency bound]\label{prop:domain}
The $K{=}1$ framework is self-consistent only for
$\sigma_1>\sigma_{1,c}:=\hbar/(4\pi)\approx\ell_P/(4\pi)$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Near the horizon, $T_{\mathrm{eff}}\approx\hbar/(4\pi\sigma_1)$
(from~\eqref{eq:nearhorizon}).
$\mathrm{Var}(K)=T_{\mathrm{eff}}$; for $K{=}1$ to be a
well-defined equilibrium, $\mathrm{Var}(K)<1$, hence
$\sigma_1>\hbar/(4\pi)$.\qed
\end{proof}

\noindent
Below $\sigma_{1,c}$, thermal fluctuations of $K$ exceed its
mean value and the semiclassical metric loses its classical meaning.
This is a \emph{soft} bound---analogous to classical mechanics
at action~$\sim\hbar$---derived entirely within the framework.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection*{Two routes to Einstein}

The framework reaches the Einstein equations from R+E+T by two routes sharing the same starting point ($G$ indefinite, $K{=}1$, OU dynamics) but diverging thereafter. Route~A uses stochastic dynamics and the Jacobson bridge with $S\propto A$; Route~B uses the d'Alembertian of symplectic eigenvalues. In the spherically symmetric sector, Route~B is fully independent of Jacobson and of thermodynamics.

The Jacobson argument~\cite{Jacobson} reconstructs the Einstein equations from three inputs: Lorentzian signature, the Clausius relation on every local Rindler horizon, and $S=A/4$. Route~A supplies the first two: signature is forced by realizability (Theorem~4) and Clausius follows algebraically on each horizon (Proposition~\ref{prop:clausius}); $S\propto A$ is then the sole remaining external input. The patching across horizon directions requires no additional assumptions: at each spacetime point, every null direction defines a two-dimensional normal plane on which $\dim(\mathrm{Skew}_2)=1$, so Theorem~1 guarantees a unique~$J_G$ with no pairing ambiguity.

\subsection*{The $K{=}1$ self-referential structure}

The condition $K{=}1$ has a self-referential character: the metric~$G$ defines the quadratic form~$K$, and $K{=}1$ requires that this form reproduce~$G$'s own normalisation. The residual $K{-}1$ is the metric's own measure of departure from self-consistency; the leading-order restoring potential $V=\frac{1}{2}(K{-}1)^2$ is therefore fixed by smoothness alone. At the field level, this self-referential loop extends: the eigenvalue principle $\sigma_2^2\Box\ln\sigma_i=1$ requires each symplectic eigenvalue to propagate at the rate determined by the metric it inhabits.

\subsection*{Stages}

\noindent\textit{Stage~0:} R+E+T $\to$ $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$ (Theorem~4). Conversely, for definite~$G$ no nontrivial stability boundary exists (Theorem~3): the framework produces physical content only when the cost structure is genuinely Lorentzian.

\noindent\textit{Stage~1:} $G$ defines light cones and causal structure.

\noindent\textit{Stage~2:} $K{=}1$ $\to$ OU $\to$ Class~A/B; Clausius follows (Proposition~\ref{prop:clausius}).

\noindent\textit{Stage~3 (field):} $\sigma_2^2\Box\ln\sigma_i=1\Leftrightarrow R_{\mu\nu}=0$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:field}, spherical).
For non-vacuum configurations, $\rho=(1{-}K_2)/(8\pi r^2)$: matter is the angular eigenvalue's deficit from self-consistency per unit area. (This \emph{rewrites} the Einstein $G_{tt}$-component; the coefficient $8\pi$ is inherited from GR, not derived.)

\noindent\textit{Stage~4 (quantum interface):} For $G=\mathrm{diag}(-(\kappa\ell)^2,1)$, defining $\sigma_1:=|\det G|^{1/2}/\alpha=\kappa\ell/\alpha$ gives $d_c\cdot\sigma_1=1$ identically. This is an uncertainty relation: the stabilisation cost~$d_c$ and the symplectic eigenvalue~$\sigma_1$ are reciprocal. The observable~$K$ and the boost angle~$\varphi$ along $\{K{=}1\}$ satisfy $\{K,\varphi\}=-2d_c$; whether quantisation of this conjugate pair yields a hard lower bound on~$\sigma_1$ remains open. Meanwhile, Axiom~T forces $\sigma_1>0$ (Proposition~\ref{prop:sigma1}) and the self-consistency bound gives $\sigma_1\gtrsim\ell_P$ (Proposition~\ref{prop:domain}).

\subsection*{Extension to $(1{+}3)$ dimensions}

In $(1{+}3)$ dimensions, $\{K{=}1\}\cong H^3$ carries $S^2$ cross-sections with area $4\pi(\kappa\ell)^2$. With $\alpha=1$, $\sigma_1=\kappa\ell$, so the cross-sectional area is $A=4\pi\sigma_1^2$ and the Bekenstein--Hawking entropy $S_{\mathrm{BH}}=A/4=\pi\sigma_1^2$, tracing both to the same symplectic invariant. The coefficient $\eta=1/4$ need not be independently assumed: for Schwarzschild, the first law $\delta M=T_H\,\delta S$ with $A_H=16\pi M^2$ determines it algebraically. Two structural changes accompany the extension: the equivalence $d_c>0\Leftrightarrow\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$ weakens to a one-directional implication, and the uniqueness of~$J_G$ (guaranteed by $\dim(\mathrm{Skew}_2)=1$) requires a symplectic pairing choice when $\dim(\mathrm{Skew}_4)=6$. For the Rindler metric with isotropic spatial sector, the three standard pairings (block-diagonal, canonical~$J_4$, and anti-diagonal) yield identical~$d_c$; general pairings do not, and whether a natural selection principle exists is open.

\subsection*{Beyond GENERIC}

For Lorentzian~$G$, the symmetric part $S=(J_G+J_G^\top)/2\neq 0$ departs from the GENERIC formalism~\cite{GENERIC} while $d_c$ is controlled by $\det G$ alone, suggesting a Lorentzian extension of non-equilibrium thermodynamics in the spirit of Wiener's programme~\cite{Wiener}. The $2\times 2$ framework is not a truncation of a $(1{+}3)$-dimensional theory but the natural dimensionality of a single local Rindler horizon: one temporal and one spatial direction normal to the horizon.

\subsection*{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
\item Route~B is proved for spherical symmetry only.
\item Route~A requires $S\propto A$.
\item No deviation from GR is predicted in vacuum.
\item The $\sigma_1$ bound is soft, not hard.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Open problems}

\begin{enumerate}
\item Complete Route~B for general metrics (Gauss--Codazzi).
\item Prove $\sigma_{1,\min}>0$ from $K$--$\theta$ conjugacy.
\item Derive $8\pi$ from cost (non-vacuum Route~B).
\item Identify testable strong-field predictions.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Conclusion}

General relativity prescribes Lorentzian signature; the present work derives it from a cost asymmetry. At the point level, the Clausius relation follows algebraically. At the field level, the eigenvalue self-consistency principle yields the vacuum Einstein equations directly. Axiom~T excludes the singularity from the theory's domain, and the OU variance sets a Planck-scale resolution limit. What remains prescribed is the cost asymmetry itself: why one direction carries positive cost while others do not. This is Riemann's question in economic form.
