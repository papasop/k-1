## 0. The Central Idea: Time Is Not External

In every framework of physics — from Newton through quantum mechanics — time enters as a primitive: an external parameter, a background coordinate, something given before physics begins.

This document traces the consequences of a single redefinition:

> **Time is the cost of structural change.**

Time is not a container in which physics happens. Time is what physics produces. From this redefinition, a chain of consequences flows — each link algebraically forced by the previous one — reaching from the origin of causality through thermodynamics to the threshold of quantum mechanics.

## 1. Information Time and the Metric

### 1.1 Definition

Let Φ parametrise structural variation and H > 0 an entropic resistance. The information time is:

$$dt_{\text{info}} := d\Phi / H$$

This is a scalar: the cost of an infinitesimal structural change. It depends on where the system is (through Φ and H) and in what direction it moves (through dΦ).

### 1.2 From Scalar to Metric

A scalar does not distinguish directions. Physics requires directions — which way is "time," which way is "space." The metric emerges from asking: *how does the cost depend on direction?*

The squared cost dt²_info, expanded to second order in displacement δx, gives:

$$dt_{\text{info}}^2 \approx \delta x^\top G \, \delta x$$

where G = ∇²(dt²_info) is a symmetric matrix — the metric. This is precisely Riemann's construction (1854): the metric is the second-order structure of an infinitesimal cost function. Riemann used distance; here the cost is informational.

### 1.3 Why the Metric Must Be Indefinite

Three conditions constrain G (proved rigorously in the Realizability paper):

- **Assumption E** (Expansion): G exists as a non-degenerate quadratic form.
- **Assumption T** (Temporal cost): At least one direction has positive cost — G(e_t, e_t) > 0.
- **Assumption R** (Realizability): Some directions have vanishing cost — persistent configurations require that G not be positive definite.

For a non-degenerate 2×2 matrix with one positive and one non-positive direction:

$$\text{Sig}(G) = (1,1) \quad \text{(Lorentzian)}$$

This is Theorem 4 of the K=1 paper. Lorentzian signature is not assumed — it is the unique possibility consistent with R, E, and T.

### 1.4 Connection to Zeno

Zeno's two paradoxes implicitly assumed the opposite cost structure:

- **Achilles**: spatial cost has a positive lower bound (space is expensive) → R violated.
- **Flying arrow**: temporal cost can be zero (time is free) → T violated.

The physical world inverts Zeno:

- Space is free (R satisfied) → Achilles catches the tortoise.
- Time is expensive (T satisfied) → the arrow moves.

R + T = Lorentzian. Zeno had the cost assignments backwards.

Given G, define:

$$K(x) := x^\top G \, x$$

K measures the temporal cost at configuration x. The condition K = 1 means: the metric reproduces its own normalisation. Below 1, the metric underestimates; above 1, it overestimates.

### 2.2 Self-Reference and the Status of K = 1

This is not a passive normalisation. G defines K, and K = 1 constrains G. The metric is checking itself.

**Why K = 1 and not K = c for some other constant?** K is dimensionless (G is dimensionless in the information-time parametrisation). The condition K = c for any c > 0 defines a hyperboloid, but only K = 1 is self-referential: it requires that the quadratic form defined by G, evaluated on the unit hyperboloid of G, return exactly the value that defines that hyperboloid. It is the least arbitrary choice, but it is a choice.

Departures from K = 1 generate a restoring potential:

$$V = \tfrac{1}{2}(K - 1)^2$$

This potential is not independently chosen — it is the unique leading-order term in the Taylor expansion of any smooth function V(K) with a minimum at K = 1: since V(1) = 0 and V'(1) = 0 at a minimum, V ≈ ½V''(1)(K − 1)², and V''(1) can be absorbed into the time scale. ### 4.2 Clausius Relation

## 5. Wick Rotation: From Thermal to Quantum

### 5.1 The Spectral Mapping

**Terminology.** Standard Wick rotation in QFT is the analytic continuation t → iτ of the time coordinate, preserving the metric signature while changing the coordinate representation. What we describe here is different: a change of the metric signature itself, G_L = diag(−σ₁², 1) → G_E = diag(+σ₁², 1). The algebraic effects parallel those of standard Wick rotation (real eigenvalues → imaginary, decay → oscillation), but the operations are distinct. We use "Wick rotation" as shorthand for this algebraic parallel throughout this section, not as a claim of mathematical equivalence with path-integral Wick rotation. Whether the two operations are compatible — i.e., whether the K=1 signature change can be embedded in a path-integral framework — is an open question.

Under signature change:

| Lorentzian | Euclidean |
|---|---|
| spec(J_G) = ±d_c (real) | spec(J_G) = ±iω (imaginary) |
| decay: e^{−κt} | oscillation: e^{−iωτ} |
| dissipation | unitary evolution |

The formal continuation of the algebraic formula d_c = α√(−1/det G) gives iω = iα/σ₁ when det G > 0. Strictly, Theorem 3 gives d_c = 0 for Euclidean G (no real stability boundary); the value iω is obtained by formally evaluating the same algebraic expression at det G > 0, yielding an imaginary number. The magnitude α/σ₁ is the same — only real vs imaginary changes.

### 5.2 Fokker-Planck to Schrödinger

The OU process generates a Fokker-Planck equation. The standard similarity transform ρ = e^{−V/(2T)} ψ converts it to:

$$\partial\psi/\partial t = -H_s \psi$$

$$H_s = -D_K \partial^2/\partial\varepsilon^2 + \kappa_K^2 \varepsilon^2/(4D_K) - \kappa_K/2$$

This is the quantum harmonic oscillator in imaginary time, with frequency Ω = κ_K = 4d_c.

Wick rotating t → iτ:

$$i\partial\psi/\partial\tau = H_s \psi$$

This is the Schrödinger equation.

### 5.3 Temperature and Planck Constant: Same Dimensionless Number

Within the dimensionless K=1 framework, the same algebraic expression appears in both signatures:

$$T_{\text{eff}} = \sigma^2/(2d_c) \quad \text{[Lorentzian: effective temperature]}$$

$$\hbar_{\text{eff}} = \sigma^2/(2\omega) \quad \text{[Euclidean: effective Planck constant]}$$

Since d_c = ω = α/σ₁ (the same number in both signatures):

$$T_{\text{eff}} = \hbar_{\text{eff}} \quad \text{(as dimensionless numbers within the framework)}$$

**Dimensional caveat.** In physical units, temperature has dimensions [energy] (with k_B = 1) while ℏ has dimensions [energy·time] = [action]. These are not the same dimension. The equality T_eff = ℏ_eff holds only within the dimensionless framework where G, d_c, σ, and all derived quantities are pure numbers. Connecting to physical dimensions requires the external identification T_eff = T_tol, which introduces ℏ through T_H = ℏκ/(2π). The framework does not derive ℏ as a dimensional constant — it identifies the dimensionless ratio that, upon dimensional restoration, becomes ℏ.

**What this means.** The algebraic identity d_c = ω implies that the same dimensionless noise-to-restoring ratio σ²/(2α/σ₁) governs thermal fluctuations in one signature and quantum fluctuations in the other. This is consistent with the standard Matsubara correspondence β = it/ℏ (i.e., inverse temperature ↔ imaginary time in units of ℏ), but does not go beyond it.

### 5.4 What the Signature Change Adds to K=1

The signature change does not derive quantum mechanics from K=1. It confirms internal consistency: the K=1 thermal dynamics, when the algebraic formulas are continued to the Euclidean signature, produces the quantum harmonic oscillator — a known physical object — with parameters determined by det G (not arbitrary). This is a consistency check, not a derivation.

What is standard: FP ↔ Schrödinger via similarity transform (Nelson 1966), OU ↔ quantum oscillator, Matsubara formalism.

What K=1 adds: the thermal side has a geometric origin (d_c from det G, not a free parameter), and the signature change gives a geometric interpretation to the thermal-quantum correspondence (changing det G from negative to positive, rather than the coordinate operation t → iτ).

What K=1 does not establish: whether the K=1 signature change is mathematically equivalent to standard Wick rotation in path-integral QFT, whether the correspondence extends beyond quadratic potentials, or whether σ (and thus ℏ_eff) can be determined geometrically.

## 6. Touching the Born Rule

### 6.1 The Mathematical Fact

The FP probability density ρ and the wave function ψ are related by:

$$\rho = e^{-U/2} \psi, \quad \text{where } U = V/T_{\text{eff}}$$

The stationary FP solution is:

$$\rho_{\text{eq}} = Z^{-1} e^{-U}$$

The corresponding ψ:

$$\psi_0 = e^{+U/2} \rho_{\text{eq}} = Z^{-1/2} e^{-U/2}$$

Therefore:

$$|\psi_0|^2 = Z^{-1} e^{-U} = \rho_{\text{eq}}$$

**The ground-state Born probability equals the Boltzmann distribution.** This is exact, not approximate.

### 6.2 What This Means

In the FP framework, ρ is a genuine probability density — it describes the statistical distribution of the OU process. It is not postulated; it emerges from the dynamics of K=1.

Through the similarity transform, ρ maps to ψ. At equilibrium, |ψ₀|² = ρ_eq. After Wick rotation, ψ becomes a quantum state and |ψ|² becomes the Born probability.

The Born rule (for the ground state) is thus the Wick-rotated Boltzmann distribution — not an independent postulate, but a consequence of metric self-consistency.

### 6.3 Limitations

This only holds for the ground state. For excited states, φ_n = e^{−U/2} ψ_n can be negative — not a probability density. The uniqueness argument (|ψ|² is the only conserved non-negative quantity under unitary evolution) extends the result formally, but does not constitute a full derivation.

## 7. Signature Dynamics and the Measurement Question

### 7.1 Two Signatures, Two Roles

The K=1 framework with two metric signatures produces two types of physics. The physical assignment — established by the structure of the framework — is:

| | Lorentzian (wave) | Euclidean (collapse) |
|---|---|---|
| Signature | (−,+) | (+,+) |
| d_c | real | imaginary (iω) |
| OU process | exists → FP → ψ | does not exist |
| ψ status | supported (ρ → ψ = e^{U/2}ρ) | unsupported (no FP equilibrium) |
| Dynamics | superposition, interference | ψ loses probability support |
| Role | **quantum wave evolution** | **collapse** |

The wave function ψ exists *because* of the Lorentzian OU process: ψ = e^{U/2}ρ, where ρ is the FP stationary distribution. In the Euclidean regime, no OU process exists, no FP equilibrium exists, and ψ has no probability support — the wave function is destroyed. This is collapse.

### 7.2 Lorentzian Self-Stability: One-Way Barrier (Computed)

Modelling Δ = det G as a scalar with state-dependent noise reveals an asymmetric barrier at the signature boundary Δ = 0.

**Lorentzian side (Δ < 0):** The noise coefficient D_L(Δ) = c·√|Δ| vanishes as Δ → 0⁻. The OU process suppresses its own fluctuations near the boundary. The effective potential acquires an entropic correction −¼ ln|Δ| → +∞, creating an infinite barrier. Lorentzian signature is self-stabilising: the wave function cannot spontaneously collapse.

**Euclidean side (Δ > 0):** No OU process exists, so no self-suppression mechanism operates. The noise on Δ is constant (external, not from K-fluctuations). No entropic barrier exists from this side. The confining potential drives the system toward Δ₀ < 0 (Lorentzian).

**Barrier heights:**

- From Lorentzian (Lor → Euc): Φ_barrier ≈ 2.25 (entropic + confining)
- From Euclidean (Euc → Lor): Φ_barrier ≈ 0 (downhill, no entropic term)
- Asymmetry ratio: >200×

**Monte Carlo verification (200 runs, full effective potential):**

- Direction A (Lor → Euc): 0/200 = 0% crossed — completely blocked
- Direction B (Euc → Lor): 200/200 = 100% crossed — completely allowed (mean time 0.39)
- Robust across all D₀ (0.01–0.5) and all μ (0.1–2.0)

**The barrier is one-way.** Quantum coherence (Lorentzian) is stable; collapse (Euclidean excursion) cannot happen spontaneously but, once it occurs, recovery to Lorentzian is immediate and irreversible.

### 7.3 Measurement as Euclidean Excursion

The one-way barrier provides a geometric mechanism for quantum measurement:

1. **Wave evolution (Lorentzian).** The system sits in the Lorentzian regime. The OU process supports ψ through ρ = e^{−U/2}ψ. Superposition is maintained. The entropic barrier (Φ ≈ 2.25) prevents spontaneous collapse. This is why quantum coherence persists.

2. **Measurement (external push).** An external perturbation — coupling to a macroscopic environment — pushes Δ past the barrier into the Euclidean regime. The push must overcome the entropic barrier; this is the energetic cost of measurement.

3. **Collapse (Euclidean).** In the Euclidean regime, d_c is imaginary, the OU process ceases, and the FP equilibrium disappears. The wave function ψ loses its probability support. This is collapse: the destruction of the superposition.

4. **Recovery (Euc → Lor).** With no barrier from the Euclidean side, the system naturally falls back to Lorentzian (100% of runs, mean time ~0.4). The OU process restarts.

5. **Re-equilibration (Born rule).** The restarted OU process decays to ρ_eq = |ψ₀|². The system does not return to its original superposition — it returns to the ground state. Information about the pre-measurement superposition is lost. The Born probability |ψ₀|² = ρ_eq is the re-equilibration distribution.

### 7.4 Why Measurement Is Irreversible

The one-way barrier explains irreversibility without additional assumptions:

- **Forward (collapse):** Lor → Euc requires overcoming a barrier of ~2.25 — requires external force (measurement apparatus).
- **Return (recovery):** Euc → Lor has no barrier — happens spontaneously.
- **Net effect:** The system returns to Lorentzian, but in the ground state, not the original superposition. The barrier prevents the reverse cycle (Lor → Euc → original Lor), so the original quantum state cannot be recovered.

This is measurement irreversibility: it arises from the geometric asymmetry of the entropic barrier, not from an ad hoc collapse postulate.

### 7.5 Why the World Is Lorentzian

The self-stability of the Lorentzian regime (§7.2) answers an independent question: *why is the physical signature Lorentzian?*

Lorentzian is the unique dynamically stable signature. The OU process creates an entropic barrier that protects the Lorentzian regime from spontaneous signature change. The Euclidean regime has no such protection — it is unstable and decays to Lorentzian. The physical world is Lorentzian because Lorentzian is the only self-sustaining signature.

### 7.6 Comparison with Decoherence

Standard decoherence explains the apparent disappearance of interference through environmental entanglement. The K=1 one-way barrier is compatible with decoherence and adds geometric content: decoherence is the dynamical process by which the environment pushes Δ past the entropic barrier, triggering the Euclidean excursion. The K=1 framework explains *why* decoherence is irreversible (one-way barrier) and *why* the outcome probabilities are |ψ|² (post-collapse Boltzmann equilibrium).
