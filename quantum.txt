\begin{abstract}
Within the $K{=}1$ chronogeometrodynamic framework~\cite{K1paper},
we show that the wave function's existence has a geometric precondition ---
Lorentzian metric signature --- and that this precondition is dynamical.
The Ornstein--Uhlenbeck process on $\{K{=}1\}$ generates a Fokker--Planck
equation; the standard similarity transform~\cite{Nelson} followed by
Wick rotation yields a Schr\"odinger equation.
This chain requires $d_c = \alpha/\sigma_1 > 0$, which holds if and only if
$\det G < 0$ (Lorentzian). Modelling $\Delta = \det G$ as a stochastic
scalar with state-dependent noise $D(\Delta) = c\sqrt{|\Delta|}$, we demonstrate
that the signature boundary $\Delta = 0$ carries a \emph{one-way barrier}:
Lorentzian~$\to$~Euclidean is blocked (0\% of Monte Carlo runs),
while Euclidean~$\to$~Lorentzian is spontaneous (100\%). The barrier
is intrinsic (survives artificial Euclidean traps) and irreversible
($D(\Delta)$ is discontinuous at $\Delta = 0$, breaking detailed balance ---
proved analytically, not merely observed numerically). In the multi-mode
extension, cross-mode noise $D_{\mathrm{cross}} > 0$ renders the barrier
finite, permitting Kramers escape at rate $\Gamma \sim \exp(-B_{\mathrm{FP}})
\approx 0$; the measurement apparatus acts as a catalyst that exponentially
accelerates this intrinsically possible process. After collapse, the
restarted OU process decays to $\rho_{\mathrm{eq}} = |\psi_0|^2$, deriving
the ground-state Born rule as a Boltzmann equilibrium --- not a postulate.
\end{abstract}

%=====================================================================
\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
%=====================================================================

Every approach to the quantum measurement problem operates at the level of the Schr\"odinger equation or its solutions: Copenhagen adds a projection postulate~\cite{vonNeumann}, decoherence traces over environmental degrees of freedom~\cite{Zurek}, many-worlds reinterprets the solution space~\cite{Everett}, and spontaneous collapse theories (GRW~\cite{GRW}, Penrose~\cite{Penrose}) modify the equation itself. All share a common assumption: the Schr\"odinger equation exists unconditionally.

This paper identifies a geometric precondition for the Schr\"odinger equation's existence and shows that the precondition is dynamical:

\begin{quote}
\itshape
Within the $K{=}1$ framework, the wave function $\psi$ exists if and only if the metric signature is Lorentzian.
Collapse is the temporary removal of this precondition.
\end{quote}

The argument proceeds in four steps:
\begin{enumerate}
\item The $K{=}1$ Ornstein--Uhlenbeck (OU) process~\cite{K1paper} generates a Fokker--Planck (FP) equation. The standard similarity transform~\cite{Nelson} converts FP to an imaginary-time Schr\"odinger equation; Wick rotation gives the real-time form. This chain requires $d_c > 0$, which holds iff $\det G < 0$ (Lorentzian)~\cite[Theorem~4]{K1paper}.
\item The noise coefficient $D(\Delta) \propto \sqrt{|\Delta|}$ vanishes at the signature boundary $\Delta = 0$, creating an entropic barrier that protects Lorentzian signature.
\item The barrier is \emph{one-way}: Lor~$\to$~Euc is blocked; Euc~$\to$~Lor is spontaneous. This is proved analytically from the discontinuity of $D(\Delta)$ at $\Delta = 0$.
\item After collapse (Euclidean excursion), the OU process restarts and decays to $\rho_{\mathrm{eq}} = |\psi_0|^2$: the ground-state Born rule emerges as Boltzmann equilibrium.
\end{enumerate}

The approach does not modify the Schr\"odinger equation. It operates one level below --- at the geometric precondition for the equation's existence. This is the sense in which it differs from all five preceding approaches.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Scope.} All results are derived in the $2\times 2$ setting natural to a single Rindler horizon~\cite{K1paper}. The $(1{+}3)$-dimensional extension is discussed in \S\ref{sec:discussion} but not required for the core results; each 2D normal plane closes independently because $\dim(\mathrm{Skew}_2) = 1$ guarantees unique symplectic structure~\cite[Theorem~1]{K1paper}.

\section{Review of the $K{=}1$ framework}\label{sec:review}
%=====================================================================

We collect the results from~\cite{K1paper} needed below. All proofs are in~\cite{K1paper}; none are repeated here.

Let $G$ be a non-degenerate symmetric $2\times 2$ real matrix, $K(x) = x^\top G x$, $V = \tfrac{1}{2}(K-1)^2$, and $J_G = \alpha G^{-1}J$ the unique symplectic generator~\cite[Theorem~1]{K1paper}. The stochastic dynamics is $dx = (J_G - D)\nabla V\,dt + \sigma\,dW_t$ (It\^o), with $D = d_c I$ at critical damping.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Stability boundary}~\cite[Theorem~4]{K1paper}: $d_c > 0 \Leftrightarrow \mathrm{Sig}(G) = (1,1)$ (Lorentzian), with $d_c = \alpha\sqrt{-1/\det G}$. For the Rindler metric $G = \mathrm{diag}(-\sigma_1^2, 1)$, this simplifies to $d_c = \alpha/\sigma_1$. The Lorentzian requirement is derived from cost-asymmetry axioms in~\cite{Realizability}.
\item \textbf{Restoring rate}~\cite[Theorem~6]{K1paper}: $\kappa_K = 4d_c$ for a normalised $G$-eigenvector on $\{K{=}1\}$.
\item \textbf{OU process}: Near $K=1$, the deviation $\varepsilon = K - 1$ obeys $d\varepsilon = -\kappa_K \varepsilon\,dt + \sigma_K\,dW$, where $\sigma_K = 2\sigma\|Gx_*\|$ is the projected noise amplitude ($\sigma$ is the isotropic noise in $\mathbb{R}^2$). The stationary variance is $T_{\mathrm{eff}} = \sigma_K^2/(2\kappa_K) = \sigma^2/(2d_c)$ (the second equality uses $\sigma_K = 2\sigma\sigma_1$ and $\kappa_K = 4d_c$ at the Rindler base point $x_* = (0,1)$).
\item \textbf{Clausius relation}~\cite[Proposition~7]{K1paper}: Under $T_{\mathrm{eff}} = T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ (the sole external axiom), $\delta Q = T_{\mathrm{eff}}\,\delta S$.
\end{itemize}

\section{Wave function from Lorentzian geometry}\label{sec:wavefunction}
%=====================================================================

\subsection{Fokker--Planck to Schr\"odinger}

The OU process for $\varepsilon = K - 1$ generates the FP equation
\begin{equation}
\partial_t \rho = \partial_\varepsilon\bigl[\kappa_K \varepsilon\,\rho\bigr] + D_K\,\partial_\varepsilon^2 \rho,
\label{eq:FP}
\end{equation}
with $D_K = \sigma_K^2/2$ and stationary solution $\rho_{\mathrm{eq}} \propto e^{-\kappa_K \varepsilon^2/(2D_K)} = e^{-V/T_{\mathrm{eff}}}$.

The similarity transform $\rho = e^{-U/2}\psi$ with $U = V/T_{\mathrm{eff}}$ converts~\eqref{eq:FP} to
\begin{equation}
\partial_t \psi = -H_s\,\psi, \qquad H_s = -D_K\partial_\varepsilon^2 + \frac{\kappa_K^2\varepsilon^2}{4D_K} - \frac{\kappa_K}{2}.
\label{eq:imaginary_time}
\end{equation}
This is the quantum harmonic oscillator in imaginary time. Wick rotating $t \to i\tau$:
\begin{equation}
i\,\partial_\tau \psi = H_s\,\psi.
\label{eq:Schrodinger}
\end{equation}

\begin{remark}[What is standard vs.\ what $K{=}1$ adds]
The similarity transform FP~$\to$~imaginary-time Schr\"odinger is standard~\cite{Nelson}; the Wick rotation is a formal algebraic operation, not a claim of physical equivalence between dissipation and unitary evolution. The $K{=}1$ framework adds two things: (i)~the parameters $\kappa_K$, $D_K$ are not free but determined by $\det G$ through~\cite[Theorems~4,\,6]{K1paper}; (ii)~the existence of the OU process --- and hence of $\psi$ --- is conditional on signature, providing a geometric on/off switch absent in Nelson's original construction.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Existence requires Lorentzian signature}

The chain~\eqref{eq:FP}--\eqref{eq:Schrodinger} requires:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $d_c > 0$ (OU process exists $\Rightarrow$ $\kappa_K > 0$ $\Rightarrow$ FP has a normalisable stationary solution).
\item $d_c > 0 \Leftrightarrow \det G < 0$ (Lorentzian)~\cite[Theorem~4]{K1paper}.
\end{enumerate}

For Euclidean signature ($\det G > 0$), $d_c$ is formally imaginary: $d_c = i\omega$ with $\omega = \alpha/\sigma_1$. The OU process does not exist (no real restoring rate), the FP equation has no normalisable stationary solution, and the similarity transform does not yield a physical $\psi$.

\begin{corollary}[Geometric precondition]
Within the $K{=}1$ framework, the wave function $\psi$ is well-defined if and only if $\mathrm{Sig}(G) = (1,1)$.
\end{corollary}

\subsection{Born rule as Boltzmann equilibrium}

The ground-state wave function satisfies $\psi_0 \propto e^{-U/2}$; upon normalisation,
\begin{equation}
\psi_0 = Z^{-1/2} e^{-U/2}, \qquad Z = \int e^{-U}\,d\varepsilon.
\end{equation}
Therefore $|\psi_0|^2 = Z^{-1}e^{-U} = \rho_{\mathrm{eq}}$. The Born probability equals the Boltzmann distribution --- not a postulate but a consequence of metric self-consistency.

\begin{remark}[Ground state only]
This identity holds exactly for the ground state. For excited states, $e^{-U/2}\psi_n$ can be negative and is not a probability density. Extension to excited states requires the uniqueness of $|\psi|^2$ as the conserved non-negative quantity under unitary evolution --- a standard result, not re-derived here.
\end{remark}

\section{Signature dynamics: one-way barrier}\label{sec:barrier}
%=====================================================================

We now treat $\Delta = \det G$ as a dynamical scalar and study its stochastic evolution near the signature boundary $\Delta = 0$.

\subsection{Noise coefficient from fluctuation-dissipation}

From the $K{=}1$ OU process~\cite[Proposition~7]{K1paper}, the effective temperature is $T_{\mathrm{eff}} = \sigma^2\sigma_1/(2\alpha)$. For the Rindler metric $G = \mathrm{diag}(-\sigma_1^2, 1)$, one has $|\Delta| = \sigma_1^2$, so $\sigma_1 = \sqrt{|\Delta|}$ and the noise coefficient in the Lorentzian regime is
\begin{equation}
D_L(\Delta) = \frac{\sigma^2}{2\alpha}\sqrt{|\Delta|}, \qquad \Delta < 0.
\label{eq:D_Lor}
\end{equation}
The scaling $D_L \propto \sqrt{|\Delta|} \to 0$ as $\Delta \to 0^-$ holds for any diagonal Lorentzian $G$ (since $T_{\mathrm{eff}} \propto \sigma_1 \to 0$); the coefficient $\sigma^2/(2\alpha)$ is Rindler-specific.

In the Euclidean regime ($\Delta > 0$), no OU process exists; the noise is external (e.g., thermal environment):
\begin{equation}
D_E(\Delta) = D_0 > 0, \qquad \Delta > 0.
\label{eq:D_Euc}
\end{equation}
The value $D_0$ is a modelling assumption; the one-way barrier requires only $D_0 > 0$ (any positive value suffices).

\subsection{Entropic barrier from Lorentzian side}

As $\Delta \to 0^-$: $D_L \to 0$. The Fokker--Planck stationary distribution (It\^o convention) is
\begin{equation}
\rho_{\mathrm{ss}}(\Delta) \propto \frac{1}{D(\Delta)}\exp\!\biggl(\int^{\Delta}\frac{F(s)}{D(s)}\,ds\biggr).
\label{eq:rho_ss}
\end{equation}
The $1/D$ prefactor diverges as $\Delta \to 0^-$, concentrating probability \emph{away} from the boundary. Meanwhile, $D \to 0$ means no diffusion can carry the system across $\Delta = 0$. Together these create an effective barrier: the OU process suppresses its own fluctuations near the signature boundary.

From the Euclidean side ($\Delta > 0$), $D_E = D_0$ is constant. No self-suppression operates, and the confining potential drives the system toward $\Delta_0 < 0$ (Lorentzian): the Euclidean side is downhill.

\begin{remark}[Noise interpretation]
Equation~\eqref{eq:rho_ss} is the It\^o-convention stationary distribution, consistent with the Euler--Maruyama simulation below and the Fokker--Planck barrier~\eqref{eq:barrier} in \S\ref{sec:multimode}. In the Stratonovich convention, $\rho_{\mathrm{ss}} \propto D^{-1/2}\exp(-\Phi_{\mathrm{conf}}/D)$, yielding an entropic correction $-\tfrac{1}{2}\ln D \to +\infty$ that looks like a divergent potential. Both conventions give the same physical conclusion: $D \to 0$ at the boundary prevents crossing from the Lorentzian side. The direction of the one-way barrier is convention-independent.
\end{remark}

\subsection{One-way barrier: Monte Carlo verification}

We simulate the It\^o Langevin equation for $\Delta$ with confining drift and state-dependent noise:
\begin{equation}
d\Delta = F(\Delta)\,dt + \sqrt{2D(\Delta)}\,dW_t, \qquad F(\Delta) = -\mu(\Delta - \Delta_0),
\end{equation}
where $\mu > 0$ is the confining strength and $\Delta_0 < 0$ the equilibrium. Steps that would cross $\Delta = 0$ are rejected (the trajectory remains at its previous position).

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Results} (100 runs per direction, $dt = 10^{-4}$, $T = 2.5$):
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
Direction & Crossing rate & Mean time \\
\midrule
A: Lor $\to$ Euc & 0/100 = 0\% & --- \\
B: Euc $\to$ Lor & 100/100 = 100\% & 0.42 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent Robust across all tested parameters: $D_0 \in \{0.01, 0.05, 0.1, 0.5\}$ and $\mu \in \{0.1, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0\}$ give ONE-WAY in every case.

\subsection{Intrinsic barrier: Euclidean trap test}

To confirm that the one-way barrier arises from noise asymmetry (not potential shape), an artificial Gaussian trap is added on the Euclidean side at $\Delta_E = 1.0$.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{cccc}
\toprule
$\mu_E$ & Lor $\to$ Euc & Euc $\to$ Lor & Verdict \\
\midrule
0.5 & 0\% & 100\% & ONE-WAY \\
1.0 & 0\% & 100\% & ONE-WAY \\
2.0 & 0\% & 0\% & TRAPPED \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Weak traps ($\mu_E \le 1$) do not affect the one-way barrier. Strong traps ($\mu_E \ge 2$) can hold the system in the Euclidean regime, but no natural Euclidean trap exists within $K{=}1$: the free energy $F(\sigma_1)$ is everywhere concave ($d^2F/d\sigma_1^2 = -c/(2\sigma_1) < 0$), precluding any Euclidean minimum.

\subsection{Detailed balance: analytical proof}\label{sec:detailed_balance}

\begin{proposition}[Detailed balance violation]\label{prop:DB}
Detailed balance is necessarily broken at $\Delta = 0$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The FP stationary condition $J_{\mathrm{ss}} = F\rho - \partial_\Delta(D\rho) = 0$ requires $D\rho$ to have a continuous derivative at every point.

At $\Delta = 0$:
\begin{equation}
D(0^-) = \lim_{\Delta \to 0^-} \frac{\sigma^2}{2\alpha}\sqrt{|\Delta|} = 0, \qquad D(0^+) = D_0 > 0.
\end{equation}
$D(\Delta)$ is discontinuous at $\Delta = 0$. Therefore $D\rho$ cannot have a continuous derivative at the boundary, $J_{\mathrm{ss}} \neq 0$, and detailed balance is impossible across $\Delta = 0$.

The discontinuity is not a modelling choice: $D_L \propto T_{\mathrm{eff}} \to 0$ follows from the OU self-suppression (the OU process exists only for $d_c > 0$, i.e., Lorentzian signature), while $D_E = D_0 > 0$ follows from the absence of self-suppression in the Euclidean regime.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Detailed balance \emph{holds} within the Lorentzian regime, where $D(\Delta)$ is continuous and the system obeys standard Langevin dynamics. The violation is only across the signature boundary.
\end{remark}

\section{Measurement mechanism}\label{sec:measurement}
%=====================================================================

\subsection{Collapse as Euclidean excursion}

The barrier structure provides a geometric mechanism for quantum measurement:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Wave evolution (Lorentzian).} The system resides in the Lorentzian regime. The OU process supports $\psi$ through $\rho = e^{-U/2}\psi$. Superposition is maintained; the entropic barrier prevents spontaneous collapse.

\item \textbf{Measurement trigger.} Environmental coupling increases the effective cross-mode noise $D_{\mathrm{cross}}$ (more modes, stronger coupling), reducing the barrier and exponentially increasing the collapse rate.

\item \textbf{Collapse.} $\Delta$ crosses zero. In the Euclidean regime, $d_c$ is imaginary, the OU process ceases, and $\psi$ loses its probability support.

\item \textbf{Recovery.} With no barrier from the Euclidean side, the system falls back to Lorentzian (100\% of runs, mean time $\sim 0.4$). The OU process restarts.

\item \textbf{Born rule.} The restarted OU process decays to $\rho_{\mathrm{eq}} = |\psi_0|^2$. Because the OU stationary distribution is independent of initial conditions --- this is the standard ergodic property of Ornstein--Uhlenbeck dynamics --- all information about the pre-measurement superposition is erased during re-equilibration.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Multi-mode coupling and finite barrier}\label{sec:multimode}

In $(1{+}3)$D, multiple horizon directions coexist. We adopt an illustrative mean-field model: each direction $n$ has its own OU process for $\varepsilon_n = K_n - 1$, with all modes coupled through a shared effective $\sigma_1$. The effective noise for mode~1's $\sigma_1$ decomposes as
\begin{equation}
D_{\mathrm{total}} = \underbrace{D_{\mathrm{self}}}_{\propto \sqrt{\sigma_1} \to 0} + \underbrace{D_{\mathrm{cross}}}_{> 0}.
\end{equation}
$D_{\mathrm{self}} \to 0$ at the boundary (same mechanism as single-mode), but $D_{\mathrm{cross}} > 0$ because other modes remain Lorentzian and their OU processes provide finite noise.

The Fokker--Planck stationary solution for mode~1, $\rho_{\mathrm{ss}} \propto D^{-1}\exp\!\bigl(\int F/D\,d\sigma_1\bigr)$, yields a finite barrier:
\begin{equation}
B_{\mathrm{FP}} = \Phi_{\mathrm{FP}}(\text{boundary}) - \Phi_{\mathrm{FP}}(\text{minimum}), \qquad \Phi_{\mathrm{FP}} = -\!\int \frac{F}{D}\,d\sigma_1 + \ln D.
\label{eq:barrier}
\end{equation}

\noindent\textbf{Numerical results} ($\gamma = 0.1$, $N = 8$, $\sigma = 0.3$, $\alpha = 1$):
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
& Single-mode & Multi-mode \\
\midrule
Barrier $B_{\mathrm{FP}}$ & $\to \infty$ & $\sim 6400$ \\
Rate $\Gamma$ & 0 & $\sim e^{-6400} \approx 0$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Spontaneous collapse is theoretically possible but practically negligible --- far rarer than the age of the universe. The measurement apparatus acts as a \emph{catalyst}: it increases $D_{\mathrm{cross}}$ (by coupling more environmental modes), exponentially reducing $B_{\mathrm{FP}}$ and accelerating collapse to observable timescales.

\begin{remark}[Infinite regress resolved]
In the single-mode picture, collapse requires an external push --- raising the question: what pushes the pusher? The multi-mode picture resolves this: other modes' OU fluctuations provide $D_{\mathrm{cross}}$, enabling self-consistent collapse without external input. Measurement creates nothing; it accelerates an intrinsically possible process.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Model dependence]
The multi-mode analysis uses a mean-field coupling with free parameter $\gamma$. The qualitative conclusion ($D_{\mathrm{cross}} > 0 \Rightarrow$ finite barrier) is robust, but the numerical value $B_{\mathrm{FP}} \sim 6400$ depends on $\gamma$ and $N$. Deriving $\gamma$ from $K{=}1$ geometry is an open problem.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Why measurement is irreversible}

The barrier asymmetry explains irreversibility:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Forward} (Lor $\to$ Euc): barrier $B_{\mathrm{FP}} \gg 1$. Rate $\sim e^{-B_{\mathrm{FP}}} \approx 0$ without environmental catalysis.
\item \textbf{Return} (Euc $\to$ Lor): no barrier. Spontaneous in $\sim 0.4$ time units.
\item \textbf{Net effect}: the system returns to the equilibrium distribution $\rho_{\mathrm{eq}}$ (which equals $|\psi_0|^2$ for the harmonic potential), not the original superposition. The barrier prevents return to the pre-measurement state.
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}
%=====================================================================

\subsection{Comparison with other approaches}

All five prior approaches to the measurement problem operate at the level of the Schr\"odinger equation or its solutions:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcl}
\toprule
Approach & Level & What is changed \\
\midrule
Copenhagen~\cite{vonNeumann} & Interpretation & Add projection postulate \\
Decoherence~\cite{Zurek} & Solution & Trace over environment \\
Many-worlds~\cite{Everett} & Interpretation & Remove collapse postulate \\
GRW~\cite{GRW} & Equation & Add stochastic collapse term \\
Penrose~\cite{Penrose} & Equation & Add gravitational collapse term \\
\midrule
$K{=}1$ (this work) & \textbf{Precondition} & \textbf{Signature is dynamical} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The present work does not modify the Schr\"odinger equation --- it identifies the geometric condition under which the equation exists, and shows that the condition can be dynamically violated.

\subsection{Relation to decoherence}

Standard decoherence explains the apparent disappearance of interference through environmental entanglement. The $K{=}1$ one-way barrier is compatible with decoherence and adds geometric content: decoherence is the dynamical process by which the environment pushes $\Delta$ past the entropic barrier. The framework explains \emph{why} decoherence is irreversible (one-way barrier from $D$ discontinuity) and \emph{why} outcome probabilities are $|\psi|^2$ (post-collapse Boltzmann equilibrium).

\subsection{$(1{+}3)$-dimensional extension}

In $(1{+}3)$D, $\{K{=}1\} \cong H^3$ carries $S^2$ cross-sections with area $4\pi\sigma_1^2$. Combined with the Clausius relation~\cite[Proposition~7]{K1paper}, this provides two of the three Jacobson inputs~\cite{Jacobson} for deriving the Einstein equation; the third ($S = A/4$) remains an independent assumption. The three standard pairings of a Rindler metric yield identical $d_c$~\cite[verified numerically]{K1paper}; for Schwarzschild, they do not. The core results of this paper --- one-way barrier, irreversibility, Born rule --- depend only on $D(\Delta) \to 0$ at the boundary, which holds in any dimension. The multi-mode barrier height changes quantitatively ($N = 3$ spatial directions instead of arbitrary $N$), but the qualitative mechanism is identical.

\subsection{Open questions}

\begin{enumerate}
\item The noise amplitude $\sigma$ (and hence $\hbar_{\mathrm{eff}}$) remains a free parameter. Fixing $\sigma$ geometrically would make the framework fully parameter-free.
\item The multi-mode coupling $\gamma$ is not derived from $K{=}1$.
\item Compatibility of $K{=}1$ signature change with path-integral Wick rotation.
\item Multi-particle quantum mechanics.
\item Born rule for excited states (only the ground state is derived here).
\item Absolute collapse rate (requires dimensional restoration and $(1{+}3)$D completion).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
%=====================================================================

A single algebraic fact --- $d_c > 0$ if and only if $\det G < 0$~\cite[Theorem~4]{K1paper} --- and its stochastic consequence, that the OU noise self-suppresses near the signature boundary ($D \propto \sqrt{|\Delta|} \to 0$), together yield five results:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Within this framework, the wave function exists if and only if the signature is Lorentzian.
\item Lorentzian signature is dynamically stable (entropic barrier).
\item Collapse is a temporary Euclidean excursion (one-way barrier).
\item Measurement irreversibility is a geometric fact ($D$ discontinuous at $\Delta = 0$).
\item The Born rule (ground state) is Boltzmann equilibrium ($|\psi_0|^2 = \rho_{\mathrm{eq}}$).
\end{enumerate}

The approach does not modify the Schr\"odinger equation. It operates at the level of the equation's geometric precondition --- Lorentzian signature --- and shows that the precondition is dynamical. Collapse is not a defect of quantum mechanics to be repaired; it is the temporary absence of the geometric condition that makes quantum mechanics possible.
