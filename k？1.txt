\begin{abstract}
The $K{=}1$ framework~\cite{K1} derives a restoring dynamics
$d\varepsilon = -\kappa_K\varepsilon\,dt + \sigma\,dW$
for the self-consistency deviation $\varepsilon := K-1$, with stationary
variance $\Var(\varepsilon) = T_{\mathrm{tol}} = 1/(2\pi\ell)$.
The associated thermal energy density
$\rho_\varepsilon = 1/(\pi\kappa\ell^2)$
is strictly nonzero in vacuum; general relativity, which has no
self-consistency condition, prescribes $T_{\mu\nu}=0$ in vacuum
Schwarzschild. This discrepancy is a direct consequence of the $K{=}1$
framework and requires no additional assumptions.

To obtain a tensorial correction to the Einstein equation, we introduce
Assumption~F1: $\varepsilon$ is promoted to a spacetime scalar field
with action $S_\varepsilon = \int[\frac{1}{2}(\nabla\varepsilon)^2
- \frac{1}{2}\kappa_K\varepsilon^2]\sqrt{-g}\,d^4x$.
Under~F1 the stress-energy tensor $T_{\mu\nu}^{(\varepsilon)}$ is
uniquely determined, the Jacobson thermodynamic
derivation~\cite{Jacobson} yields
$G_{\mu\nu}+\Lambda g_{\mu\nu} = 8\pi(T_{\mu\nu}^{(m)}
+T_{\mu\nu}^{(\varepsilon)})$,
and Bianchi consistency holds automatically on shell.
However, the two layers predict different variances for $\varepsilon$:
$\langle\varepsilon^2\rangle_{\mathrm{OU}} = 1/(2\pi\ell)$ depends
on~$\ell$, while
$\langle\varepsilon^2\rangle_{F1} = \kappa/(8\pi)$ does not.
F1 is therefore not a faithful promotion of the OU process but a
genuinely different model whose relation to the strict layer remains open.
For macroscopic black holes the correction is of Planck-scale magnitude
and is not observable with current or foreseeable experiments.
\end{abstract}

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Setup and notation}\label{sec:setup}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

We work in natural units $\hbar = c = k_B = G = 1$ and adopt the
metric signature $({-},{+},{+},{+})$ throughout. Under this convention
the Einstein equation reads $G_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu}
= 8\pi T_{\mu\nu}$, and for a local Rindler horizon with approximate
boost Killing vector $\chi^a$ and affine parameter $\lambda$ along the
null generators, the Jacobson relation~\cite{Jacobson} is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:jacobson_sign}
  T_{ab}\,k^a k^b = \frac{1}{8\pi}\,R_{ab}\,k^a k^b
\end{equation}
for all null $k^a$, with no relative minus sign. All sign conventions
below are consistent with~\eqref{eq:jacobson_sign}.

From the $K{=}1$ framework~\cite{K1} with normalisation $\alpha=1$:
the self-consistency parameter is $K(x) = x^\top G x$, the deviation is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:epsilon}
  \varepsilon := K - 1,
\end{equation}
and $\varepsilon$ satisfies the Ornstein--Uhlenbeck process
\begin{equation}\label{eq:OU}
  d\varepsilon = -\kappa_K\,\varepsilon\,dt + \sigma\,dW,
\end{equation}
with restoring rate $\kappa_K = 4d_c = 4/\sigma_1$, noise strength
$\sigma = \sqrt{2\kappa_K T_{\mathrm{tol}}}$, and stationary variance
\begin{equation}\label{eq:var}
  \Var(\varepsilon) = \frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa_K}
  = T_{\mathrm{tol}} = \frac{1}{2\pi\ell}.
\end{equation}
Here $\ell$ is the proper distance to the local Rindler horizon,
$\sigma_1 = \kappa\ell$ is the minimum symplectic eigenvalue of~$G$,
$\kappa$ is the surface gravity, and the identity
$d_c\cdot\sigma_1 = 1$ produces the $\kappa$-cancellation
$T_{\mathrm{tol}} = d_c\,T_H = 1/(2\pi\ell)$,
independent of~$\kappa$.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Strict layer: vacuum energy density}\label{sec:strict}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

The results of this section use only the OU
process~\eqref{eq:OU} established in~\cite{K1}. No additional
assumptions are introduced.

\begin{proposition}[$K$-fluctuation energy density]\label{prop:rho}
For the stationary state of~\eqref{eq:OU}, the thermal energy
associated with $\varepsilon$-fluctuations is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rhoA}
  \rho_\varepsilon
  := \bigl\langle\tfrac{1}{2}\kappa_K\,\varepsilon^2\bigr\rangle
  = \tfrac{1}{2}\kappa_K\,T_{\mathrm{tol}}
  = \frac{1}{\pi\kappa\ell^2}.
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
$\langle\varepsilon^2\rangle = T_{\mathrm{tol}} = 1/(2\pi\ell)$
by~\eqref{eq:var}. With $\kappa_K = 4/(\kappa\ell)$:
$\tfrac{1}{2}\kappa_K\,T_{\mathrm{tol}}
= \tfrac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{4}{\kappa\ell}\cdot\frac{1}{2\pi\ell}
= \frac{4}{4\pi\kappa\ell^2}
= \frac{1}{\pi\kappa\ell^2}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Discrepancy with GR]\label{rem:discrepancy}
In vacuum Schwarzschild, general relativity prescribes
$T_{\mu\nu} = 0$ identically.
Equation~\eqref{eq:rhoA} gives $\rho_\varepsilon > 0$ for all
finite~$\ell$, with $\rho_\varepsilon \sim \ell^{-2}$ as $\ell\to 0$.
The $K{=}1$ framework therefore predicts a nonzero vacuum energy density
that has no counterpart in~GR. The near-horizon divergence is
structurally analogous to the divergence of the Boulware vacuum
stress tensor~\cite{Boulware}, which also diverges at the horizon
in the static-observer frame.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Scope of the strict layer]\label{rem:scope}
Proposition~\ref{prop:rho} establishes that $\rho_\varepsilon > 0$
exists as a scalar quantity. It does \emph{not} determine the tensorial
form of the associated stress-energy $T_{\mu\nu}^{(\varepsilon)}$,
because the OU process~\eqref{eq:OU} is a first-order stochastic
equation for a single degree of freedom, not a covariant field equation.
In particular, the pressure $p_\varepsilon$ is not determined, so
$T_{ab}^{(\varepsilon)}k^a k^b$ is unknown and the Jacobson
derivation~\cite{Jacobson} cannot be completed within the strict layer.
To obtain a modified Einstein equation, additional structure is needed.
This is the content of the next two sections.
\end{remark}

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Assumption F1: field promotion}\label{sec:F1}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

\begin{assumption}[Field promotion --- F1]\label{ass:F1}
The self-consistency deviation $\varepsilon(x^\mu)$ is a real scalar
field on spacetime with action
\begin{equation}\label{eq:action}
  S_\varepsilon = \int\!\Bigl[\tfrac{1}{2}\,g^{\mu\nu}
  \nabla_\mu\varepsilon\,\nabla_\nu\varepsilon
  - \tfrac{1}{2}\kappa_K\,\varepsilon^2\Bigr]\sqrt{-g}\;d^4x,
\end{equation}
where $m^2 := \kappa_K = 4/(\kappa\ell)$ is the effective mass squared,
determined by the $K{=}1$ framework with no free parameter.
\end{assumption}

\noindent\textit{Motivation.}
The OU process~\eqref{eq:OU} is the overdamped (first-order) Langevin
equation for a harmonic oscillator with spring constant $\kappa_K$.
Action~\eqref{eq:action} is the standard relativistic completion: a
free massive scalar field with the same mass parameter. The promotion
is natural but not derivable from~\cite{K1} alone; it is the sole
assumption of this paper.

Under~F1 the stress-energy tensor is uniquely determined:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Tmunu}
  T_{\mu\nu}^{(\varepsilon)}
  = \nabla_\mu\varepsilon\,\nabla_\nu\varepsilon
  - g_{\mu\nu}\bigl[\tfrac{1}{2}\,\nabla_\alpha\varepsilon\,
  \nabla^\alpha\varepsilon
  - \tfrac{1}{2}\kappa_K\,\varepsilon^2\bigr].
\end{equation}
The field equation is $\Box\varepsilon + \kappa_K\,\varepsilon = 0$.
On shell, $\nabla^\mu T_{\mu\nu}^{(\varepsilon)} = 0$ identically
(Noether's theorem applied to diffeomorphism invariance
of~\eqref{eq:action}).

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Modified Einstein equation (under F1)}\label{sec:einstein}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

All results in this section assume~F1. We follow the Jacobson
thermodynamic derivation~\cite{Jacobson} with the sign conventions
stated in~\S\ref{sec:setup}.

At each spacetime point $p$ and for each null direction $k^a$,
consider a local Rindler horizon $\mathcal{H}$ with approximate boost
Killing vector $\chi^a \approx -\kappa\lambda\,k^a$. The total energy
flux across $\mathcal{H}$ now has two contributions:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:flux}
  \delta Q_{\mathrm{total}} = \delta Q_m + \delta Q_\varepsilon
  = T_H\,\delta S,
\end{equation}
where, following~\cite{Jacobson},
\[
  \delta Q_m = \kappa\!\int_0^{\lambda_1}\!\lambda\,
  T_{ab}^{(m)}\,k^a k^b\;\delta A_0\;d\lambda, \qquad
  \delta Q_\varepsilon = \kappa\!\int_0^{\lambda_1}\!\lambda\,
  T_{ab}^{(\varepsilon)}\,k^a k^b\;\delta A_0\;d\lambda.
\]
With $T_H = \kappa/(2\pi)$, $\delta S = \frac{1}{4}\delta A$, and the
Raychaudhuri equation
$\delta A = -R_{ab}\,k^a k^b\;\delta A_0\;\lambda_1^2/2$ at leading
order, equation~\eqref{eq:flux} gives, after cancellation of the common
factor $\kappa\,\delta A_0\,\lambda_1^2/2$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:null}
  T_{ab}^{(m)}\,k^a k^b + T_{ab}^{(\varepsilon)}\,k^a k^b
  = \frac{1}{8\pi}\,R_{ab}\,k^a k^b.
\end{equation}

\begin{theorem}[Modified Einstein equation]\label{thm:einstein}
Under Assumption~F1, the Jacobson thermodynamic argument applied to the
total flux~\eqref{eq:flux} yields, for all null $k^a$,
equation~\eqref{eq:null}. Since every symmetric two-tensor is determined
by its null-null components up to a term proportional to $g_{\mu\nu}$,
this implies
\begin{equation}\label{eq:modified}
  G_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda\,g_{\mu\nu}
  = 8\pi\bigl(T_{\mu\nu}^{(m)} + T_{\mu\nu}^{(\varepsilon)}\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\Lambda$ is an undetermined cosmological constant and
$T_{\mu\nu}^{(\varepsilon)}$ is given by~\eqref{eq:Tmunu}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Equation~\eqref{eq:null} for all null $k^a$ determines the traceless
part of $R_{\mu\nu} - 8\pi(T_{\mu\nu}^{(m)} +
T_{\mu\nu}^{(\varepsilon)})$. Following~\cite{Jacobson}, the trace
freedom introduces the cosmological constant $\Lambda$, giving
$R_{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{2}Rg_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu}
= 8\pi(T_{\mu\nu}^{(m)} + T_{\mu\nu}^{(\varepsilon)})$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Bianchi consistency]\label{cor:bianchi}
Under~F1, $\nabla^\mu T_{\mu\nu}^{(\varepsilon)} = 0$ on shell.
Combined with the contracted Bianchi identity
$\nabla^\mu G_{\mu\nu} = 0$, equation~\eqref{eq:modified} implies
$\nabla^\mu T_{\mu\nu}^{(m)} = 0$: matter conservation is preserved.
\end{corollary}

\begin{remark}[Statistical discrepancy between the two layers]\label{rem:discrepancy2}
Under~F1 the equipartition theorem gives
$\langle\tfrac{1}{2}\kappa_K\varepsilon^2\rangle
= \tfrac{1}{2}T_{\mathrm{tol}}$, so
\begin{equation}\label{eq:var_F1}
  \langle\varepsilon^2\rangle_{F1}
  = \frac{T_{\mathrm{tol}}}{\kappa_K}
  = \frac{1}{2\pi\ell}\cdot\frac{\kappa\ell}{4}
  = \frac{\kappa}{8\pi},
\end{equation}
which is a constant independent of~$\ell$. The strict-layer result is
$\langle\varepsilon^2\rangle_{\mathrm{OU}} = T_{\mathrm{tol}}
= 1/(2\pi\ell)$, which depends on~$\ell$. The two agree only at
$\kappa\ell = 4$, not generically. This is not a minor discrepancy:
it means F1 changes the statistical properties of~$\varepsilon$ itself,
not merely the tensorial interpretation of its energy. The OU process
and the scalar field are therefore genuinely different models for
$\varepsilon$-fluctuations, not two descriptions of the same physics.
This discrepancy is an open problem; resolving it requires either a
modified field-theory action that reproduces
$\langle\varepsilon^2\rangle = T_{\mathrm{tol}}(\ell)$ pointwise,
or a physical argument for why the scalar-field statistics should
supersede those of the OU process.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Energy densities under each model]\label{rem:energies}
Despite the variance discrepancy, the two models give well-defined
(but different) energy densities:
\[
  \rho_\varepsilon^{(\mathrm{OU})}
  = \tfrac{1}{2}\kappa_K\langle\varepsilon^2\rangle_{\mathrm{OU}}
  = \frac{1}{\pi\kappa\ell^2},
  \qquad
  \rho_\varepsilon^{(F1)}
  = \langle T_{00}^{(\varepsilon)}\rangle
  = T_{\mathrm{tol}}
  = \frac{1}{2\pi\ell}.
\]
The ratio $\rho^{(\mathrm{OU})}/\rho^{(F1)} = \tfrac{1}{2}\kappa_K = 2/(\kappa\ell)$
is large near the horizon and approaches unity at
$\ell_* = 2/\kappa$.
\end{remark}

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Schwarzschild profile (under F1)}\label{sec:schwarz}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

For Schwarzschild with mass $M$: $\kappa = 1/(4M)$, $r_s = 2M$, and
the proper distance from the horizon is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ell}
  \ell(r) = \sqrt{r(r-2M)}
  + 2M\ln\!\Bigl(\sqrt{\tfrac{r}{2M}}
  + \sqrt{\tfrac{r}{2M}-1}\Bigr).
\end{equation}
Near the horizon, $\delta r := r - 2M \ll 2M$ gives
$\ell \approx 2\sqrt{2M\,\delta r}$. The two energy densities are:

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Strict layer (potential only):}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rhoA_r}
  \rho_\varepsilon^{(A)}(r)
  = \frac{1}{\pi\kappa\ell^2}
  \approx \frac{1}{2\pi\,\delta r}.
\end{equation}

\noindent\textit{Under F1 (KE + PE):}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rhoB_r}
  \rho_\varepsilon^{(B)}(r)
  = \frac{1}{2\pi\ell}
  \approx \frac{1}{4\pi\sqrt{2M\,\delta r}}.
\end{equation}

The ratio $\rho^{(A)}/\rho^{(B)} = \tfrac{1}{2}\kappa_K = 2/(\kappa\ell)$ is
large near the horizon and approaches unity at the crossover
$\ell_* = 2/\kappa = 8M$.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Magnitude estimate}\label{sec:magnitude}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

Restoring SI units:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:SI}
  \rho_\varepsilon^{(\mathrm{SI})}
  \sim \frac{\hbar}{\pi\kappa\ell^2\,\ell_P^2\,c},
\end{equation}
where $\ell_P = \sqrt{\hbar G/c^3} \approx 1.616\times 10^{-35}$\,m.
The ratio to the Planck density scales as
$\rho_\varepsilon/\rho_{\mathrm{Planck}} \sim (\ell_P/\ell)^2$.
For a $10\,M_\odot$ black hole at $\ell = r_s \approx 3\times 10^4$\,m,
this is $\sim 10^{-78}$. The correction is far below any foreseeable
experimental sensitivity.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Discussion}\label{sec:disc}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

This paper contains two layers of results, sharply separated.

The \emph{strict layer} (\S\ref{sec:strict}) uses only the OU dynamics
of~\cite{K1} and establishes a single fact: the $K{=}1$ framework
predicts a nonzero vacuum energy density
$\rho_\varepsilon = 1/(\pi\kappa\ell^2)$ that general relativity does
not contain. This fact alone does not yield a modified field equation,
because the OU process determines only $\rho_\varepsilon$ (a scalar),
not the full stress-energy tensor $T_{\mu\nu}^{(\varepsilon)}$
(Remark~\ref{rem:scope}). The strict layer is therefore a
\emph{diagnostic}: it identifies a gap between the $K{=}1$ framework
and~GR, without resolving it.

The \emph{conditional layer} (\S\S\ref{sec:F1}--\ref{sec:schwarz})
resolves the gap by a single assumption (F1): $\varepsilon$ is a
spacetime scalar field. Under~F1, $T_{\mu\nu}^{(\varepsilon)}$ is
uniquely fixed by the action~\eqref{eq:action}, the Jacobson argument
yields the modified Einstein equation~\eqref{eq:modified}, and Bianchi
consistency holds automatically (Corollary~\ref{cor:bianchi}).
However, as shown in Remark~\ref{rem:discrepancy2}, F1 changes the
statistical properties of~$\varepsilon$: the strict layer gives
$\langle\varepsilon^2\rangle = 1/(2\pi\ell)$ (position-dependent),
while F1 gives $\langle\varepsilon^2\rangle = \kappa/(8\pi)$ (constant).
The two layers are therefore genuinely different models that share the
same mass parameter~$\kappa_K$, not descriptions of the same physics
at different levels of detail. This variance discrepancy is the
principal open problem of the present work.

The novelty of the conditional layer is not the form
of~\eqref{eq:modified} --- GR with a minimally coupled massive scalar
is textbook --- but the origin: the scalar $\varepsilon$ is motivated by
the self-consistency condition of~\cite{K1}; its mass
$m^2 = 4/(\kappa\ell)$ and temperature
$T_{\mathrm{tol}} = 1/(2\pi\ell)$ are determined by the framework with
no free parameters.

The correction is of Planck-scale magnitude for macroscopic black holes,
placing it alongside other quantum-gravity corrections (loop quantum
gravity area gap, string $\alpha'$ corrections) that are formally
derivable but experimentally inaccessible. We make no claim of
observable predictions.

Four open problems remain: (i)~the variance discrepancy between the
strict and conditional layers (Remark~\ref{rem:discrepancy2});
(ii)~whether a rigorous $(1{+}3)$-dimensional derivation can establish
F1 from the $K{=}1$ framework;
(iii)~the symplectic pairing ambiguity in $(1{+}3)$ dimensions
noted in~\cite{K1};
(iv)~whether the near-horizon divergence of $\rho_\varepsilon$ has
implications for the information paradox.
