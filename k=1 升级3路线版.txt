\begin{abstract}
\textit{Background.} Time has invariably entered physics as a primitive --- an underived input.

\textit{Framework.} Here the cost of change is modelled through an information time $dt_{\mathrm{info}}:=d\Phi/H$. The object constrained is not $dt_{\mathrm{info}}$ itself, but the signed leading quadratic form $G$ associated with the local second-order expansion of $dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2}$. Modelling this form as satisfying Axioms~R, E, T of~\cite{Realizability} yields, under nondegeneracy, $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$, equivalently $\det G<0$; the unit-cost locus is $K(x)=x^{\top}Gx=1$.

\textit{Dynamics and thermodynamics.} Within the symplectic--dissipative class $GA=\alpha J-GD$, the evolution law takes the uniquely reduced form $\dot x=(J_G-D)\nabla V$. Near $K=1$, the framework supplies the ingredients for a local Clausius relation, thereby providing Route~A's thermodynamic bridge to the Jacobson argument once the two external inputs $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ and $S\propto A$ are supplied.

\textit{Gravitational content.} In Route~B, for static, spherically symmetric metrics with $f>0$, the conjectured field-level condition $\sigma_2^2\Box\ln\sigma_i=1$ is shown to be algebraically equivalent to the vacuum Einstein equations $R_{\mu\nu}=0$ in the spherical vacuum sector (Theorem~\ref{thm:field}). Thus Lorentzian type is supplied internally at the point level, while the vacuum Einstein equations admit an equivalent reformulation in that sector.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Lorentzian signature; Information time; Symplectic-dissipative dynamics; Jacobson argument; Rindler horizon

\section{Introduction: time as consequence}

\textit{The problem.} Riemann's 1854 Habilitation lecture observed that, for a continuous manifold, the basis of metric relations must be sought outside the manifold itself, in forces acting upon it~\cite{Riemann1854}.\footnote{Riemann's formulation: ``the basis of metric relations must be sought outside that actuality, in colligating forces that operate upon it.''} Most standard formulations of spacetime physics, however, assume a metric or a causal structure as input. The Jacobson argument~\cite{Jacobson} obtains the Einstein equations from thermodynamics on local Rindler horizons, but Lorentzian signature is universally assumed --- as in the approaches of Padmanabhan~\cite{Padmanabhan} and Verlinde~\cite{Verlinde}. The EPS axiomatisation~\cite{EPS} derives causal structure from free-fall and light propagation, but uses kinematic primitives. In each of these approaches to Riemann's open problem, either the signature is prescribed or the derivation presupposes kinematic primitives.

\textit{The gap.} In the ``local cost-based leading quadratic form'' sense adopted here, existing approaches typically take Lorentzian signature or an equivalent causal structure as input; the present work addresses the prior question: when does the cost structure itself enforce a Lorentzian leading symbol?

\textit{A local answer.} In the present modelling, time is neither a coordinate nor a metric primitive, but the cost layer associated with change. Information time is introduced as
\[
dt_{\mathrm{info}}:=\frac{d\Phi}{H},
\]
where $\Phi$ parametrises structural variation and $H>0$ is the local cost per unit variation. The object constrained in the present paper is not $dt_{\mathrm{info}}$ itself, but the signed leading quadratic form $G$ associated with the local second-order expansion of $dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2}$, modelled as the leading form $Q$ of~\cite{Realizability}. \textbf{The paper's first result is local and point-level: in a $2\times 2$ realizability setting, the cost structure forces Lorentzian type} --- Axioms~R, E, T together with nondegeneracy imply $\det G<0$, equivalently $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$ (\S\ref{sec:lawI}). What is obtained at this stage is the algebraic type of the leading form, not a full reconstruction of all metric data. The induced generator $J_G$, the threshold $d_c>0$, and the unit-cost locus $K(x)=x^{\top}Gx=1$ are then algebraic consequences of this sign structure; making $K=1$ a locally self-consistent locus requires the dynamical completion of Laws~II--III. \emph{Thus the present work adopts a structural rather than fully reconstructive framework: $\Phi$ and $H$ are not fixed to specific physical quantities, and the results below constrain the algebraic type of the leading quadratic form rather than derive all metric data from first principles.} Connecting this point-level result to the full Einstein equations requires, in the thermodynamic route (Route~A), two external inputs: the identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ and the entropy--area law $S\propto A$. The further identification of the motivating information time $dt_{\mathrm{info}}$ with the later dynamical evolution parameter is not completed here and is left as an open problem.

\textit{Two routes connecting to the Einstein equations.}
\begin{itemize}
\item[\textbf{A.}] \emph{Thermodynamic bridge} (\S\S\ref{sec:lawI}--\ref{sec:thermo}): on local Rindler horizons, $K{=}1\xrightarrow{\mathrm{lin.}}\mathrm{OU}\to\mathrm{Clausius}$; under the two external inputs $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ and $S\propto A$, and through the Jacobson argument~\cite{Jacobson}, this supplies the thermodynamic input for the Jacobson route to $R_{\mu\nu}-\tfrac12 R\,g_{\mu\nu}=8\pi T_{\mu\nu}$ for general metrics.
\item[\textbf{B.}] \emph{Equivalence theorem} (\S~\ref{sec:field}): in the spherical vacuum sector, the eigenvalue self-consistency condition $\sigma_2^2\Box\ln\sigma_i=1$ --- a conjectured field-level uplift of $K{=}1$, introduced as a conjecture --- stands in strict algebraic equivalence to $R_{\mu\nu}=0$, with \emph{no thermodynamic input}. This is not a derivation of the Einstein equations from the framework axioms, but a new equivalent formulation of the vacuum equations in the language of the $K{=}1$ framework.
\end{itemize}
The two routes are complementary: Route~A applies locally on Rindler horizons but requires external thermodynamic input, whereas Route~B is thermodynamically independent but presently limited to spherical symmetry. They are summarised in Fig.~\ref{fig:routes}.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Main results.} \emph{Point-level}: under isotropic linearisation, Lorentzian signature, real spectrum of the induced generator $J_G$, and the positive stability threshold $d_{c}>0$ are equivalent (Theorem~\ref{thm:equivalence}). \emph{Route~A}: conditionally recovers $R_{\mu\nu}-\tfrac12 R\,g_{\mu\nu}=8\pi T_{\mu\nu}$ for general metrics, given the two external inputs $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ and $S\propto A$. \emph{Route~B (field-level)}: in static, spherically symmetric vacuum metrics on intervals where $f>0$, the conjectured eigenvalue self-consistency condition $\sigma_2^2\,\Box\ln\sigma_i=1$ is algebraically equivalent to $R_{\mu\nu}=0$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:field}). Full statements follow in \S\S\ref{sec:symplectic}--\ref{sec:singularity}.

\section{Metric from information time (Law~I)}\label{sec:lawI}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Law~I.} \textit{The metric $G$ is a non-degenerate symmetric bilinear form measuring local displacement cost, and the temporal direction is singled out as that of positive cost \textup{(Axiom~T of~\cite{Realizability})}.}

\medskip\noindent\textit{Framework definition.} The present paper is organised around the following layers. \emph{Input layer}: a structural parameter $\Phi$ and a positive scalar cost $H$, defining the information time $dt_{\mathrm{info}}:=d\Phi/H$. \emph{Axiom layer}: a signed leading quadratic form $G$ associated with $dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2}$, modelled as satisfying Axioms~R, E, T of~\cite{Realizability} together with nondegeneracy. \emph{Additional condition}: the unit-cost locus $K(x):=x^{\top}Gx=1$, imposed as a point-level self-consistency condition. \emph{Dynamical completion}: Laws~II--III determine the canonical local evolution law and the critical-damping selection. \emph{Internal conclusions}: at the point level one obtains Lorentzian signature $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$, the threefold equivalence of Theorem~\ref{thm:equivalence}, the local restoring law $\kappa_K=4d_c$ at a normalised eigenvector base point, and a unified local dynamics combining irreversible and reversible sectors; conditionally, under the conjectured Route~B uplift, one obtains algebraic equivalence to the Einstein vacuum equations in the spherical vacuum sector (Theorem~\ref{thm:field}). \emph{External inputs}: only for Route~A, the thermal identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ and the entropy--area law $S\propto A$ are required.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Main bridge.} The information time is defined by
\[
dt_{\mathrm{info}}:=\frac{d\Phi}{H},
\]
where $\Phi$ parametrises structural variation and $H>0$ is the local \emph{scalar} cost per unit variation of $\Phi$ (an entropic resistance, unrelated to the matrix weight $H_{*}$ of~\S\ref{sec:stability}). Associated with the local second-order part of $dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2}$, we introduce a signed leading quadratic form $G$, heuristically written as $G:=\nabla^{2}(dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2})$. Strictly speaking, this is shorthand: $G$ denotes the \emph{signed leading quadratic coefficient form} associated with $dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2}$, not a full coordinate-level reconstruction obtained from an explicit expansion of $dt_{\mathrm{info}}$ in a specified chart. More precisely, the present framework models this signed leading coefficient form as the leading quadratic form $Q$ of~\cite{Realizability}. Thus the step from $G$ to the realizability theorem is a \emph{structural identification at the level of the signed leading form}, not an independent re-derivation of Axioms~R, E, T from the information-time construction itself.

At the motivating level, the nonnegative cost is represented by the positive-part construction $(x^{\top}Gx)_{+}$, exactly as in the realizability framework of~\cite{Realizability}. Once the signature conclusion has been obtained, the later algebra and dynamics work directly with the underlying signed leading form $G$ itself, and no longer require the positive-part construction. The positive-part cost belongs to the realizability layer that constrains the signature of the leading form. The later potential
\[
V=\tfrac12(K-1)^2
\]
is not the primitive cost functional of the realizability layer, but an additional dynamical choice introduced after the Lorentzian type of $G$ has been established, in order to study local restoring behaviour near $K=1$. Hence the main theorem of~\cite{Realizability} applies directly: Axioms~R, E, T together with nondegeneracy imply
\[
\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1),
\qquad\text{equivalently}\qquad
\det G<0.
\]
Once this Lorentzian signature conclusion has been fixed, the subsequent dynamical development does not call upon Axiom~R again. Axiom~R may be read intuitively as saying that there exist arbitrarily small forward displacements whose information cost vanishes relative to their size\footnote{Here ``size'' refers to the Euclidean norm $\|x\|$.}; this is the ingredient that rules out a positive-definite leading form. Thus, in the present modelling, the cost structure forces Lorentzian signature at the level of the signed leading form.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Deeper motivation.} Axioms~R, E, T of~\cite{Realizability} may be viewed as a realizability encoding of the Hessian-level degeneracy structure associated with the local second-order cost form of $dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2}$, motivated by the requirement that nontrivial variational motion remain realizable rather than collapse to trivial displacement. The present paper does not carry out a full reverse reconstruction of Axioms~R, E, T from that structure; rather, it uses the associated signed leading form $G$ together with the realizability theorem of~\cite{Realizability} as the operative bridge. For relative self-containment, \S\ref{sec:lawI} recalls only the minimal mechanism needed from~\cite{Realizability}, rather than reproducing the full proof.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Scope of the claim.} What is constrained here is the \emph{algebraic type} of $G$ (its signature), not all of its specific values. Accordingly, $G$ should be read as a local cost-form forced by realizability, rather than as a complete reconstruction of the spacetime metric $g_{\mu\nu}$. The present work is therefore structural rather than fully reconstructive: $\Phi$ and $H$ are not fixed to specific physical quantities, and $dt_{\mathrm{info}}$ serves as the motivating cost-time layer from which the local leading form $G$ is introduced, rather than as a complete first-principles derivation of all metric data.

The quadratic form
\[
K(x):=x^{\top}Gx
\]
measures local temporal cost, and the condition $K=1$ identifies the self-consistent locus. We adopt the normalisation $K=1$ by a choice of cost units, without loss of generality at the level of the present local theory. This condition is \emph{not} a conclusion of~\cite{Realizability}, but an additional self-consistency condition introduced in the present paper.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Role of information time in the present paper.} In the present work, $dt_{\mathrm{info}}$ plays a motivating and structural role: it specifies the cost-time layer from which the local leading form $G$ is introduced, and, as shown in \S\ref{sec:infodynamics}, it also determines a distinguished irreversible principal direction of the local dynamics. The later dynamical sections use an effective evolution parameter $t$; an explicit first-principles identification between that parameter and $dt_{\mathrm{info}}$ is not derived here. Throughout this paper, the dynamical evolution parameter $t$ is an effective evolution time independent of $dt_{\mathrm{info}}$; establishing their full relation is left to future work. In this sense, Law~I establishes the local cost-geometric layer of the framework, while a complete dynamical closure from information time alone remains open. Throughout the paper, this $2\times 2$ construction is applied on distinguished local two-planes, such as the $(t,r)$ wedge of a local Rindler horizon in Route~A and the radial-time plane in spherical symmetry for Route~B. These are not arbitrary two-planes: they are the physically distinguished local planes selected by the problem under consideration.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Sign conventions.} In this section, $G$ is written in the cost-sign convention, so that the temporal direction carries positive cost. In later sections, especially in the Rindler realisation of Route~A, we switch to the spacetime-sign convention
\[
g_{\mu\nu}=-G_{\mu\nu}.
\]
This overall sign reversal does not affect the Lorentzian signature statement $\det G<0$; it changes only the convention used when the quadratic form is presented in spacetime language. In later sections, when the physical spacetime metric is used directly, we switch notation explicitly to $g_{\mu\nu}$ to avoid conflating it with the cost-sign form of \S\ref{sec:lawI}.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Minimal content used from~\cite{Realizability}.} For relative self-containment, the mechanism used here is the following. Axiom~E reduces the leading-order displacement cost to a non-degenerate quadratic type $Q$. Axiom~T supplies a distinguished temporal direction with strictly positive cost, thereby excluding a purely negative-definite leading form. Axiom~R, via the limiting argument proved in~\cite{Realizability}, requires spatially nontrivial forward displacements whose cost-to-size ratio tends to zero, thereby excluding a positive-definite leading form. In two dimensions, once both definite signatures are excluded and nondegeneracy is imposed, the only remaining inertia is $(1,1)$. Identifying the present $G$ with this signed leading form $Q$, one obtains $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$, equivalently $\det G<0$. The full statement, including the precise cost functional and the limiting argument in Axiom~R, is proved in~\cite{Realizability}.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Consequence.} Lorentzian signature is therefore derived from the cost structure at the level of the leading form: $G$ is indefinite, and its null set
\[
\{x:x^{\top}Gx=0\}
\]
determines a local causal structure. In general relativity, the Lorentzian metric is prescribed on a pre-existing differentiable manifold; here the Lorentzian algebraic type of the leading cost form is derived rather than postulated.

\section{Information-time-induced dissipative flow}\label{sec:infodynamics}

\noindent\textit{From information time to irreversible dynamics.}
Law~I identifies the local signed leading cost form $G$, but it does not yet specify how states evolve. The present section extracts from the information-time layer a natural \emph{irreversible} dynamical sector. The conclusion is not yet the full symplectic--dissipative law of later sections: what is obtained here is the dissipative principal direction induced by information time. The reversible symplectic sector will be supplied separately, in \S\ref{sec:symplectic}, as the unique algebraic completion in the $2\times 2$ Lorentzian setting.

\medskip

Let $\Phi=\Phi(x)$ be a local structural functional of the state $x\in\mathbb{R}^{2}$, and recall the information time
\[
dt_{\mathrm{info}}:=\frac{d\Phi}{H},
\qquad H(x)>0.
\]
Equivalently,
\[
\frac{d\Phi}{dt_{\mathrm{info}}}=H.
\]
Writing $\dot x_{\mathrm{info}}:=dx/dt_{\mathrm{info}}$, the chain rule gives
\begin{equation}
\nabla\Phi(x)\cdot \dot x_{\mathrm{info}}=H(x).
\label{eq:info_constraint}
\end{equation}
Thus the information-time layer imposes a scalar constraint on the state velocity: the component of motion along the structural gradient must reproduce the local cost rate. Note that this constraint forces $\Phi$ to increase along $t_{\mathrm{info}}$.

\medskip

To select a concrete velocity from~\eqref{eq:info_constraint}, we impose the following local minimality principle: among all velocities satisfying~\eqref{eq:info_constraint}, the realised motion is the one of smallest Euclidean norm. This is the natural local least-motion choice compatible with the information-time constraint.

\begin{proposition}[Information-time principal direction]\label{prop:infodir}
Let $\Phi=\Phi(x)$ be smooth and let $H(x)>0$. Among all velocities $\dot x_{\mathrm{info}}$ satisfying
\[
\nabla\Phi(x)\cdot \dot x_{\mathrm{info}}=H(x),
\]
the unique minimiser of $\|\dot x_{\mathrm{info}}\|^{2}$ is collinear with $\nabla\Phi(x)$, namely
\begin{equation}
\dot x_{\mathrm{info}}
=
\frac{H(x)}{\|\nabla\Phi(x)\|^{2}}\nabla\Phi(x).
\label{eq:minflow_plus}
\end{equation}
This velocity defines the \emph{information-time flow}: it increases $\Phi$ at the prescribed rate $H$ with minimal instantaneous cost.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Minimise $\|\dot x\|^{2}$ subject to the linear constraint
$\nabla\Phi(x)\cdot \dot x=H(x)$. By the Lagrange multiplier method,
\[
2\dot x-\lambda\,\nabla\Phi(x)=0,
\]
so $\dot x=(\lambda/2)\nabla\Phi(x)$. Substituting into the constraint gives
\[
\frac{\lambda}{2}\|\nabla\Phi(x)\|^{2}=H(x),
\]
hence
\[
\dot x=\frac{H(x)}{\|\nabla\Phi(x)\|^{2}}\nabla\Phi(x).\qed
\end{proof}

\medskip

\noindent\textit{Dissipative direction in physical time.}
The information-time flow increases $\Phi$. In the later physical evolution, the structural functional $\Phi$ typically decreases (e.g., free energy, Lyapunov functional). Therefore the \emph{dissipative} direction relevant for the physical dynamics is opposite to the information-time flow. Introducing an effective physical time parameter $t$ (distinct from $t_{\mathrm{info}}$), the dissipative sector is taken to be
\begin{equation}
\dot x_{\mathrm{irr}} = -\frac{H(x)}{\|\nabla\Phi(x)\|^{2}}\nabla\Phi(x).
\label{eq:dissipative}
\end{equation}
This sign choice will be adopted in the subsequent construction of Law~II. The relation between $t$ and $t_{\mathrm{info}}$ is not fixed here and is left as an open problem.

\medskip

\noindent\textit{Local quadratic reduction.}
Near a base point, suppose the structural functional admits the leading approximation
\[
\Phi(x)\approx \Phi_{0}+\tfrac12 x^{\top}Gx,
\]
where $G$ is the signed leading quadratic form identified in Law~I. Then
\[
\nabla\Phi(x)\approx Gx,
\]
and the dissipative direction becomes
\begin{equation}
\dot x_{\mathrm{irr}}
\approx
-\frac{H(x)}{\|Gx\|^{2}}\,Gx.
\label{eq:Gflow}
\end{equation}
Thus the information-time layer naturally generates a local gradient-type irreversible flow determined by the same leading form $G$ whose Lorentzian type was established in \S\ref{sec:lawI}.

\medskip

\noindent\textit{Relation to the self-consistency potential.}
The later dynamical analysis is organised not by $\Phi$ directly, but by the self-consistency functional
\[
K(x):=x^{\top}Gx,
\qquad
V(x):=\tfrac12(K-1)^{2}.
\]
Since
\[
\nabla K=2Gx,
\qquad
\nabla V=2(K-1)Gx,
\]
the dissipative direction~\eqref{eq:Gflow} is locally collinear with the gradient sector used later in the paper. In this sense, the information-time layer supplies the \emph{dissipative principal sector} of the later dynamics: once the self-consistency potential $V$ is adopted, the natural irreversible completion is of gradient type,
\[
\dot x_{\mathrm{irr}}=-D\,\nabla V,
\qquad D\ge 0.
\]
What is not yet supplied by information time alone is the reversible sector orthogonal to the gradient flow. That sector will be introduced in \S\ref{sec:symplectic} as the unique algebraic completion in the $2\times2$ Lorentzian setting.

\begin{remark}[Scope of the present section]
The present section does not identify the later effective evolution parameter with $dt_{\mathrm{info}}$ globally, nor does it derive the full symplectic--dissipative law from information time alone. Its claim is narrower and structural: the information-time layer already determines a distinguished irreversible direction, and this direction is locally generated by the same leading form $G$ that governs the cost geometry.
\end{remark}

\section{Symplectic structure from \texorpdfstring{$G$}{G}}\label{sec:symplectic}

\noindent\textit{From irreversible direction to algebraic completion.}
Section~\ref{sec:infodynamics} extracted from the information-time layer a distinguished dissipative principal direction: locally, the leading cost form $G$ generates a gradient-type irreversible flow. What remains is to determine a complementary reversible sector that is algebraically compatible with the same Lorentzian cost geometry. The present section supplies that sector in terms of a linear-algebraic completion, without claiming that the full nonlinear dynamics preserves $K$.

\medskip

\noindent\textit{Linear-algebraic structure preservation.}
The guiding principle is that the reversible drift, at the \emph{linearised} level, should be compatible with the cost structure encoded by
\[
K(x)=x^{\top}Gx.
\]
Compatibility is expressed by requiring that the linear generator $M$ satisfy
\[
GM \in \mathrm{Skew}_{2},
\]
so that the linearised flow is $G$-antisymmetric. This condition ensures that the quadratic form $K$ is conserved by the linearised reversible dynamics. Imposing, in addition, the Euclidean normalisation condition that the generator reduce to $\alpha J$ when $G=I$, one obtains a unique algebraic completion. The construction below requires only nondegeneracy; the specifically Lorentzian reading --- real spectrum and the positive stability threshold $d_c>0$ --- enters subsequently through Theorem~\ref{thm:spectral} and the isotropic interpretation of \S\ref{sec:stability}.

\begin{theorem}[Structural Uniqueness]\label{thm:unique}
Let $G$ be a non-degenerate symmetric $2\times 2$ real matrix. Among all matrices $M$ such that
\textup{(i)}~$GM$ is skew-symmetric and
\textup{(ii)}~$M=\alpha J$ when $G=I$ \textup{(Euclidean normalisation)},
the unique solution is $J_G=\alpha G^{-1}J$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Since $\dim(\mathrm{Skew}_2)=1$ and $J$ spans $\mathrm{Skew}_2$, condition~\textup{(i)} forces $GM=\beta J$ for some $\beta\in\mathbb{R}$, hence $M=\beta G^{-1}J$; condition~\textup{(ii)} fixes $\beta=\alpha$.\qed
\end{proof}

\medskip

\noindent\textit{From admissible generator to algebraic reversible completion.}
Theorem~\ref{thm:unique} identifies $J_G$ as the unique generator compatible with the linearised cost structure and the Euclidean normalisation. The next result strengthens this interpretation: it shows that, within the upgraded information-time reading, $J_G$ is not merely an admissible algebraic generator, but the unique local linear-algebraic completion of the dissipative principal dynamics under the stated principles.

\begin{theorem}[Linear-algebraic characterisation of the reversible sector]\label{thm:completion}
Let $G$ be a non-degenerate symmetric $2\times2$ real matrix, and let $V$ be a smooth local potential. Consider a local reversible completion of gradient-type dynamics of the form
\[
Y=M(G)\,\nabla V,
\]
where $M(G)$ depends only on the local cost geometry encoded by $G$. Assume:

\begin{itemize}
\item[\textup{(i)}] \textup{(locality)} $Y$ depends on the state only through $G$ and $\nabla V$;
\item[\textup{(ii)}] \textup{(linearised structure preservation)} $GM(G)\in\mathrm{Skew}_2$;
\item[\textup{(iii)}] \textup{(Euclidean normalisation)} $M(I)=\alpha J$ for a fixed constant $\alpha>0$;
\item[\textup{(iv)}] \textup{(normalisation rigidity)} the local geometry determines the reversible direction but does not introduce an additional $G$-dependent scalar normalisation.
\end{itemize}

Then, under assumptions (i)--(iv), the reversible completion is fixed uniquely in the form
\[
Y=J_G\nabla V,
\qquad
J_G=\alpha G^{-1}J.
\]
Equivalently, the unique linear-algebraic completion satisfying (i)--(iv) is
\[
M(G)=\alpha G^{-1}J.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By assumption~\textup{(ii)}, the matrix $GM(G)$ is skew-symmetric. In two dimensions,
\[
\dim(\mathrm{Skew}_2)=1,
\]
and $J$ spans $\mathrm{Skew}_2$. Hence there exists a scalar $\beta(G)$ such that
\[
GM(G)=\beta(G)\,J.
\]
Since $G$ is non-degenerate,
\[
M(G)=\beta(G)\,G^{-1}J.
\]
Therefore every local linear-algebraic completion is necessarily of the form
\[
Y=\beta(G)\,G^{-1}J\,\nabla V.
\]
This proves uniqueness of the reversible direction up to a scalar factor.

Now impose the Euclidean normalisation~\textup{(iii)}. Setting $G=I$ gives
\[
M(I)=\beta(I)\,J=\alpha J,
\]
hence
\[
\beta(I)=\alpha.
\]
Thus the Euclidean limit fixes the normalisation at the identity.

It remains to determine whether $\beta(G)$ may vary with $G$. Assumption~\textup{(iv)} excludes such a dependence: once the local geometry has fixed the reversible direction through the factor $G^{-1}J$, no additional $G$-dependent scalar dressing is allowed. In other words, the reversible completion is permitted to depend on the local geometry only through its directional structure, not through an independent geometry-dependent rescaling. Therefore
\[
\beta(G)\equiv \alpha
\]
for all non-degenerate symmetric $2\times2$ matrices $G$, and hence
\[
M(G)=\alpha G^{-1}J.
\]
Substituting into $Y=M(G)\nabla V$ yields
\[
Y=\alpha G^{-1}J\,\nabla V=J_G\nabla V,
\]
as claimed.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Status of the rigidity assumption]
The substantive content of Theorem~\ref{thm:completion} is the uniqueness of the reversible direction under the linearised structure-preserving condition $GM\in\mathrm{Skew}_2$. The final step fixing the scalar factor to $\alpha$ uses the additional normalisation-rigidity principle, namely that the local geometry determines the reversible direction but does not supply an independent geometry-dependent rescaling. This principle is structural rather than purely algebraic.
\end{remark}

\medskip

\begin{remark}[Non-degeneracy vs.\ signature]
Theorems~\ref{thm:unique} and~\ref{thm:completion} require only $\det G\neq 0$; at that level, they are statements about structural admissibility and algebraic completion, not yet about Lorentzian dynamics. The specifically Lorentzian content enters through Theorem~\ref{thm:spectral}: when $\det G<0$, the completion $J_G$ becomes hyperbolic with real eigenvalues, and the corresponding stability threshold $d_c>0$ emerges in the isotropic reading. Since $GJ_G=\alpha J$ is skew-symmetric, $J_G$ is $G$-antisymmetric by construction; the Lorentzian condition is what turns this algebraic completion into the reversible sector relevant for the later stability and thermodynamic analysis. It is important to stress that $J_G\nabla V$ does \emph{not} conserve $K$ globally; only its linearisation does. The full nonlinear expression for $\dot K$ is given in Theorem~\ref{thm:restoring}.
\end{remark}

\medskip

\begin{theorem}[Spectral Reality]\label{thm:spectral}
Let $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$. The characteristic polynomial of $G^{-1}J$ is $\lambda^2+1/\det G=0$. Since $\det G<0$, the roots are real: $\lambda=\pm\sqrt{-1/\det G}$. In the isotropic reading, the corresponding stability boundary is
\begin{equation}
d_c(\alpha)=\alpha\sqrt{-1/\det G}>0.\label{eq:dc}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$\mathrm{tr}(G^{-1}J)=0$ and $\det(G^{-1}J)=1/\det G$; the characteristic polynomial $\lambda^2+1/\det G=0$ then gives $\mathrm{spec}(J_G)=\{\pm d_c\}$. For the isotropic case $H_*=I$, $\max \mathrm{Re}\,\mathrm{spec}(J_G-dI)=d_c-d$, which vanishes at $d=d_c$.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Necessity of Lorentzian Signature]\label{thm:necessity}
If $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(2,0)$ or $(0,2)$, then $\det G>0$, so $\lambda^2+1/\det G=0$ has purely imaginary roots; no positive real $d_c>0$ exists in the sense of Theorem~\ref{thm:equivalence}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$\det G>0$ gives $1/\det G>0$, so $\lambda^2=-1/\det G<0$ and both roots are purely imaginary; no positive real eigenvalue $d_c>0$ exists.\qed
\end{proof}

\section{The isotropic stability boundary}\label{sec:stability}

\noindent\textit{From algebraic identity to dynamical threshold.}
Section~\ref{sec:symplectic} established the purely algebraic identity
$d_{c}=\alpha\sqrt{-1/\det G}$ for any $2\times2$ Lorentzian $G$:
$d_{c}$ is simply the magnitude of the real eigenvalues of $J_{G}$.
No dynamics has been invoked so far.
The purpose of the present section is to give this algebraic quantity
a \emph{stability interpretation} under one additional specification:
the linearisation weight matrix is taken as $H_{*}=I$ (isotropic).
When $H_{*}=I$, the auxiliary linear system $d(\delta x)/dt=(J_{G}-dI)\delta x$
has $\max\mathrm{Re}\,\mathrm{spec}(J_{G}-dI)=d_{c}-d$; hence $d>d_{c}$
guarantees exponential decay of all modes, $d=d_{c}$ is critical damping,
and $d_{c}>0$ becomes a positive stability threshold.
This interprets the signature--spectrum triple of Fig.~\ref{fig:triangle}
in dynamical terms. For general positive-definite $H_{*}\neq I$,
the zero-eigenvalue locus of $(J_{G}-dI)H_{*}$ is unchanged
(Proposition~\ref{prop:diag_inv}), but the full Lyapunov stability
threshold may shift (Remark~\ref{rem:Hstar_dep}); the equivalence
stated in Theorem~\ref{thm:equivalence} therefore holds \emph{within
the isotropic reading}. No new algebraic derivation is introduced
in this section.

\medskip

\begin{theorem}[Signature-Stability Equivalence]\label{thm:equivalence}
For non-degenerate symmetric $2\times 2$ real $G$, under the isotropic linearisation $H_{*}=I$, the following are equivalent:
\begin{itemize}
\item[\textup{(a)}] The isotropic stability threshold
\[
d_{c}=\alpha\sqrt{-1/\det G}
\]
is a positive real quantity.
\item[\textup{(b)}] $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$ \textup{(Lorentzian)}, equivalently $\det G<0$.
\end{itemize}
For general $H_{*}$, the zero-eigenvalue locus in $d$ is invariant under invertible right multiplication by $H_{*}$ (Proposition~\ref{prop:diag_inv}), but the full real-part stability interpretation may fail (Remark~\ref{rem:Hstar_dep}); thus $d_{c}$ is a signature invariant only within the isotropic stability interpretation.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$d_{c}>0 \Leftrightarrow -1/\det G>0$ (eq.~\eqref{eq:dc}) $\Leftrightarrow \det G<0 \Leftrightarrow \mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$, via Theorems~\ref{thm:spectral} and~\ref{thm:necessity}.\qed
\end{proof}

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Scope.} The closed-form $d_{c}=\alpha\sqrt{-1/\det G}$ relies on $\dim(\mathrm{Skew}_{2})=1$; higher-dimensional extensions require additional structure and are not fully developed in the present paper.

\begin{remark}[Isotropic scaling]
$H$ (scalar, \S\ref{sec:lawI}) is the local cost per unit variation of $\Phi$ (an entropic resistance), appearing in $dt_{\mathrm{info}}=d\Phi/H$; $H_{*}$ (matrix) denotes the cost-function weight matrix in $d(\delta x)/dt=(J_{G}-dI)H_{*}\,\delta x$ (in the linearised stability analysis of the present section). The two are distinct mathematical objects and are not identified in the present paper; establishing an explicit relation between them from a single deeper cost principle is left to future work. $H_{*}=I$ is the default. For $H_{*}=cI$ ($c>0$), the zero-real-part threshold in $d$ remains $d_{c}$, although rates are rescaled by $c$. For general positive-definite $H_{*}$, the full stability threshold may shift.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Dependence on $H_{*}$]\label{rem:Hstar_dep}
With $G=\mathrm{diag}(0.1,-10)$ and $H_{*}=\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}1&0.9\\0.9&1\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$, $d_{c}^{\mathrm{iso}}=1$ while $d_{c}(H_{*})=4.55$, confirming that $d_{c}$ may depend on $H_{*}$ outside the isotropic class. Here $d_{c}(H_{*})$ refers to the full real-part stability threshold, not the zero-eigenvalue locus (which remains at $d_{c}=1$ by Proposition~\ref{prop:diag_inv}).
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}[$H_{*}$-invariance of the zero-eigenvalue locus]\label{prop:diag_inv}
For any invertible $H_{*}$, the zero-eigenvalue values of $(J_{G}-dI)H_{*}$ coincide with those of $J_{G}-dI$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$\det((J_{G}-dI)H_{*})=\det(J_{G}-dI)\det H_{*}$; since $\det H_{*}\neq 0$, the determinant vanishes exactly when $\det(J_{G}-dI)=0$.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
For the Rindler metric with diagonal $H_{*}>0$, the zero-eigenvalue threshold in $d$ remains $d_{c}=\alpha/(\kappa\ell)$. The thermodynamic applications of \S~\ref{sec:thermo} use this threshold directly, without requiring the full isotropic equivalence of Theorem~\ref{thm:equivalence}.
\end{remark}

\section{Unique dynamics (Law~II)}\label{sec:lawII}

\noindent\textit{From two sectors to one canonical law.}
The preceding sections have now isolated two complementary ingredients of the dynamics. First, \S\ref{sec:infodynamics} extracted from the information-time layer a distinguished irreversible sector of gradient type. Second, \S\ref{sec:symplectic} identified the unique linear-algebraic reversible completion compatible with the Lorentzian cost form $G$, namely the generator $J_G=\alpha G^{-1}J$. Law~II combines these two sectors into a single canonical evolution law.

\medskip

\noindent\textbf{Law~II.} \textit{The generator of the dynamics splits as $GA = \alpha J - GD$ with $D\ge 0$ (positive-semidefinite dissipation). This symplectic-dissipative decomposition is the content of Law~II. In the present upgraded reading, it expresses the combination of two structures: an irreversible gradient sector, naturally suggested by the information-time layer, and a reversible sector, fixed as the unique linear-algebraic completion associated with $G$. Under this axiom, the generator is uniquely reduced to the form $J_{G}-D$, so that the evolution law for any smooth potential $V$ is
\[
\dot{x}=(J_{G}-D)\nabla V,
\]
with the dissipation matrix $D$ remaining free within the positive-semidefinite class until it is fixed further, in the isotropic critical-damping reading, by Law~III. The local restoring role of $\{K=1\}$ is specified separately by Law~III.}

The splitting $GA=\alpha J-GD$ is therefore the canonical synthesis of the reversible and irreversible sectors. Combined with Theorem~\ref{thm:unique} (which fixes $J_G$), it yields the unique admissible evolution law stated in Theorem~\ref{thm:lawII}.

\begin{theorem}[Canonical Unified Form of Law~II]\label{thm:lawII}
Let $G$ be non-degenerate, let
\[
J_G=\alpha G^{-1}J
\]
be the unique linear-algebraic reversible completion associated with $G$ \textup{(Theorems~\ref{thm:unique} and~\ref{thm:completion})}, and let $D\ge 0$ be a positive-semidefinite dissipation matrix representing the irreversible gradient sector. If the generator satisfies the Law~II synthesis condition
\[
GA=\alpha J-GD,
\]
then the evolution law for any smooth potential $V$ takes the uniquely reduced form
\begin{equation}
\dot x=(J_G-D)\nabla V.
\label{eq:lawIIcanonical}
\end{equation}
Equivalently, the physically admissible evolution laws in the present framework are exactly those obtained by combining the reversible completion $J_G\nabla V$ with the dissipative gradient sector $-D\nabla V$:
\[
\dot x
=
\underbrace{J_G\nabla V}_{\text{reversible (linear-algebraic completion)}}
\;+\;
\underbrace{(-D\nabla V)}_{\text{irreversible, dissipative}}.
\]
Thus Law~II is the canonical synthesis of the two sectors isolated in the preceding sections: the irreversible principal direction suggested by the information-time layer, and the unique reversible completion fixed by the Lorentzian cost geometry.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Starting from the Law~II synthesis condition
\[
GA=\alpha J-GD,
\]
left-multiplication by $G^{-1}$ gives
\[
A=G^{-1}(\alpha J-GD)=\alpha G^{-1}J-D=J_G-D.
\]
Therefore, for any smooth potential $V$,
\[
\dot x=A\nabla V=(J_G-D)\nabla V,
\]
which is~\eqref{eq:lawIIcanonical}. The decomposition into reversible and irreversible sectors is immediate from the two terms: $J_G\nabla V$ is the linear-algebraic completion fixed by Theorems~\ref{thm:unique} and~\ref{thm:completion}, while $-D\nabla V$ is the dissipative gradient sector associated with the positive-semidefinite matrix $D$.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Status of the synthesis]
Theorem~\ref{thm:lawII} does not claim that the full symplectic--dissipative law is derived from information time alone. Rather, its content is that once the irreversible gradient sector and the reversible linear-algebraic completion have been identified, the Law~II condition $GA=\alpha J-GD$ yields their unique canonical synthesis in the form~\eqref{eq:lawIIcanonical}.
\end{remark}

For Lorentzian $G$, the reversible completion $J_G$ has symmetric part
\[
S=\frac12(J_G+J_G^\top)\neq 0,
\]
so the present synthesis departs from the standard GENERIC requirement of Euclidean antisymmetry: reversibility is encoded here relative to the Lorentzian cost form $G$, not relative to a Euclidean metric.

\section{Critical damping and fluctuation structure (Law~III)}\label{sec:lawIII}

\noindent\textbf{Law~III (Dissipation selection).}
\emph{The dissipation matrix $D$ is selected by requiring that
$K{=}1$ be a locally stable equilibrium of the drift
$\dot x=(J_G-D)\nabla V$ with $V=\tfrac12(K{-}1)^2$, and by choosing
the smallest isotropic dissipation compatible with that stability.}

\medskip

\noindent\textit{From canonical synthesis to quantitative dissipation.}
The preceding sections have identified the two structural sectors of the dynamics: the irreversible gradient sector, suggested by the information-time layer, and the reversible linear-algebraic sector, fixed uniquely by the Lorentzian cost geometry. Law~II canonically synthesised these two ingredients into the unified evolution law
\[
\dot x=(J_G-D)\nabla V.
\]
What remains is to determine the \emph{strength} of the dissipative sector, that is, to select a concrete dissipation matrix $D$.

\medskip

\noindent\textit{Minimal isotropic stabilisation of the self-consistency locus.}
The natural criterion is that the unit-cost locus $K{=}1$, introduced in Law~I as the self-consistency condition of the framework, should be dynamically stable: small departures from $K{=}1$ should be driven back toward it by the irreversible sector, while the reversible sector (at linearised level) is tangent to the local cost geometry. Restricting to the isotropic class
\[
D=dI,\qquad d>0,
\]
the relevant scale is the spectral threshold $d_c$ obtained in \S\ref{sec:stability}. Stability requires $d\ge d_c$, while the boundary choice $d=d_c$ gives critical damping. Law~III therefore selects the unique member of the isotropic class that stabilises $K{=}1$ with minimal dissipation,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Dcritical}
D=d_cI,
\end{equation}
with
\[
d_c=\alpha\sqrt{-1/\det G}.
\]

The remainder of this section develops the consequences of this selection: first, the local effective restoring rate toward $K{=}1$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:restoring}); and second, the natural separation of fluctuations on $\{K=1\}$ into a thermodynamic class and a purely kinematic class.

\begin{theorem}[Local Effective Restoring Rate]\label{thm:restoring}
Let $G$ be a $2\times 2$ Lorentzian metric, $D=d_{c}I$ (Law~III), and $J_{G}=\alpha G^{-1}J$ (Theorems~\ref{thm:unique} and~\ref{thm:completion}). Define $K(x)=x^{\top}Gx$, $V=\tfrac{1}{2}(K-1)^{2}$, with drift $\dot{x}=(J_{G}-d_{c}I)\nabla V$. Then for all $x\in\mathbb{R}^{2}$:
\begin{equation}
\dot{K} = 4(K-1)\bigl[\alpha\,x^{\top}(JG)\,x - d_{c}\,x^{\top}G^{2}x\bigr].\label{eq:Kdot}
\end{equation}
At any $x_{*}\in\{K=1\}$ with $Gx_{*}=\lambda x_{*}$, the term $x_{*}^{\top}JGx_{*}=0$; freezing the coefficient in~\eqref{eq:Kdot} at such an $x_{*}$ and retaining the first-order term in $(K-1)$ gives
\begin{equation}
\dot{K} = -4\,d_{c}\,x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}\cdot(K-1)+o(K-1).\label{eq:Kdot_eigen}
\end{equation}
If, in addition, the base point is normalised by $x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}=1$ (i.e., $x_*$ is a unit-eigenvalue $G$-eigenvector), then
\begin{equation}
\dot{K} = -\kappa_{K}(K-1)+o(K-1),\qquad \kappa_{K}=4\,d_{c}.\label{eq:kappa}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$\dot{K}=2x^{\top}G\dot{x}=4(K-1)\,x^{\top}G(J_{G}-d_{c}I)Gx$. Since $GJ_{G}=\alpha J$, right-multiplying by $G$ gives $GJ_{G}G=\alpha(JG)$, yielding~\eqref{eq:Kdot}. At a $G$-eigenvector, $x_{*}^{\top}JGx_{*}=\lambda\,x_{*}^{\top}Jx_{*}=0$ by skew-symmetry.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Base-point dependence]
The local effective restoring rate is $\kappa_{K}(x_{*})=4d_{c}\cdot x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}$; it depends on the base point. For the Rindler metric, $x_{*}=(0,1)$ is the unique $G$-eigenvector on $\{K{=}1\}$ with $x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}=1$. The factor~$4=2\times 2$ reflects two sources: the quadratic form~$K$ and the quadratic penalty~$V$; each contributes a factor of~$2$ upon differentiation.
\end{remark}

\subsection*{Two classes of fluctuation on $\{K=1\}$}

Adding isotropic additive white noise (It\^{o} convention) to Law~II gives $dx=(J_{G}-D)\nabla V\,dt+\sigma\,dW_{t}$, where $W_{t}$ is a standard $\mathbb{R}^{2}$-valued Wiener process and $\sigma>0$ is constant. At $D=d_{c}I$ (critical damping, Law~III), two geometrically distinct classes arise:

\medskip\noindent
\textit{Class~A} (thermodynamic): fluctuations of $K$ perpendicular to $\{K=1\}$ --- departures from metric self-consistency --- governed by restoring rate $\kappa_{K}(x_{*})=4d_{c}\,x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}$; the linearised stationary distribution of the $K$-fluctuation at a fixed base point is Gaussian with variance $\sigma^{2}/(2d_{c})$, as follows from the standard stationary distribution of the one-dimensional OU process~\cite{Gardiner1985}. Although both the restoring rate and the $K$-space noise amplitude depend on $x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}$, this factor cancels in the OU variance.

\medskip\noindent
\textit{Class~B} (kinematic; thermodynamically inert here): the tangential component of the fluctuation along $\{K=1\}$ in the boost direction. For the Rindler metric $g=\mathrm{diag}(-(\kappa\ell)^{2},1)$, $\{K=1\}$ is the hyperbola $-(\kappa\ell)^{2}x_{1}^{2}+x_{2}^{2}=1$; motion along it generates Lorentz boosts, changing the inertial observer but not the thermodynamic state. The Hessian of $V$ restricted to $\{K=1\}$ is rank-one with null space containing this tangent, confirming that Class~B carries no restoring force and no heat. The geometric picture of the two classes on the $\{K=1\}$ hyperbola is shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:K1geometry}.

\textbf{Summary of logical status.}
Law~III closes the dynamical sector by selecting $D=d_{c}I$ through a stability criterion with minimal isotropic dissipation. \section{Spacetime thermodynamics (Route~A)}\label{sec:thermo}

This section develops Route~A as the thermodynamic continuation of the unified local dynamics established in the preceding sections. Law~I supplied the Lorentzian cost geometry; \S\ref{sec:infodynamics} extracted from the information-time layer a distinguished irreversible gradient sector; \S\S\ref{sec:symplectic}--\ref{sec:lawII} completed that sector by the unique linear-algebraic reversible contribution, yielding the canonical local evolution law; and Law~III fixed the minimal isotropic dissipation compatible with local stability of $\{K=1\}$. Route~A now uses the thermodynamic fluctuation channel of that local dynamics as a conditional bridge to the Einstein equations. Every external identification used in Route~A is marked explicitly; the full input list is summarised at the end of the section.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Sign convention and object split.} In Route~A we use the spacetime-sign convention
\[
g=\mathrm{diag}(-(\kappa\ell)^{2},1)
\]
for direct comparison with Rindler geometry. Statements depending only on $\det g$, $d_{c}$, or the hyperbolic spectrum are invariant under the overall sign change. Statements invoking Axiom~T as positive temporal cost are to be read in the cost-sign convention
\[
G_{\mathrm{cost}}=-g=\mathrm{diag}((\kappa\ell)^{2},-1),
\]
used explicitly in \S~\ref{sec:singularity}. Both conventions have determinant $-(\kappa\ell)^{2}$ and give the same $d_{c}$.

In this section, $g_{\mu\nu}$ denotes the physical Rindler metric used as the external spacetime background. By contrast, the quadratic form $G$ entering
\[
K=x^{\top}Gx,\qquad J_G=\alpha G^{-1}J,
\]
and the OU reduction is the cost-form used by the framework. In the Rindler realisation, these are related by the sign convention discussed in \S~\ref{sec:lawI}, but they should not be conflated notationally: $g$ denotes the physical spacetime metric, whereas $G$ denotes the dynamical cost-form. In all OU and thermodynamic formulas below, $G$ denotes the dynamical cost-form, whereas $g$ denotes the external spacetime metric.

As a concrete realisation, we apply the framework to the Rindler metric
\[
g=\mathrm{diag}(-(\kappa\ell)^{2},1)
\]
\textup{[external: $g$ is the standard Rindler metric from GR, not derived from Law~I]},
where $\kappa$ is the surface gravity and $\ell$ the proper distance
from the horizon. The Rindler expressions are understood for a non-extremal local horizon, $\kappa>0$, and for $\ell>0$. Equation~\eqref{eq:dc} gives $d_{c}=\alpha/(\kappa\ell)$ (i.e., $\alpha/\sqrt{|g_{00}|}$ with $g_{00}=-(\kappa\ell)^{2}$ the temporal component of the Rindler metric). The expression for $d_{c}$ uses the diagonal/isotropic threshold described in \S\ref{sec:stability}; for general $H_{*}$, the threshold may shift. With $T_{H}=\hbar\kappa/(2\pi)$~\cite{Hawking},
\begin{equation}
d_{c}\cdot T_{H} = \frac{\alpha}{\kappa\ell}\cdot\frac{\hbar\kappa}{2\pi} = \frac{\alpha\hbar}{2\pi\ell},\label{eq:nearhorizon}
\end{equation}
exhibiting exact $\kappa$-cancellation. Hence the internal algebraic identity $d_{c}\cdot T_{H}=\alpha\hbar/(2\pi\ell)$ matches the Tolman temperature~\cite{Tolman} $T_{\mathrm{tol}}=\alpha\hbar/(2\pi\ell)$, depending only on the proper distance $\ell$ from the horizon, not on the surface gravity $\kappa$. This is an internal algebraic identity; it does not independently derive the Tolman temperature, but establishes a bridge between the framework's scale $d_{c}$ and the known thermal scale of the horizon.

\subsection*{Clausius relation (from OU dynamics) within Class~A}

In this subsection we work within the Sekimoto--Seifert stochastic thermodynamics framework~\cite{Sekimoto}; within that external framework, the key additional external input is the identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$, which is assumed here and not derived from the present framework.

Within Class~A (fixed inertial observer), and at a fixed $G$-eigenvector base point on $\{K=1\}$, the normal $K$-dynamics near $K=1$ reduces to a one-dimensional Ornstein--Uhlenbeck process. Its stationary variance is
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Var}(K)\approx\frac{\sigma^{2}}{2d_{c}},\label{eq:var}
\end{equation}
where the base-point factor cancels between the effective restoring rate and the induced $K$-space noise amplitude.
With this stationary variance, the FDT form $\sigma_{K}^{2}=2\kappa_{K}T_{\mathrm{eff}}$ defines an effective temperature
\[
T_{\mathrm{eff}}=\frac{\sigma^{2}}{2d_{c}},
\]
consistent with Law~II and Theorem~\ref{thm:restoring}. This is, at this stage, a formal effective-temperature definition: its identification with a physical horizon temperature requires the external input $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ introduced above. The Fokker--Planck equation then yields a Boltzmann stationary distribution $\rho\propto\exp(-V/T_{\mathrm{eff}})$; assuming the external identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$, the Clausius relation $\delta Q=T_{\mathrm{tol}}\,dS$ follows from local detailed balance for the OU process, with
\[
\delta Q := dV + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma_{K}^{2}\,dt,\qquad \sigma_{K}=2\sigma\|Gx_{*}\|.
\]
(The heat definition follows the Sekimoto--Seifert stochastic thermodynamics convention~\cite{Sekimoto}.) A derivation of the identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ from a more fundamental principle (e.g.\ a KMS condition~\cite{Haag,Connes1994}) remains an open problem.

\medskip\noindent
The following statement is conditional on the external identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$; it is not an autonomous derivation of the Clausius relation, but the equivalence theorem establishing what $\sigma^{2}$ must be when that identification is given.

\begin{proposition}[Local Clausius Equivalence]\label{prop:clausius}
Assume (i) the OU approximation near $K=1$, (ii) the Sekimoto--Seifert heat convention, (iii) local detailed balance for the OU process, and (iv) the \emph{external} identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$, together with the Tolman identity $T_{\mathrm{tol}}=d_{c}T_{H}$ (precise forms in the Remark below). Then the Clausius relation $\delta Q=T_{\mathrm{tol}}\,dS$ is equivalent to
\begin{equation}
\sigma^{2} = 2\,d_{c}\cdot T_{\mathrm{tol}} = 2\,d_{c}^{2}\cdot T_{H}.\label{eq:noise}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
$(\Rightarrow)$: Setting $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}=d_{c}T_{H}$ gives $\sigma^{2}/(2d_{c})=d_{c}\cdot T_{H}$, hence $\sigma^{2}=2d_{c}^{2}\cdot T_{H}$.
$(\Leftarrow)$: $\sigma^{2}=2d_{c}\cdot T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ gives $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=\sigma^{2}/(2d_{c})=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$, hence Clausius holds.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Assumptions of Proposition~\ref{prop:clausius}]
The assumptions entering Proposition~\ref{prop:clausius} are:
(i)~the OU approximation near $K=1$ ($|K-1|\ll 1$, equivalently $T_{\mathrm{eff}}\ll 1$);
(ii)~the Sekimoto--Seifert heat convention $\delta Q := dV + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma_{K}^{2}\,dt$;
(iii)~local detailed balance for the OU process;
(iv)~the external identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$;
together with the algebraic Tolman identity $T_{\mathrm{tol}}=d_{c}T_{H}$ ($\kappa$-factors cancel in eq.~\eqref{eq:nearhorizon}).
\end{remark}

\subsection*{Position-dependent temperature and It\^{o} corrections}

Applying It\^{o}'s formula to $K=x^{\top}Gx$ gives an additional drift $\sigma^{2}\,\mathrm{tr}(G)\,dt$. This shifts the mean of $K$ but not its variance: at any $G$-eigenvector base point, the FDT ratio $\sigma_{K}^{2}/(2\kappa_{K})$ reduces to $\sigma^{2}/(2d_{c})$ regardless of $x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}$. Hence~\eqref{eq:var} and $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=\sigma^{2}/(2d_{c})$ hold for any Lorentzian $G$.

The $K$-space noise amplitude $\sigma_{K}(x)=2\sigma\|Gx\|$ varies along $\{K=1\}$, yielding a position-dependent temperature (eq.~\eqref{eq:Tloc}):
\begin{equation}
T_{\mathrm{loc}}(x) := \frac{\sigma_{K}(x)^{2}}{2\kappa_{K}(x_{*})} = \frac{\sigma^{2}\,x^{\top}G^{2}x}{2d_{c}\,x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}}.\label{eq:Tloc}
\end{equation}
This position dependence should not be read as a Class~B heat fluctuation: variation along the boost orbit changes the observer/position assignment, while Class~B remains thermodynamically inert and carries no heat.

\begin{remark}[Stability constraint]
For macroscopic black holes, $T_{\mathrm{tol}}\ll d_{c}$; setting $D=T_{\mathrm{tol}}I$ renders $K=1$ unstable. Stability requires $D\ge d_{c}I$; critical damping $D=d_{c}I$ is the unique marginally stable choice.
\end{remark}

Since every local Rindler horizon admits the standard Rindler approximation~\cite{Jacobson} with $g=\mathrm{diag}(-(\kappa\ell)^{2},1)$ (where $\kappa$ and $\ell$ vary by location), Proposition~\ref{prop:clausius} applies to each such horizon, yielding the Clausius relation locally. Together with Jacobson's argument~\cite{Jacobson}, this supplies the local Clausius input needed for the Einstein equations. Once the external identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ has been given, the remaining external input required to invoke Jacobson's argument is $S\propto A$.

\medskip
\noindent
\textbf{Summary of logical status.}
Route~A is a thermodynamic bridge rather than an autonomous derivation of the Einstein equations. Its internal inputs are the Lorentzian structure supplied by Law~I, the isotropic stability scale $d_{c}$, the irreversible principal sector suggested by the information-time layer, and the OU dynamics of Class~A near $K=1$. To connect these ingredients to Jacobson's argument, two external inputs are required: (i) the identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$, and (ii) the entropy--area law $S\propto A$. With these added, the framework supplies a local Clausius relation on each local Rindler horizon, thereby providing the local Clausius input used in Jacobson's route to $R_{\mu\nu}-\tfrac12 R\,g_{\mu\nu}=8\pi T_{\mu\nu}$.

\section{Eigenvalue self-consistency (Route~B: direct algebraic)}\label{sec:field}

The eigenvalue self-consistency condition $\sigma_2^2\,\Box\ln\sigma_i=1$ is a conjectured field-level uplift of $K{=}1$, introduced as a conjecture rather than derived from the framework's axioms; Theorem~\ref{thm:field} establishes a strict algebraic equivalence to $R_{\mu\nu}=0$ in the spherical vacuum sector, with no thermodynamic input.

All statements below are local on intervals where $f>0$, so that $\sigma_1=r\sqrt{f}$ and $\ln\sigma_1$ are real. Consider the static spherically symmetric metric
\begin{equation}\label{eq:metric}
  ds^2=-f(r)\,dt^2+f(r)^{-1}dr^2+r^2\,d\Omega^2,
\end{equation}
with symplectic eigenvalues $\sigma_1:=r\sqrt{f}$, $\sigma_2:=r$
(on each local Rindler wedge, $d_{c}\sigma_1=\alpha$, connecting $\sigma_1$ to the $2\times 2$ symplectic eigenvalue of~\S\ref{sec:symplectic}),
and d'Alembertian
$\Box\phi=r^{-2}\partial_r(r^2f\,\partial_r\phi)$ (the radial d'Alembertian, exact for $r$-dependent $\phi$; primes denote $d/dr$).

\begin{definition}[Eigenvalue self-consistency functionals]\label{def:esc}
\begin{align}
  K_1 &:= \sigma_2^2\,\Box\ln\sigma_1,\label{eq:K1def}\\
  K_2 &:= \sigma_2^2\,\Box\ln\sigma_2.\label{eq:K2def}
\end{align}
\end{definition}

\noindent
The eigenvalue self-consistency condition $\sigma_2^2\,\Box\ln\sigma=1$, imposed on each eigenvalue ($K_1=1$, eq.~\eqref{eq:K1def}, and $K_2=1$, eq.~\eqref{eq:K2def}), states that the d'Alembertian of each log-eigenvalue equals the angular curvature cost $1/\sigma_2^2$. This is a conjectured field-level uplift of the point-level balance $K{=}1$: each eigenvalue must propagate at the rate set by the angular sector, in the same way that $K=1$ requires the quadratic cost form $K$ to reproduce its own normalisation.

Expanding (direct computation using $\Box\ln\sigma_1=\Box\ln r+\tfrac{1}{2}\Box\ln f$):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:K1exp}
  K_1 = f+2rf'+\tfrac{r^2}{2}f'',
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:K2exp}
  K_2 = rf'+f = \frac{d(rf)}{dr}.
\end{equation}
(For $K_2$: $\Box\ln r = (1/r^2)d/dr[r^2f/r]=(rf'+f)/r^2$,
so $r^2\Box\ln r = rf'+f$.)

\begin{lemma}[Scalar Curvature Formula]\label{lem:R}
$R=-2\,\Box\ln\sigma_1+2/r^2$ for~\eqref{eq:metric}.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
$R=-f''-4f'/r+2(1{-}f)/r^2$.
$\Box\ln\sigma_1=f/r^2+2f'/r+f''/2$ (from $K_1=r^2\Box\ln\sigma_1$ and eq.~\eqref{eq:K1exp}).
Then $-2\Box\ln\sigma_1+2/r^2=R$.\qed
\end{proof}

\noindent
An immediate computation gives $R_{tt}=\tfrac{f}{r^2}(K_1-K_2)$, so $K_1=K_2$ implies $R_{tt}=0$. Substituting $K_1=r^2\Box\ln\sigma_1$ into Lemma~\ref{lem:R} gives $R=2(1-K_1)/r^2$; hence $K_1{=}1\Leftrightarrow R{=}0$ and
$K_2{=}1\Leftrightarrow R_{\theta\theta}{=}0$
(since $R_{\theta\theta}=1{-}f{-}rf'=1{-}K_2$).
Equivalently, $K_2{=}1$ states that the Misner--Sharp mass
$m=\frac{r}{2}(1{-}f)$ is constant: $dm/dr=(1{-}K_2)/2=0$.

\medskip\noindent
The following is an equivalence theorem in the spherical vacuum sector, not an autonomous derivation: it asserts that the conjectured eigenvalue condition, introduced as a field-level assumption rather than derived from the framework's axioms, is algebraically equivalent to $R_{\mu\nu}=0$.

\begin{theorem}[Field-Level Equivalence]\label{thm:field}
For~\eqref{eq:metric} with $f\in C^{2}(I)$ and $f>0$ on an interval $I\subset(0,\infty)$ (so that $\sigma_{1}=r\sqrt{f}$ and $\ln\sigma_{1}$ are real), and $K_1,K_2$ as in Definition~\ref{def:esc}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:equiv}
  \sigma_2^2\,\Box\ln\sigma_i=1\;\;\text{for }i=1,2
  \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad R_{\mu\nu}=0,
\end{equation}
where $R_{\mu\nu}=0$ are the vacuum Einstein field equations~\cite{Einstein1915,Hilbert1915}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$(\Rightarrow)$\;
$K_2{=}1$ gives $R_{\theta\theta}{=}0$.
$K_1{-}K_2=rf'+\frac{r^2}{2}f''=0$ (from eqs.~\eqref{eq:K1exp}--\eqref{eq:K2exp}) gives $f''=-2f'/r$; hence $R_{tt}{=}0$ (by the observation $R_{tt}=\tfrac{f}{r^2}(K_1-K_2)$ above). (For this metric $R_{tt}=-f^{2}R_{rr}$~\cite{Wald1984}, so $R_{tt}=0\Leftrightarrow R_{rr}=0$.)

$(\Leftarrow)$\;
$R_{\theta\theta}{=}0$ gives $K_2{=}1$.
$R_{tt}{=}0$ gives $f''=-2f'/r$; substituting:
$K_1=f+2rf'-rf'=f+rf'=K_2=1$.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
The condition $K_1{=}1$ is the second-order linear ODE
$f+2rf'+\frac{r^2}{2}f''=1$, equivalent to $R{=}0$, with general solution
$f=1-C_1/r-C_2/r^2$; $K_2{=}1$ forces $C_2{=}0$,
recovering the Schwarzschild solution uniquely, in agreement with Birkhoff's theorem.
In the weak field ($f=1+\varepsilon\varphi$),
$K_2{=}1$ gives $\varphi=C/r$ (Newton) in one step;
$K_1{=}1$ is automatically satisfied at linear order,
so its independent content is purely nonlinear (strong-field).
\end{corollary}

\begin{lemma}[Asymmetric rigidity]\label{lem:rigidity}
For metric~\eqref{eq:metric} with $f\in C^{2}(I)$ on an interval $I\subset(0,\infty)$:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[\textup{(a)}] $K_{2}\equiv 1$ on $I$ if and only if $f=1+C/r$ for some constant $C$, in which case $K_{1}\equiv 1$ on $I$ as well.
\item[\textup{(b)}] $K_{1}\equiv 1$ on $I$ if and only if $f=1+A/r+B/r^{2}$ for some constants $A,B$. Substituting into the definition of $K_{2}$ gives
\[
K_{2}=1-B/r^{2},
\]
so the converse of (a) fails when $B\neq 0$: $K_{1}\equiv 1$ does not imply $K_{2}\equiv 1$.
\end{enumerate}
Thus $K_{2}\equiv 1$ is the strictly stronger condition; the two-parameter family $f=1+A/r+B/r^{2}$ satisfies $K_{1}\equiv 1$ but generically violates $K_{2}\equiv 1$. Reissner--Nordstr\"{o}m is one such non-vacuum example.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
$(a)$\;$K_{2}=f+rf'=d(rf)/dr$, so $K_{2}\equiv 1$ is the first-order ODE $d(rf)/dr=1$ with general solution $rf=r+C$, i.e.\ $f=1+C/r$. Direct substitution then gives $K_{1}=(1+C/r)+2r(-C/r^{2})+(r^{2}/2)(2C/r^{3})=1+C/r-2C/r+C/r=1$.\;
$(b)$\;$K_{1}\equiv 1$ is the second-order linear ODE $f+2rf'+(r^{2}/2)f''=1$, which is the Euler-type equation having Schwarzschild--Reissner-Nordstr\"{o}m form general solution $f=1+A/r+B/r^{2}$. Substituting into $K_{2}=f+rf'$ gives $K_{2}=(1+A/r+B/r^{2})+r(-A/r^{2}-2B/r^{3})=1-B/r^{2}$. Hence $K_{2}\equiv 1$ requires $B=0$.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Asymmetric roles of $K_1$ and $K_2$]
Lemma~\ref{lem:rigidity} formalises the asymmetry: $K_{2}\equiv 1$ is rigid (forces $K_{1}\equiv 1$ via $f=1+C/r$), whereas $K_{1}\equiv 1$ is flexible (admits a one-parameter family of $K_{2}$-violating solutions). This asymmetry underlies the testability discussion of Open Problem~7.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Observational implications]
Within the present spherical-vacuum analysis, any observational distinction between the eigenvalue principle and standard GR must therefore arise from strong-gravity data.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[$\Lambda$-extension of Theorem~\ref{thm:field}]\label{cor:SdS}
For metric~\eqref{eq:metric} with $f\in C^{2}(I)$ and $f>0$ on $I\subset(0,\infty)$, and $K_{1},K_{2}$ as in Definition~\ref{def:esc}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Lambda-equiv}
K_{1}=1-2\Lambda r^{2}\;\;\text{and}\;\;K_{2}=1-\Lambda r^{2}
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad R_{\mu\nu}=\Lambda g_{\mu\nu}.
\end{equation}
The general solution is the Schwarzschild--de~Sitter family $f=1-2M/r-\Lambda r^{2}/3$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
$(\Rightarrow)$\;
$K_{2}=1-\Lambda r^{2}$ rewrites as $f+rf'=1-\Lambda r^{2}$, i.e.\ $d(rf)/dr=1-\Lambda r^{2}$, with general solution $rf=r-\Lambda r^{3}/3+\text{const}$, so $f=1-2M/r-\Lambda r^{2}/3$ for some constant $M$. Direct substitution then gives $K_{1}=1-2\Lambda r^{2}$ automatically. For this $f$, $R_{\theta\theta}=1-K_{2}=\Lambda r^{2}=\Lambda g_{\theta\theta}$ and $R_{tt}=(f/r^{2})(K_{1}-K_{2})=-\Lambda f=\Lambda g_{tt}$. For this metric $R_{tt}=-f^{2}R_{rr}$~\cite{Wald1984}, so $R_{tt}=\Lambda g_{tt}$ is equivalent to $R_{rr}=\Lambda g_{rr}$; the angular components are fixed by $R_{\theta\theta}=\Lambda g_{\theta\theta}$ and spherical symmetry ($R_{\phi\phi}=\sin^{2}\!\theta\,R_{\theta\theta}=\Lambda g_{\phi\phi}$), closing all four nontrivial Ricci components.\;
$(\Leftarrow)$\;
$R_{\theta\theta}=\Lambda g_{\theta\theta}=\Lambda r^{2}$ gives $K_{2}=1-\Lambda r^{2}$. $R_{tt}=\Lambda g_{tt}=-\Lambda f$ combined with $R_{tt}=(f/r^{2})(K_{1}-K_{2})$ gives $K_{1}-K_{2}=-\Lambda r^{2}$, hence $K_{1}=1-2\Lambda r^{2}$.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Matter and the role of $\Lambda$]
From the $tt$ component of the Einstein equations,
\[
\rho = \frac{1-K_2}{8\pi r^2} = \frac{1-\sigma_2^2\,\Box\ln\sigma_2}{8\pi\sigma_2^2},
\]
matter may be read as the angular eigenvalue's failure to self-consistently propagate, measured per unit area.
This relation \emph{uses} the Einstein equations;
it is a cost rewriting, not an independent derivation
(in particular, $8\pi$ is inherited from GR).
The value of $\Lambda$ is not predicted by the present framework.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[General metrics and polarization]
The extension to general metrics is not yet complete: the step connecting the eigenvalue condition to $R_{\mu\nu}v^\mu v^\nu=0$ on each null plane requires the Gauss--Codazzi relations for null hypersurfaces, and remains open.
A possible generalisation would apply an analogue of $\sigma_2^2\Box\ln\sigma_i=1$ on each null direction.
The \emph{polarization identity} guarantees that if
$R_{\mu\nu}v^\mu v^\nu=0$ for every null~$v^\mu$ and $R=0$,
then $R_{\mu\nu}=0$.
(Proof: in an orthonormal frame, taking $v=e_0\pm e_k$ gives
$R_{0k}=0$, $R_{00}+R_{kk}=0$; mixed spatial components vanish
by comparing $v=(e_0\pm\frac{e_i+e_j}{\sqrt{2}})$ and $v=(e_0\pm\frac{e_i-e_j}{\sqrt{2}})$, giving $R_{ij}=0$ for $i\neq j$; the trace $R=0$ then
forces $R_{00}=0$.)
\end{remark}

\section{Domain of validity and self-consistency resolution scale}\label{sec:singularity}

The following results concern the \emph{domain} of the framework rather than the physical existence of singularities: where $\sigma_1=0$, the $K{=}1$ locus is empty and the framework is undefined, not incorrect.

\begin{proposition}[Positivity from Axiom~T]\label{prop:sigma1}
Among the three axioms, only Axiom~T is used here; Axioms~R and~E are needed only in \S~\ref{sec:lawI} to establish $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$. In the $2\times 2$ wedge representation used here, Axiom~T implies $\sigma_1>0$ wherever $K{=}1$ holds.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For the $(t,r)$ wedge (and by the same argument for any $2\times 2$ Lorentzian $G$), $G=\mathrm{diag}(\sigma_1^2,-1)$: Axiom~T gives $G_{00}=\sigma_1^2>0$; contrast: $g_{00}=-f<0$ for the spacetime metric; the sign convention here differs from \S\ref{sec:thermo} but gives the same $d_{c}$, since $\det G=-\sigma_1^2$ in both cases (only $|\det G|$ and the positive-cost temporal direction enter the argument). If $\sigma_1=0$ then $G_{00}=0$, violating Axiom~T; with $\sigma_1$ taken as the positive scale factor (e.g., $\sigma_1=r\sqrt{f}$ on the real branch in Route~B), this yields $\sigma_1>0$.\qed
\end{proof}

\noindent
A direct consequence: when $\sigma_1=0$, $K=-x_2^2\le 0$ and $K{=}1$ has no solutions. The Schwarzschild interior, including the singular limit $r\to 0$, therefore lies outside the domain of the framework: $\sigma_1=r\sqrt{f}$ is defined on the real branch $f>0$, so the interior $r<2M$ (where $f<0$) and the limit $r\to 0$ fall outside this domain --- the framework is undefined there, not incorrect.

\medskip\noindent
The following is a soft consistency bound from the OU validity regime, not a hard quantum-gravity cutoff.

\begin{proposition}[Self-consistency resolution scale]\label{prop:domain}
In Planck units ($\hbar=\ell_P=1$), under the Tolman-temperature identification, the OU approximation, and Schwarzschild near-horizon normalisation, thermal self-consistency of the $K{=}1$ framework requires $\sigma_1>\sigma_{1,c}:=\hbar/(4\pi)=\ell_P/(4\pi)$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
\textup{[External identification: $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$,
as in \S~\ref{sec:thermo}; all other steps are internal.]}
Near the horizon, $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}=\hbar\kappa/(2\pi\sqrt{f})$
(Tolman redshift factor; cf.~\eqref{eq:nearhorizon} with $\alpha=1$). With $\sigma_1=r\sqrt{f}$ and $r\approx 2M$, $\kappa=1/(4M)$ near the Schwarzschild horizon:
\begin{equation*}
T_{\mathrm{eff}}\approx\frac{\hbar\cdot(1/(4M))}{2\pi\cdot\sigma_1/(2M)}=\frac{\hbar}{4\pi\sigma_1}.
\end{equation*}
Equivalently, since $\ell\approx 2\sigma_1$ near the Schwarzschild horizon, this agrees with $T_{\mathrm{tol}}=\hbar/(2\pi\ell)$ from~\eqref{eq:nearhorizon} (with $\alpha=1$).
$\mathrm{Var}(K)\approx T_{\mathrm{eff}}$ (from eq.~\eqref{eq:var} and $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$, in the OU approximation); for $K{=}1$ to be a
well-defined equilibrium, $\mathrm{Var}(K)<1$, hence
$\sigma_1>\hbar/(4\pi)$. This is a self-consistent bound: the OU approximation must be valid, i.e., $\mathrm{Var}(K)<1$.\qed
\end{proof}

\noindent
Below $\sigma_{1,c}$, thermal fluctuations of $K$ become of order one, and the classical description of the $K{=}1$ locus dissolves into noise; this bound does not, by itself, imply a fundamental spacetime discreteness.

\medskip\noindent
\textit{Scope of the bound.}
The derivation of $\sigma_{1,c}$ makes explicit use of the external identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$, which belongs to Route~A (\S\ref{sec:thermo}). Within Route~B, where no such thermal identification is invoked, whether an analogous self-consistency scale exists remains an open question. Turning the soft consistency bound into a hard uncertainty bound \emph{independent} of the Route~A identification is the subject of Open Problem~6 in \S\ref{sec:discussion}.

\medskip\noindent
\textit{Terminology.}
The adjective ``soft'' emphasises that the bound follows from the validity regime of the OU approximation ($\mathrm{Var}(K)<1$), not from an independent quantisation principle.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection*{Two routes: one thermodynamic continuation, one equivalence theorem}

The framework developed in this paper now has a unified local structure. Law~I derives the Lorentzian type of the leading cost form from the realizability bridge; \S\ref{sec:infodynamics} extracts from the information-time layer a distinguished irreversible gradient sector; \S\S\ref{sec:symplectic}--\ref{sec:lawII} identify the unique linear-algebraic reversible completion and combine the two sectors into the canonical local evolution law; and Law~III selects the minimal isotropic dissipation compatible with local stability of $\{K=1\}$.

Within this upgraded reading, Route~A is the thermodynamic continuation of that local unified dynamics. Once the required external identifications are supplied, the Einstein equations are conditionally recovered via stochastic dynamics and the Jacobson argument (\S\ref{sec:thermo}). Route~B, by contrast, remains a strict algebraic equivalence theorem: it connects the conjectured field-level eigenvalue condition to $R_{\mu\nu}=0$ in the spherical vacuum sector, with no thermodynamic input.

The input structure of both routes --- including the Jacobson inputs and the external identifications $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ and $S\propto A$ --- is summarised in Fig.~\ref{fig:routes} and detailed in \S\ref{sec:thermo}. The novelty here is therefore twofold: first, Lorentzian signature is supplied internally by the cost-realizability bridge of Law~I rather than assumed as a kinematic primitive; second, the local dissipative sector is traced back to the information-time layer, while the reversible sector is fixed as the unique linear-algebraic completion in the $2\times2$ Lorentzian setting.

The two routes meet in the vacuum sector: Route~B establishes $R_{\mu\nu}=0$ directly, while Route~A recovers the same conclusion through
\[
R_{\mu\nu}-\tfrac12 R\,g_{\mu\nu}=8\pi T_{\mu\nu}
\]
with $T_{\mu\nu}=0$.

\subsection*{Scope of claims}

The present paper does not claim that the full symplectic--dissipative dynamics is derived from information time alone. Its claim is narrower, but stronger than in the earlier structural reading: the information-time layer determines a distinguished irreversible gradient sector, while the reversible sector is fixed as the unique linear-algebraic completion associated with the Lorentzian cost form $G$. Law~II then canonically synthesises these two sectors into the local evolution law
\[
\dot x=(J_G-D)\nabla V.
\]

Within Route~A, given the external identifications $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ and $S\propto A$, the Einstein equations
\[
R_{\mu\nu}-\tfrac12 R\,g_{\mu\nu}=8\pi T_{\mu\nu}
\]
are conditionally recovered through the Jacobson argument. Within Route~B, the paper establishes a strict algebraic equivalence between the conjectured eigenvalue condition and $R_{\mu\nu}=0$ in the spherical vacuum sector, with no thermodynamic input. Thus, in the spherical vacuum sector, the vacuum equations admit an equivalent cost-structural formulation (Theorem~\ref{thm:field}).

The contribution of the present paper is therefore conceptual, structural, and partially generative. Lorentzian signature is supplied internally by the cost-realizability bridge of Law~I; the information-time layer already fixes the irreversible principal direction of the local dynamics; and the reversible sector is no longer merely appended heuristically, but identified as the unique linear-algebraic completion in the local $2\times2$ Lorentzian setting. What remains open is a full first-principles derivation that would identify the entire symplectic--dissipative law, including its reversible sector, directly from information time without additional completion principles.

Accordingly, the matter density $\rho=(1-K_2)/(8\pi r^2)$ and the interpretation of matter remain GR rewritings; $\Lambda$ is not predicted by the framework; $S\propto A$ remains an external input borrowed from black-hole thermodynamics; and Route~B in its current form remains an equivalence theorem conditional on a conjectured field-level uplift. A first-principles justification of why each symplectic eigenvalue satisfies the same d'Alembertian equation also remains open (cf.\ Open Problem~4 below).

\subsection*{Extension to $(1{+}3)$ dimensions}

The following remarks are heuristic and should be read as an outlook rather than a partial extension theorem. In $(1{+}3)$ dimensions, $\{K{=}1\}\cong H^3$ carries $S^2$ cross-sections with area $4\pi\sigma_1^2$ when $\alpha=1$ and $\sigma_1=\kappa\ell$, linking the symplectic invariant to the Bekenstein--Hawking area~\cite{Bekenstein}; the $1/4$ coefficient still requires the usual Hawking/first-law input. Two structural changes remain: Lorentzian signature alone is no longer sufficient to guarantee $d_c>0$, and $J_G$ requires a symplectic pairing choice. Whether the cost-realizability mechanism that forces $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$ in two dimensions admits a natural extension that constrains the full $(1{+}3)$-dimensional metric signature without assuming it, is an open conceptual question not addressed in this paper.

\subsection*{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
\item Route~B is currently confined to spherical symmetry. \textit{(scope)}
\item Route~A still depends on the external inputs $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ and $S\propto A$. In particular, the identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ is a phenomenological assumption and is not explained by the internal structure of Laws~I--III. \textit{(external input)}
\item Although the information-time layer now fixes the irreversible principal sector of the local dynamics, the full symplectic--dissipative law is not yet derived from information time alone: the reversible sector still enters through a linear-algebraic completion principle. \textit{(incomplete dynamical closure)}
\item No observational deviation from GR is obtained in the present analysis. \textit{(predictive gap)}
\item The $\sigma_1$ threshold derived in \S\ref{sec:singularity} is a soft consistency bound only, not a hard quantum-gravitational cutoff. \textit{(strength)}
\item The scalar $H$ (information-time layer) and the matrix weight $H_{*}$ (stability layer) are not unified in the present framework. \textit{(notation and layering)}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Open problems}

The first three problems concern completion of the unified local dynamics; the next two concern extension beyond the present local and spherical settings; the remaining problems concern quantum consistency and empirical distinctness.

\begin{enumerate}
\item Derive the reversible sector from a deeper first principle. In the present paper, the information-time layer determines the irreversible principal sector, while the reversible term is introduced through a linear-algebraic completion principle. The main open problem is to replace that completion principle by a genuine derivation from the same underlying information-time or cost structure. In particular, determine whether the reversible contribution $J_G\nabla V$ can be obtained as a necessary consequence of preserving the local cost geometry under information-time evolution, rather than as an additional structural input.

\item Establish a first-principles relation between the motivating information time $dt_{\mathrm{info}}$ and the effective dynamical evolution parameter used in Laws~II--III and Route~A. In the present paper, the information-time layer already determines the irreversible principal direction, but the later effective evolution parameter remains independent. A full dynamical closure would require identifying whether the later parameter coincides with $dt_{\mathrm{info}}$, with a reparametrization thereof, or with a distinct emergent time variable.

\item Justify the passage from the structural functional $\Phi$ to the later self-consistency potential
\[
V=\tfrac12(K-1)^2.
\]
At present, the information-time layer acts through $\Phi$, while the later dynamics is organised by the potential $V$. Clarify whether $V$ can be derived from $\Phi$ by a local reduction, variational principle, or self-consistency requirement, rather than introduced as an additional dynamical choice.

\item Complete Route~B for general metrics. The present field-level equivalence theorem is confined to static spherical symmetry. Extending the eigenvalue self-consistency condition to general metrics requires identifying the appropriate null or local $2$-plane version of the condition and relating it to $R_{\mu\nu}v^\mu v^\nu$ through the Gauss--Codazzi relations for null hypersurfaces.

\item Extend Route~B to non-vacuum matter. In particular, determine whether
\[
\rho=\frac{1-K_2}{8\pi r^2}
\]
is an intrinsic consequence of the cost framework rather than a GR rewriting, whether matter can thereby be understood as the failure of angular eigenvalue self-consistency per unit area, and whether this perspective extends to the full stress-energy tensor $T_{\mu\nu}$.

\item Prove
\[
\sigma_{1,\min}\ge \frac{\hbar}{4\pi}
\]
as a hard bound from $K$--$\varphi$ canonical conjugacy \emph{without} invoking the Route~A identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$, where $\varphi$ is the boost angle along $\{K=1\}$. This would upgrade the present soft consistency bound to an intrinsic quantum constraint.

\item Identify testable strong-field predictions. Within the present spherical-vacuum analysis, since in the weak-field regime $K_2=1$ already reproduces the Newtonian potential while $K_1=1$ is automatic at linear order, any observational distinction between the eigenvalue principle and standard GR must arise from the nonlinear strong-field sector, i.e.\ from the term $B/r^2$ in the $K_1$-only family that distinguishes $K_1$-equivalence from pure Schwarzschild (Lemma~\ref{lem:rigidity}). Possible windows include high-precision black-hole shadow data, strong-field gravitational-wave signals, and near-horizon quantum effects.

\item Prove global attractivity of $K=1$: show that for any $x_0\in\mathbb{R}^{2}$, the trajectory of
\[
\dot{x}=(J_G-d_{c}I)\nabla V,
\qquad
V=\tfrac{1}{2}(K-1)^2,
\]
satisfies $K(x(t))\to 1$ as $t\to\infty$.
\end{enumerate}
