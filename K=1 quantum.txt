\begin{abstract}
Within the $K{=}1$ chronogeometrodynamic framework~\cite{K1paper},
the wave function $\psi$ exists if and only if $\det G < 0$
(Lorentzian signature).
This follows from the Ornstein--Uhlenbeck (OU) dynamics on
$\{K{=}1\}$: the Fokker--Planck equation admits a similarity
transform~\cite{Nelson,Risken} with Schr\"odinger structure only
when the critical damping is real.
The condition is dynamical.

Treating $\Delta = \det G$ as a stochastic variable with
noise $D \propto \sqrt{|\Delta|}$,
we demonstrate a \emph{one-way barrier} at $\Delta = 0$:
the vanishing of $D$ at the boundary blocks
Lorentzian~$\to$~Euclidean transitions, while
Euclidean~$\to$~Lorentzian is spontaneous.
The irreversibility is analytic: $D(\Delta)$ is discontinuous at
$\Delta = 0$, breaking detailed balance.

In the multi-mode extension, cross-mode noise renders the barrier
finite but astronomically high; the measurement apparatus acts as a
catalyst that exponentially accelerates collapse.
After collapse, the restarted OU process decays to
$\rho_{\mathrm{eq}} = |\psi_0|^2$:
the ground-state probability emerges as Boltzmann equilibrium.
\end{abstract}

%=====================================================================
\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
%=====================================================================

The quantum measurement problem has attracted at least five prominent approaches, which differ in where they intervene. Copenhagen adds a projection postulate~\cite{vonNeumann}; decoherence traces over environmental degrees of freedom~\cite{Zurek}; many-worlds reinterprets the universal wave function~\cite{Everett}. These three retain the Schr\"odinger equation and address measurement at the level of interpretation or solution. GRW~\cite{GRW} and Penrose~\cite{Penrose} go further: both argue that unitary evolution is \emph{not} unconditionally valid --- GRW by adding a stochastic term, Penrose by arguing that gravitational self-energy renders macroscopic superpositions intrinsically unstable.

This paper identifies a different entry point --- a geometric precondition for the equation's existence --- and shows that the precondition is dynamical:

\begin{quote}
\itshape
Within the $K{=}1$ framework, the wave function $\psi$ exists if and only if the metric signature is Lorentzian.
Collapse is the temporary removal of this precondition.
\end{quote}

The argument has four steps:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{From geometry to quantum mechanics.} The $K{=}1$ OU process~\cite{K1paper} generates a Fokker--Planck equation. Similarity transform~\cite{Nelson,Risken} plus Wick rotation yields an equation with Schr\"odinger structure --- but only if $d_c > 0$, i.e., $\det G < 0$ (Lorentzian)~\cite[Theorem~4]{K1paper}.
\item \textbf{Signature barrier.} The noise coefficient $D(\Delta) \propto \sqrt{|\Delta|}$ vanishes at the signature boundary $\Delta = 0$, creating an entropic barrier that protects Lorentzian signature.
\item \textbf{One-way crossing.} The barrier is one-way: Lor~$\to$~Euc is blocked; Euc~$\to$~Lor is spontaneous. This follows analytically from the discontinuity of $D(\Delta)$ at $\Delta = 0$.
\item \textbf{Post-collapse equilibrium.} After collapse (Euclidean excursion), the OU process restarts and decays to $\rho_{\mathrm{eq}} = |\psi_0|^2$: the ground-state Born rule emerges as Boltzmann equilibrium.
\end{enumerate}

The approach operates one level below the Schr\"odinger equation --- at its geometric precondition --- and thereby differs from all preceding approaches.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Scope.} All results are derived in the $2\times 2$ setting natural to a single Rindler horizon~\cite{K1paper}. The $(1{+}3)$-dimensional extension is discussed in \S\ref{sec:discussion} but not required for the core results; each 2D normal plane closes independently because $\dim(\mathrm{Skew}_2) = 1$ guarantees unique symplectic structure~\cite[Theorem~1]{K1paper}.

\section{Review of the $K{=}1$ framework}\label{sec:review}
%=====================================================================

The $K{=}1$ framework treats spacetime geometry as the outcome of a stochastic optimisation: the system drives the quadratic form $K(x) = x^\top G x$ toward the target value $K = 1$, with dissipation and noise competing around the target surface $\{K{=}1\}$. We collect the specific results from~\cite{K1paper} needed below; all proofs are in~\cite{K1paper}.

Let $G$ be a non-degenerate symmetric $2\times 2$ real matrix, $V = \tfrac{1}{2}(K-1)^2$ the cost function, and $J_G = \alpha G^{-1}J$ the unique symplectic generator~\cite[Theorem~1]{K1paper}. The stochastic dynamics is $dx = (J_G - D)\nabla V\,dt + \sigma\,dW_t$ (It\^o), with $D = d_c I$ at critical damping.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Stability boundary}~\cite[Theorem~4]{K1paper}: $d_c > 0 \Leftrightarrow \mathrm{Sig}(G) = (1,1)$ (Lorentzian), with $d_c = \alpha\sqrt{-1/\det G}$. For the Rindler metric $G = \mathrm{diag}(-\sigma_1^2, 1)$, this simplifies to $d_c = \alpha/\sigma_1$. The Lorentzian requirement is derived from cost-asymmetry axioms in~\cite{Realizability}.
\item \textbf{Restoring rate}~\cite[Theorem~6]{K1paper}: $\kappa_K = 4d_c$ for a normalised $G$-eigenvector on $\{K{=}1\}$.
\item \textbf{OU process}: Near $K{=}1$, the deviation $\varepsilon = K - 1$ obeys $d\varepsilon = -\kappa_K \varepsilon\,dt + \sigma_K\,dW_t$, where $\sigma_K = 2\sigma\|Gx_*\|$ is the projected noise amplitude ($\sigma$ is the isotropic noise in $\mathbb{R}^2$, $x_*$ is the base point on $\{K{=}1\}$). At the canonical Rindler point $x_* = (0,1)$: $Gx_* = (0,1)$, $\|Gx_*\| = 1$, and $\sigma_K = 2\sigma$. The stationary variance is $T_{\mathrm{eff}} = \sigma_K^2/(2\kappa_K) = \sigma^2/(2d_c)$.
\item \textbf{Clausius relation}~\cite[Proposition~7]{K1paper}: Under the identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}} = T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ --- the principal external (phenomenological) input connecting the stochastic model to Rindler thermodynamics --- the Clausius relation $\delta Q = T_{\mathrm{eff}}\,\delta S$ follows algebraically. Matching with the standard Tolman result $T_{\mathrm{tol}} = \hbar\kappa/(2\pi\sigma_1)$ fixes $\alpha = 1$; consequently $\sigma^2 = \hbar\kappa/(\pi\sigma_1^2)$, leaving no free parameters beyond $\hbar$ and the spacetime quantities $\kappa$, $\sigma_1$.
\end{itemize}

\section{Wave function from Lorentzian geometry}\label{sec:wavefunction}
%=====================================================================

\subsection{Fokker--Planck to Schr\"odinger}

The OU process for $\varepsilon = K - 1$ generates the FP equation
\begin{equation}
\partial_t \rho = \partial_\varepsilon\bigl[\kappa_K \varepsilon\,\rho\bigr] + D_K\,\partial_\varepsilon^2 \rho,
\label{eq:FP}
\end{equation}
with $D_K = \sigma_K^2/2$ and stationary solution $\rho_{\mathrm{eq}} \propto e^{-\kappa_K \varepsilon^2/(2D_K)} = e^{-V/T_{\mathrm{eff}}}$.

The similarity transform $\rho = e^{-U/2}\psi$ with $U = V/T_{\mathrm{eff}}$ converts~\eqref{eq:FP} to
\begin{equation}
\partial_t \psi = -H_s\,\psi, \qquad H_s = -D_K\partial_\varepsilon^2 + \frac{\kappa_K^2\varepsilon^2}{4D_K} - \frac{\kappa_K}{2}.
\label{eq:imaginary_time}
\end{equation}
This is the quantum harmonic oscillator in imaginary time. Wick rotating $t \to i\tau$:
\begin{equation}
i\,\partial_\tau \psi = H_s\,\psi.
\label{eq:Schrodinger}
\end{equation}

\begin{remark}[What is standard vs.\ what $K{=}1$ adds]
The similarity transform FP~$\to$~imaginary-time Schr\"odinger is standard~\cite{Nelson,Risken}; the Wick rotation $t \to i\tau$ is a formal algebraic operation that maps the spectral structure of the FP generator to a Hermitian Hamiltonian. It is not a claim of physical equivalence between dissipative relaxation and unitary evolution; the physical content resides entirely in the OU/FP dynamics, and the Wick-rotated equation inherits its structure as a mathematical consequence. The $K{=}1$ framework adds two things: (i)~the parameters $\kappa_K$, $D_K$ are not free but determined by $\det G$ through~\cite[Theorems~4,\,6]{K1paper}; (ii)~the existence of the OU process --- and hence of $\psi$ --- is conditional on signature, providing a geometric on/off switch absent in Nelson's original construction.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Existence requires Lorentzian signature}

The chain~\eqref{eq:FP}--\eqref{eq:Schrodinger} requires:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $d_c > 0$ (OU process exists $\Rightarrow$ $\kappa_K > 0$ $\Rightarrow$ FP has a normalisable stationary solution).
\item $d_c > 0 \Leftrightarrow \det G < 0$ (Lorentzian)~\cite[Theorem~4]{K1paper}.
\end{enumerate}

For Euclidean signature ($\det G > 0$), $d_c$ is formally imaginary: $d_c = i\omega$ with $\omega = \alpha/\sigma_1$. The OU process does not exist (no real restoring rate), the FP equation has no normalisable stationary solution, and the similarity transform does not yield a physical $\psi$.

\begin{corollary}[Geometric precondition]\label{cor:precondition}
Within the $K{=}1$ framework, the Fokker--Planck equation admits a similarity transform with Schr\"odinger structure if and only if $\mathrm{Sig}(G) = (1,1)$ (Lorentzian). Equivalently, a wave function $\psi$ with Schr\"odinger dynamics can be defined if and only if $\det G < 0$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{remark}[Status of the FP--Schr\"odinger correspondence]
The similarity transform FP~$\to$~imaginary-time Schr\"odinger is standard~\cite{Nelson,Risken}; stochastic quantisation~\cite{ParisiWu} extends it to field theory. The $K{=}1$ framework adds the \emph{conditional} aspect: the correspondence exists only when $\det G < 0$, providing a geometric on/off switch absent in Nelson's original construction. The OU structure is not an approximation but the unique compatible linearisation~\cite[Theorem~5]{K1paper}. For $V = \tfrac{1}{2}(K-1)^2$ as defined in Law~III~\cite{K1paper}, the ground state $\psi_0 \propto e^{-U/2}$ is exact; if $V$ contained higher-order terms, $\psi_0$ would be modified, but the existence condition $d_c > 0 \Leftrightarrow \det G < 0$ is independent of the form of~$V$ and holds non-perturbatively. The physical interpretation --- that $\psi$ exists when the signature is Lorentzian and ceases to exist when it is not --- is an inference from the mathematical structure, not a separate postulate.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Ground-state probability as Boltzmann equilibrium}

The ground-state wave function satisfies $\psi_0 \propto e^{-U/2}$; upon normalisation,
\begin{equation}
\psi_0 = Z^{-1/2} e^{-U/2}, \qquad Z = \int e^{-U}\,d\varepsilon.
\end{equation}
Therefore $|\psi_0|^2 = Z^{-1}e^{-U} = \rho_{\mathrm{eq}}$. The ground-state probability equals the Boltzmann distribution --- not a postulate but a consequence of metric self-consistency.

\begin{remark}[Ground state only]
This identity holds exactly for the ground state. For excited states, $e^{-U/2}\psi_n$ can be negative and is not a probability density. However, the $K{=}1$ chain derives a concrete Hamiltonian $H_s$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:imaginary_time}); the fact that $|\psi|^2$ is the unique conserved non-negative density under the unitary evolution generated by $H_s$ is a mathematical property of the derived operator, not an external postulate.
\end{remark}

\section{Signature dynamics: one-way barrier}\label{sec:barrier}
%=====================================================================

Section~\ref{sec:wavefunction} showed that $\psi$ requires Lorentzian signature. But is Lorentzian signature stable? Can fluctuations push the system across $\Delta = 0$ into a Euclidean regime where $\psi$ ceases to exist? We now treat $\Delta = \det G$ as a dynamical variable and show that the answer is asymmetric: \emph{Lorentzian~$\to$~Euclidean is blocked, while Euclidean~$\to$~Lorentzian is spontaneous.}

\subsection{Noise coefficient from fluctuation-dissipation}

From the $K{=}1$ OU process~\cite[Proposition~7]{K1paper}, the effective temperature is $T_{\mathrm{eff}} = \sigma^2\sigma_1/(2\alpha)$. For the Rindler metric $G = \mathrm{diag}(-\sigma_1^2, 1)$, the determinant is $\Delta = \det G = -\sigma_1^2$, giving $\sigma_1 = \sqrt{|\Delta|}$ and hence
\[
T_{\mathrm{eff}} = \frac{\sigma^2}{2\alpha}\sqrt{|\Delta|}.
\]
By the fluctuation-dissipation relation, the noise coefficient $D_L$ for the $\Delta$ dynamics inherits the local effective temperature $T_{\mathrm{eff}}$:
\begin{equation}
D_L(\Delta) = \frac{\sigma^2}{2\alpha}\sqrt{|\Delta|}, \qquad \Delta < 0.
\label{eq:D_Lor}
\end{equation}
Strictly, this identification involves a variable transformation from $\varepsilon = K - 1$ to $\Delta = \det G$, introducing a Jacobian that affects the numerical coefficient but not the scaling $D_L \propto \sqrt{|\Delta|} \to 0$ as $\Delta \to 0^-$. The scaling holds for any diagonal Lorentzian $G$ (since $T_{\mathrm{eff}} \propto \sigma_1 \to 0$); the coefficient $\sigma^2/(2\alpha)$ is Rindler-specific. Only the scaling enters the barrier analysis below.

In the Euclidean regime ($\Delta > 0$), no OU process exists; the noise is external (e.g., thermal environment):
\begin{equation}
D_E(\Delta) = D_0 > 0, \qquad \Delta > 0.
\label{eq:D_Euc}
\end{equation}
The value $D_0$ is a modelling assumption. Physically, the Euclidean regime lacks the OU self-suppression mechanism that drives $D_L \to 0$: without a real restoring rate ($d_c$ is imaginary for $\det G > 0$), there is no feedback loop to quench fluctuations. External noise sources (thermal, gravitational, or quantum vacuum fluctuations) have no reason to vanish at $\Delta = 0$, so $D_E > 0$ is the natural default. The one-way barrier requires only $D_0 > 0$ (any positive value suffices).

\subsection{Entropic barrier from Lorentzian side}

As $\Delta \to 0^-$: $D_L \to 0$. The Fokker--Planck stationary distribution (It\^o convention) is
\begin{equation}
\rho_{\mathrm{ss}}(\Delta) \propto \frac{1}{D(\Delta)}\exp\!\biggl(\int^{\Delta}\frac{F(s)}{D(s)}\,ds\biggr).
\label{eq:rho_ss}
\end{equation}
The $1/D$ prefactor diverges as $\Delta \to 0^-$: in the It\^o convention, probability density accumulates near the boundary. However, $D \to 0$ means the diffusive flux vanishes --- the system cannot cross $\Delta = 0$ because mobility drops to zero. The stationary current $J_{\mathrm{ss}} = F\rho - \partial_\Delta(D\rho) = 0$ confirms zero net flux within the Lorentzian interior (the boundary requires separate analysis; see \S\ref{sec:detailed_balance}). This creates an effective barrier: the OU process suppresses its own fluctuations near the signature boundary.

From the Euclidean side ($\Delta > 0$), $D_E = D_0$ is constant. No self-suppression operates, and the confining potential drives the system toward $\Delta_0 < 0$ (Lorentzian): the Euclidean side is downhill.

\begin{remark}[Noise interpretation]
Equation~\eqref{eq:rho_ss} is the It\^o-convention stationary distribution, consistent with the Euler--Maruyama simulation below and the Fokker--Planck barrier~\eqref{eq:barrier} in \S\ref{sec:multimode}. In the Stratonovich convention, the stationary distribution takes a different form that yields an entropic correction $-\tfrac{1}{2}\ln D \to +\infty$ as $\Delta \to 0^-$, resembling a divergent effective potential. Both conventions give the same physical conclusion: $D \to 0$ at the boundary prevents crossing from the Lorentzian side. The direction of the one-way barrier is convention-independent.
\end{remark}

\subsection{One-way barrier: Monte Carlo verification}

We simulate the It\^o Langevin equation for $\Delta$ with state-dependent noise:
\begin{equation}
d\Delta = F(\Delta)\,dt + \sqrt{2D(\Delta)}\,dW_t,
\end{equation}
where $F(\Delta)$ includes a confining drift toward $\Delta_0 < 0$ and an entropic correction from the state-dependent noise. At $\Delta = 0$, the effective potential is regularised (finite barrier replaces the singularity); no artificial boundary condition is imposed. The one-way direction depends only on $D(0^-) = 0$ vs $D(0^+) > 0$, not on the specific form of $F$ or the regularisation.

To confirm that the one-way barrier arises from noise asymmetry (not potential shape), an artificial Gaussian trap is added on the Euclidean side at $\Delta_E = 1.0$.

Weak traps ($\mu_E \le 1$) do not affect the one-way barrier. Strong traps ($\mu_E \ge 2$) can hold the system in the Euclidean regime, but no natural Euclidean trap exists within $K{=}1$: the free energy $\mathcal{F}(\sigma_1)$ is everywhere concave ($d^2\mathcal{F}/d\sigma_1^2 < 0$ for all $\sigma_1 > 0$), precluding any Euclidean minimum.

\subsection{Detailed balance: analytical proof}\label{sec:detailed_balance}

\begin{proposition}[Detailed balance violation]\label{prop:DB}
Detailed balance is necessarily broken at $\Delta = 0$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The FP stationary condition $J_{\mathrm{ss}} = F\rho - \partial_\Delta(D\rho) = 0$ requires $D\rho$ to have a continuous derivative at every point.

At $\Delta = 0$:
\begin{equation}
D(0^-) = \lim_{\Delta \to 0^-} \frac{\sigma^2}{2\alpha}\sqrt{|\Delta|} = 0, \qquad D(0^+) = D_0 > 0.
\end{equation}
$D(\Delta)$ is discontinuous at $\Delta = 0$. Therefore $D\rho$ cannot have a continuous derivative at the boundary, $J_{\mathrm{ss}} \neq 0$, and detailed balance is impossible across $\Delta = 0$.

The discontinuity is not a modelling choice: $D_L \propto T_{\mathrm{eff}} \to 0$ follows from the OU self-suppression (the OU process exists only for $d_c > 0$, i.e., Lorentzian signature), while $D_E = D_0 > 0$ follows from the absence of self-suppression in the Euclidean regime.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Detailed balance \emph{holds} within the Lorentzian regime, where $D(\Delta)$ is continuous and the system obeys standard Langevin dynamics. The violation is only across the signature boundary.
\end{remark}

\section{Measurement mechanism}\label{sec:measurement}
%=====================================================================

\subsection{Collapse as Euclidean excursion}

The barrier structure provides a geometric mechanism for quantum measurement:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Wave evolution (Lorentzian).} The system resides in the Lorentzian regime, where the OU process generates $\rho$ and the similarity transform $\psi = e^{U/2}\rho$ defines a well-defined wave function (\S\ref{sec:wavefunction}). The entropic barrier prevents spontaneous escape to the Euclidean side, so superposition is maintained.

\item \textbf{Measurement trigger.} The measurement apparatus couples the system to a macroscopic environment, increasing $D_{\mathrm{cross}}$ and exponentially reducing the Kramers barrier. In the $(1{+}3)$D extension, $N = 3$ and $\gamma = 1$ are geometrically fixed; the apparatus instead modifies the \emph{effective} symplectic eigenvalue~$\sigma_1$, lowering $B_{\mathrm{FP}} \propto \sigma_1^2$. In the present $2{\times}2$ model, this effect is parameterised by the multi-mode coupling (\S\ref{sec:multimode}).

\item \textbf{Collapse.} Mode~1's $\Delta$ crosses zero. In the multi-mode picture, this is a \emph{local} event: the other modes remain Lorentzian ($\sigma_{1,m} > 0$) and their wave functions persist. Only the degree of freedom associated with the collapsing mode loses its OU process and hence its $\psi$ support.

\item \textbf{Recovery.} With no barrier from the Euclidean side, the system falls back to Lorentzian (100\% of runs, mean time $\sim 0.4$). The OU process restarts.

\item \textbf{Post-collapse equilibrium.} The restarted OU process decays to $\rho_{\mathrm{eq}} = |\psi_0|^2$. Because the OU stationary distribution is independent of initial conditions --- this is the standard ergodic property of Ornstein--Uhlenbeck dynamics --- all information about the pre-measurement superposition is erased during re-equilibration.

\item \textbf{Outcome selection (open).} The mechanism above describes \emph{that} collapse occurs and \emph{why} the post-collapse distribution is $|\psi_0|^2$, but does not determine \emph{which} eigenvalue is selected in a given run. No existing approach fully resolves outcome selection: Copenhagen postulates it~\cite{vonNeumann}; decoherence derives a preferred basis but not individual outcomes~\cite{Zurek}; GRW~\cite{GRW} and Penrose~\cite{Penrose} give collapse rates but not specific results. The present work contributes (a)~the mechanism (signature crossing) and (b)~the post-collapse distribution ($|\psi_0|^2 = \rho_{\mathrm{eq}}$) from geometry; outcome selection~(c) likely requires the $(1{+}3)$D multi-mode structure, in which the surviving Lorentzian modes ($\sigma_{1,m} > 0$, $m \neq 1$) act as an effective pointer whose configuration conditions the recovery of the collapsing mode. This geometric analogue of einselection is deferred to future work.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Multi-mode coupling and finite barrier}\label{sec:multimode}

In $(1{+}3)$D, multiple horizon directions coexist. We adopt an illustrative mean-field model with $N$ modes:
\begin{equation}
d\varepsilon_n = -\kappa_n \varepsilon_n\,dt + \sigma\,dW_n + \gamma\sum_{m\neq n}(\varepsilon_m - \varepsilon_n)\,dt,
\label{eq:multimode}
\end{equation}
where $\gamma > 0$ is the inter-mode coupling. Under mean-field approximation, each mode's $\sigma_1$ couples to the others through a shared effective $\bar\varepsilon = N^{-1}\sum\varepsilon_m$. The effective noise on mode~1's $\sigma_1$ decomposes as
\begin{equation}
D_{\mathrm{total}} = \underbrace{D_{\mathrm{self}}}_{\propto \sigma_1 \to 0} + \underbrace{D_{\mathrm{cross}}}_{> 0}.
\end{equation}
Here $D_{\mathrm{self}} \propto \gamma^2\sigma^2\sigma_1/(2\alpha N)$ arises from mode~1's own OU fluctuations, which vanish as $\sigma_1 \to 0$ by the same mechanism as the single-mode case. The cross-mode contribution $D_{\mathrm{cross}} \approx \gamma^2(N{-}1)T_{\mathrm{eff,others}}/N^2$ arises from modes $2,\ldots,N$ whose $\sigma_{1,m} > 0$ (they remain Lorentzian) and whose OU processes provide finite noise. At the boundary: $D_{\mathrm{self}}/D_{\mathrm{total}} \to 0$ and $D_{\mathrm{cross}}/D_{\mathrm{total}} \to 1$.

The Fokker--Planck stationary solution for mode~1, $\rho_{\mathrm{ss}} \propto D^{-1}\exp\!\bigl(\int F/D\,d\sigma_1\bigr)$, yields a finite barrier:
\begin{equation}
B_{\mathrm{FP}} = \Phi_{\mathrm{FP}}(\text{boundary}) - \Phi_{\mathrm{FP}}(\text{minimum}), \qquad \Phi_{\mathrm{FP}} = -\!\int \frac{F}{D}\,d\sigma_1 + \ln D.
\label{eq:barrier}
\end{equation}

\noindent\textbf{Numerical results} ($\gamma = 0.1$, $N = 8$, $\sigma = 0.3$, $\alpha = 1$):
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
& Single-mode & Multi-mode \\
\midrule
Barrier $B_{\mathrm{FP}}$ & $\to \infty$ & $\sim 7600$ \\
Rate $\Gamma$ & 0 & $\sim e^{-7600} \approx 0$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Spontaneous collapse is theoretically possible but practically negligible --- far rarer than the age of the universe. The measurement apparatus acts as a \emph{catalyst}: it increases $D_{\mathrm{cross}}$ (by coupling more environmental modes), exponentially reducing $B_{\mathrm{FP}}$ and accelerating collapse to observable timescales.

\begin{remark}[Infinite regress resolved]
In the single-mode picture, collapse requires an external push --- raising the question: what pushes the pusher? The multi-mode picture resolves this: other modes' OU fluctuations provide $D_{\mathrm{cross}}$, enabling self-consistent collapse without external input. Measurement creates nothing; it accelerates an intrinsically possible process.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Model dependence]
The multi-mode analysis uses a mean-field coupling with free parameter $\gamma$. The qualitative conclusion ($D_{\mathrm{cross}} > 0 \Rightarrow$ finite barrier) is robust, but the numerical value $B_{\mathrm{FP}} \sim 7600$ depends on $\gamma$ and $N$. Deriving $\gamma$ from $K{=}1$ geometry is an open problem.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Irreversibility of the signature excursion}

The barrier asymmetry explains irreversibility:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Forward} (Lor $\to$ Euc): barrier $B_{\mathrm{FP}} \gg 1$. Rate $\sim e^{-B_{\mathrm{FP}}} \approx 0$ without environmental catalysis.
\item \textbf{Return} (Euc $\to$ Lor): no barrier. Spontaneous in $\sim 0.4$ time units.
\item \textbf{Net effect}: the system returns to $\rho_{\mathrm{eq}}$, not the original superposition. The OU ergodicity erases the pre-measurement state; once the system re-enters the Lorentzian regime, the entropic barrier ($D \to 0$ at $\Delta = 0$) reforms and protects the new equilibrium against further spontaneous collapse.
\end{itemize}

\noindent The collapse is objective: the geometric precondition ($\det G < 0$) fails independently of any observer or measurement convention. In this respect, the $K{=}1$ mechanism is closer to Penrose~\cite{Penrose} and GRW~\cite{GRW} than to Copenhagen or many-worlds.

\subsection{Relation to decoherence}

Standard decoherence explains the apparent disappearance of interference through environmental entanglement and identifies preferred pointer states via einselection~\cite{Zurek}. The $K{=}1$ one-way barrier is compatible with decoherence and adds geometric content: environmental coupling increases $D_{\mathrm{cross}}$, catalysing the Kramers escape that drives $\Delta$ across the signature boundary. The barrier structure provides a geometric counterpart to the irreversibility of decoherence (the $D$ discontinuity at $\Delta = 0$ prevents spontaneous recoherence) and identifies the post-collapse equilibrium distribution as $|\psi_0|^2$ (Boltzmann equilibrium of the restarted OU process). The two frameworks differ in the \emph{status} of irreversibility: under decoherence, a Loschmidt echo (time-reversal of all degrees of freedom) would in principle restore interference; under $K{=}1$, the $D$ discontinuity at $\Delta = 0$ is not reversed by time-reversal, so the echo does not return. However, the $K{=}1$ framework does not yet reproduce einselection or pointer-state selection, which remain open problems (\S\ref{sec:open}, item~7).

\subsection{Relation to Penrose and GRW}

The $K{=}1$ precondition approach shares structural features with both Penrose~\cite{Penrose} and GRW~\cite{GRW}: all three identify conditions under which unitary evolution breaks down. The differences are in mechanism and scope. Penrose's criterion is energetic: superpositions become unstable when the gravitational self-energy $E_G$ exceeds $\hbar/\tau$ for the decay time $\tau$; the Schr\"odinger equation breaks down above a mass threshold. $K{=}1$'s criterion is geometric: the equation's precondition ($\det G < 0$) can be dynamically removed by signature fluctuations, regardless of mass. The two are complementary rather than competing; a synthesis would require connecting $K{=}1$ signature dynamics to the Newtonian limit of $E_G$.

GRW and $K{=}1$ both introduce stochasticity, but at different levels. In GRW, the stochastic term is added to the wave function's evolution equation: $\psi$ itself undergoes random localisation events. In $K{=}1$, the stochastic dynamics governs the \emph{metric} ($\Delta = \det G$ obeys a Langevin equation); the wave function $\psi$ evolves unitarily whenever it exists, and collapses only when its geometric precondition fails. The stochasticity is in the arena, not the player.

\subsection{$(1{+}3)$-dimensional extension}

In $(1{+}3)$D, $\{K{=}1\} \cong H^3$ carries $S^2$ cross-sections with area $4\pi\sigma_1^2$. Combined with the Clausius relation~\cite[Proposition~7]{K1paper}, this provides two of the three Jacobson inputs~\cite{Jacobson} for deriving the Einstein equation: the Clausius relation $\delta Q = T\,\delta S$ and the horizon area element $\delta A$. The third input ($S = A/4$) remains an independent assumption. The three standard pairings of a Rindler metric yield identical $d_c$~\cite[verified numerically]{K1paper}; for Schwarzschild, they do not. The core results of this paper --- one-way barrier, irreversibility, ground-state probability --- depend only on $D(\Delta) \to 0$ at the boundary, which holds in any dimension. The multi-mode barrier height changes quantitatively ($N = 3$ spatial directions instead of arbitrary $N$), but the qualitative mechanism is identical.

\subsection{Open questions}\label{sec:open}

\begin{enumerate}
\item The Tolman identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}} = T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ fixes $\alpha = 1$ and $\sigma^2 = \hbar\kappa/(\pi\sigma_1^2)$ (\S\ref{sec:review}), but this identification is itself a phenomenological input. Deriving it from a purely geometric principle would make the framework fully self-contained.
\item The multi-mode coupling $\gamma$ is not derived from $K{=}1$.
\item Compatibility of $K{=}1$ signature change with path-integral Wick rotation.
\item Multi-particle quantum mechanics.
\item Born rule for excited states (only the ground state is derived here).
\item Absolute collapse rate (requires dimensional restoration and $(1{+}3)$D completion).
\item Outcome selection: what determines \emph{which} eigenvalue is realised in a given measurement? This likely requires a pointer-state analysis within the multi-mode $(1{+}3)$D framework, analogous to einselection in decoherence theory~\cite{Zurek}.
\item Experimental predictions. The spontaneous collapse rate $e^{-B_{\mathrm{FP}}} \approx 0$ is currently indistinguishable from zero. A quantitative prediction would require deriving $\gamma$ and $N$ from $(1{+}3)$D geometry and identifying regimes where the barrier is low enough to produce observable deviations from unitary evolution --- for instance, in mesoscopic superposition experiments or near-horizon quantum effects.
\end{enumerate}

%=====================================================================
\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
%=====================================================================

A single algebraic fact --- $d_c > 0$ if and only if $\det G < 0$~\cite[Theorem~4]{K1paper} --- and its stochastic consequence, that the OU noise self-suppresses near the signature boundary ($D \propto \sqrt{|\Delta|} \to 0$), together yield five results:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Within this framework, the wave function exists if and only if the signature is Lorentzian.
\item Lorentzian signature is dynamically stable (entropic barrier).
\item Collapse is a temporary Euclidean excursion (one-way barrier).
\item The signature excursion is irreversible ($D$ discontinuous at $\Delta = 0$).
\item The ground-state probability distribution is Boltzmann equilibrium ($|\psi_0|^2 = \rho_{\mathrm{eq}}$).
\end{enumerate}

The approach does not modify the Schr\"odinger equation. It operates at the level of the equation's geometric precondition --- Lorentzian signature --- and shows that the precondition is dynamical. Collapse is not a defect of quantum mechanics to be repaired; it is the temporary absence of the geometric condition that makes quantum mechanics possible.
