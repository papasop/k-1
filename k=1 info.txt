\begin{abstract}
From Newton's absolute time through proper time in general relativity, time enters physical theory as a primitive. Redefining it as the cost of structural change and requiring realizability (positive temporal cost and zero-threshold spatial directions) forces a $2\times 2$ metric $G$ indefinite; the sign of $\det G$ then simultaneously determines Lorentzian signature, real symplectic spectrum, and a nontrivial stability boundary $d_{c}>0$ (Theorem~4). The metric arises as the Hessian of an information time $dt_{\mathrm{info}}=d\Phi/H$; the quadratic form $K(x)=x^{\top}Gx$ measures local temporal cost, and the condition $K=1$ --- the metric reproducing its own normalisation --- requires that this cost equal unity. The evolution law $\dot{x}=(J_{G}-D)\nabla V$ is unique (Theorem~5), and at critical damping the restoring rate is $\kappa_{K}=4d_{c}$ for a normalised $G$-eigenvector (Theorem~6).

Under local thermal equilibrium, the Clausius relation is equivalent to $\sigma^{2}=2d_{c}\cdot T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ (Proposition~7). The $\{K=1\}$ manifold carries two classes of fluctuation: thermodynamic (Class~A, perpendicular to $\{K=1\}$) and gauge (Class~B, tangential boost diffusion). Within Class~A the Clausius relation $\delta Q=T_{\mathrm{eff}}\,\delta S$ follows algebraically; the local noise temperature has the structure of Tolman's law. The sole external input is the identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$; Gromov non-squeezing cannot replace it.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction: time as a derived quantity}

In Newton's mechanics, time is a uniform background coordinate; in general relativity, it is the proper time defined by a Lorentzian metric whose signature is prescribed. The Jacobson derivation~\cite{Jacobson} obtains the Einstein equations from thermodynamics on local Rindler horizons, but Lorentzian signature is still assumed. The EPS axiomatisation~\cite{EPS} derives causal structure from free-fall and light propagation, but requires kinematic primitives. In every approach to Riemann's open problem --- the origin of the metric --- either the signature is prescribed or the derivation requires kinematic primitives.

Here time is neither coordinate nor metric primitive: it is the cost of structural change, and the metric --- including its Lorentzian signature --- is a consequence. This redefinition resolves the question in the $2\times 2$ setting natural to a single local Rindler horizon: an information time $dt_{\mathrm{info}}:=d\Phi/H$ defines a metric $G=\nabla^{2}(dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2})$, and realizability~\cite{Realizability} forces $\det G<0$; Lorentzian signature, symplectic structure, and $d_{c}>0$ are then algebraic consequences of a single sign.

All subsequent results depend on $G$ and its signature alone. The quadratic form $K(x)=x^{\top}Gx$ measures local temporal cost; the condition $K=1$ requires that this cost equal unity --- below unity the metric underestimates; above unity it overestimates. This is not a passive normalisation: the metric defines $K$, and $K=1$ requires the metric to reproduce its own normalisation --- departures generate a restoring dynamics (Law~III), making this self-referential condition a dynamical attractor. Geometrically, $\{K=1\}$ is the unit hyperboloid of~$G$.

The GENERIC formalism~\cite{GENERIC} provides the closest dynamical precedent; we show it is the isotropic specialisation of the unique evolution law (Theorem~5). The main results are: (i)~$d_{c}>0$ iff $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$ (Theorem~4); (ii)~unique evolution law (Theorem~5); (iii)~$\kappa_{K}=4d_{c}$ at critical damping (Theorem~6); (iv)~Clausius relation from stochastic dynamics within Class~A (Proposition~7). The physical interpretation (\S7) requires a single external identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$; scope and open questions are collected in~\S8.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Notation.} $\mathrm{spec}(A)$: spectrum of $A$. $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(p,q)$: inertia ($p$ positive, $q$ negative eigenvalues). $J=\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}0&1\\-1&0\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$: canonical symplectic matrix. $\|\cdot\|$: Euclidean norm on~$\mathbb{R}^{2}$. $\nabla$: Euclidean gradient on~$\mathbb{R}^{2}$.

\section{Metric from information time (Law~I)}

\noindent\textbf{Law~I.} \textit{The metric $G$ is a non-degenerate symmetric bilinear form measuring the local cost of temporal displacement, where `temporal' denotes the direction distinguished by positive cost \textup{(Assumption~T of~\cite{Realizability})}.}

\medskip

The information time $dt_{\mathrm{info}}:=d\Phi/H$, where $\Phi$ parametrises structural variation and $H>0$ is an entropic resistance, provides the metric through its Hessian: the local quadratic approximation $dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2}\approx \delta x^{\top}G\,\delta x$ defines $G=\nabla^{2}(dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2})$; the condition $K(x):=x^{\top}Gx=1$ then identifies the self-consistent locus. The redefinition enters the theorems through $G$ alone: $dt_{\mathrm{info}}$ serves as the motivating construction for~$G$, which enters the theorems as the leading-order form~$Q$ of~\cite{Realizability}. The Hessian $G=\nabla^{2}(dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2})$ has the structure of an information metric --- it measures the local cost of displacement, as the Fisher metric measures the local cost of statistical distinguishability~\cite{Amari} --- but with Lorentzian rather than Riemannian signature. The specific nature of~$\Phi$ is not required by the theorems; what is required is that not all directions carry equal cost --- equivalently, that the cost structure not be definite (Theorem~3). The theorems follow from the asymmetry, not from the identity of the varying structure.

The realizability theorem of~\cite{Realizability} therefore applies directly, forcing $G$ indefinite: if the cost admits zero-threshold directions while pure temporal displacements retain positive cost, then $Q$ takes both positive and non-positive values, so $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$ is the unique possibility for a non-degenerate $2\times 2$ form, giving Lorentzian geometry with light cones and a causal structure. In general relativity, the Lorentzian metric is prescribed on a pre-existing differentiable manifold; here $G$ and its causal structure are derived from the cost structure.

\section{Symplectic structure from $G$}

\begin{theorem}[Structural Uniqueness]
Let $G$ be a non-degenerate symmetric $2\times 2$ real matrix. Among all matrices $M$ such that
\textup{(i)}~$GM$ is skew-symmetric and \textup{(ii)}~$M|_{G=I}=\alpha J$,
the unique solution is $J_{G}=\alpha G^{-1}J$.
\end{theorem}

Here $\alpha>0$ is a normalisation constant fixed by condition~(ii); all subsequent results are homogeneous in $\alpha$, and $\alpha=1$ is adopted wherever $d_{c}$ is compared with thermal quantities. Since $G$ is dimensionless in the information-time parametrisation, $J_{G}=\alpha G^{-1}J$ is also dimensionless.

\begin{proof}
$\dim(\mathrm{Skew}_{2})=1$, so condition~(i) gives $GM=\beta J$, hence $M=\beta G^{-1}J$; condition~(ii) fixes $\beta=\alpha$.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Non-degeneracy vs.\ signature]
Theorem~1 requires only $\det G\neq 0$; it holds for any signature. The Lorentzian condition enters decisively only in Theorem~2, through the reality of the symplectic spectrum.
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}[Spectral Survival]
Let $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$. The characteristic polynomial of $G^{-1}J$ is $\lambda^{2}+1/\det G=0$. Since $\det G<0$, the roots are real: $\lambda=\pm\sqrt{-1/\det G}$. In the isotropic linearisation $H_{*}=I$, the stability boundary is
\begin{equation}
d_{c}(\alpha)=\alpha\sqrt{-1/\det G}>0.\label{eq:dc}
\end{equation}
The dominant eigenvalue satisfies $\lambda_{\max}(d)=-(d-d_{c})$ and $\tau\sim(d-d_{c})^{-1}$ near threshold.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$\mathrm{tr}(G^{-1}J)=0$ and $\det(G^{-1}J)=1/\det G$. For $H_{*}=I$, $\max\mathrm{Re}\,\mathrm{spec}(J_{G}-dI)=0$ at $d=d_{c}$.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Necessity of Lorentzian Signature]
If $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(2,0)$ or $(0,2)$, then $\det G>0$, so $\lambda^{2}+1/\det G=0$ has purely imaginary roots and $d_{c}=0$.
\end{theorem}

\section{The stability boundary}

\begin{theorem}[Non-Separability]
For non-degenerate symmetric $2\times 2$ real $G$, under the isotropic linearisation $H_{*}=I$, the following are equivalent:
\begin{itemize}
\item[\textup{(a)}] The metric admits a nontrivial stability boundary $d_{c}>0$.
\item[\textup{(b)}] $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$ \textup{(Lorentzian)}, equivalently $\det G<0$.
\end{itemize}
The equivalence fails for general $H_{*}$ \textup{(Remark~\ref{rem:Hstar_dep})}: $d_{c}$ is a signature invariant only within the isotropic class.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$d_{c}>0 \Leftrightarrow -1/\det G>0 \Leftrightarrow \det G<0 \Leftrightarrow \mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$, via Theorems~2 and~3.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Isotropic scaling]
$H_{*}$ denotes the cost-function weight matrix in $d(\delta x)/dt=(J_{G}-dI)H_{*}\,\delta x$; $H_{*}=I$ is the default. For $H_{*}=cI$ ($c>0$), $d_{c}$ is invariant. For general $H_{*}>0$, the threshold may shift.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Dependence on $H_{*}$]\label{rem:Hstar_dep}
With $G=\mathrm{diag}(0.1,-10)$ and $H_{*}=\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}1&0.9\\0.9&1\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$, $d_{c}^{\mathrm{iso}}=1$ while $d_{c}(H_{*})=4.55$, confirming that $d_{c}$ may depend on $H_{*}$ outside the isotropic class.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Isotropy of $H_{*}$ for the Rindler metric]
For $G=\mathrm{diag}(-(\kappa\ell)^{2},1)$ and diagonal $H_{*}$, one has $\mathrm{tr}(J_{G}H_{*})=0$ identically, so $d_{c}=\alpha/(\kappa\ell)$ independently of the diagonal entries. The thermodynamic interpretation of \S\ref{sec:physical} assumes $H_{*}$ diagonal; this is a necessary scope condition.
\end{remark}

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Numerical verification.} Among 2000 random symmetric $2\times 2$ matrices: all 1420 with $\mathrm{Sig}=(1,1)$ had $d_{c}>0$; all 580 with definite signature had $d_{c}=0$.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Scope.} The closed-form $d_{c}=\alpha\sqrt{-1/\det G}$ relies on $\dim(\mathrm{Skew}_{2})=1$; higher-dimensional extensions require additional structure and are not part of the present paper.

\section{Unique dynamics (Law~II)}

\noindent\textbf{Law~II.} \textit{The dynamics on $\{K=1\}$ take the unique form $\dot{x}=(J_{G}-D)\nabla V$, compatible with $GJ_{G}$ skew-symmetric and $D\ge 0$.}

\begin{theorem}[Law~II Canonical Decomposition]
Let $G$ be non-degenerate and let $J_{G}=\alpha G^{-1}J$ \textup{(Theorem~1)}. Suppose the generator $A$ splits as
\begin{equation}
GA = \alpha J - GD, \qquad D\ge 0.\label{eq:decomp}
\end{equation}
Then the evolution law is uniquely $\dot{x} = (J_{G}-D)\,\nabla V$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$A=G^{-1}(\alpha J-GD)=J_{G}-D$. Uniqueness is immediate: $J_{G}$ is fixed by Theorem~1 and $D\ge 0$ is prescribed.\qed
\end{proof}

The generator decomposes as $J_{G}=A_{\mathrm{skew}}+S$ where $S=(J_{G}+J_{G}^{\top})/2$. For Lorentzian $G$: $\mathrm{spec}(J_{G})\subset\mathbb{R}$, $S\neq 0$, $\mathrm{tr}\,S=0$. For definite $G$: $\mathrm{spec}(J_{G})\subset i\mathbb{R}$ and $S=0$ iff $G\propto I$.

The GENERIC formalism~\cite{GENERIC} requires Euclidean antisymmetry $L^{\top}=-L$. For $G=cI$, $J_{G}$ is antisymmetric and Law~II reduces to isothermal GENERIC. For Lorentzian $G$, $J_{G}$ is $G$-antisymmetric but not Euclidean-antisymmetric ($S\neq 0$), so Law~II falls outside GENERIC as formulated in~\cite{GENERIC}.

\section{Critical damping and fluctuation structure (Law~III)}\label{sec:lawIII}

\noindent\textbf{Law~III.} \textit{$K=1$ is selected by the stochastic dynamics as the unique self-consistent locus of the metric.}

\medskip

The auxiliary linear system $\dot{x}=(J_{G}-d_{c}I)x$ has eigenvalues $0$ and $-2d_{c}$ at critical damping. Theorem~6 concerns the nonlinear system $\dot{x}=(J_{G}-d_{c}I)\nabla V$, $V=\tfrac{1}{2}(K-1)^{2}$, where $V$ penalises departure from $K=1$. On $\{K=1\}$ one has $\nabla V=0$, so $x_{*}$ is an equilibrium; the Jacobian at $x_{*}$ is $(J_{G}-d_{c}I)\nabla^{2}V(x_{*})$, which differs from $J_{G}-d_{c}I$ because $\nabla^{2}V(x_{*})$ is rank-one.

\begin{theorem}[Local Effective Restoring Rate]
Let $G$ be a $2\times 2$ Lorentzian metric, $D=d_{c}I$. Define $K(x)=x^{\top}Gx$, $V=\tfrac{1}{2}(K-1)^{2}$, with drift $\dot{x}=(J_{G}-d_{c}I)\nabla V$. Then for all $x\in\mathbb{R}^{2}$:
\begin{equation}
\dot{K} = 4(K-1)\bigl[\alpha\,x^{\top}(J{\cdot}G)\,x - d_{c}\,x^{\top}G^{2}x\bigr].\label{eq:Kdot}
\end{equation}
Here $J{\cdot}G$ denotes the ordinary matrix product $JG$ (distinct from the symplectic generator $J_{G}=\alpha G^{-1}J$).
At any $x_{*}\in\{K=1\}$ with $Gx_{*}=\lambda x_{*}$, the term $x_{*}^{\top}JGx_{*}=0$, so
\begin{equation}
\dot{K}\big|_{x=x_{*}} = -4\,d_{c}\,x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}\cdot(K-1).\label{eq:Kdot_eigen}
\end{equation}
With the normalisation $x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}=1$,
\begin{equation}
\dot{K} = -\kappa_{K}(K-1)+o(K-1),\qquad \kappa_{K}=4\,d_{c}.\label{eq:kappa}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$\dot{K}=2x^{\top}G\dot{x}=4(K-1)\,x^{\top}G(J_{G}-d_{c}I)Gx$. Since $GJ_{G}=\alpha J$, right-multiplying by $G$ gives $GJ_{G}G=\alpha(JG)$, yielding~\eqref{eq:Kdot}. At a $G$-eigenvector, $x_{*}^{\top}JGx_{*}=\lambda\,x_{*}^{\top}Jx_{*}=0$ by skew-symmetry.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Base-point dependence]
The local effective restoring rate is $\kappa_{K}(x_{*})=4d_{c}\cdot x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}$; it depends on the base point. For the Rindler metric $G=\mathrm{diag}(-(\kappa\ell)^{2},1)$, $x_{*}=(0,1)$ is the unique $G$-eigenvector on $\{K=1\}$ with $x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}=1$.
\end{remark}

\subsection*{Two classes of fluctuation on $\{K=1\}$}

Adding isotropic additive white noise (It\^{o} convention) to Law~II gives $dx=(J_{G}-D)\nabla V\,dt+\sigma\,dW_{t}$, where $W_{t}$ is a standard $\mathbb{R}^{2}$-valued Wiener process and $\sigma>0$ is constant. At $D=d_{c}I$ (critical damping), two geometrically distinct classes arise.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Class~A \textup{(thermodynamic)}:} Fluctuations of $K$ perpendicular to $\{K=1\}$ --- departures from metric self-consistency --- governed by restoring rate $\kappa_{K}(x_{*})=4d_{c}\,x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}$. The conditional distribution $P(K|x)$ at a fixed base point is Gaussian with variance $\sigma^{2}/(2d_{c})$, verified numerically for $\sigma\in\{0.3,0.6,1.0\}$.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Class~B \textup{(gauge)}:} Free diffusion along $\{K=1\}$ in the tangential (boost) direction. For the Rindler metric, $\{K=1\}$ is the hyperbola $-(\kappa\ell)^{2}x_{1}^{2}+x_{2}^{2}=1$; the tangent direction generates Lorentz boosts --- motion along $\{K=1\}$ changes the inertial observer, not the thermodynamic state. The Hessian of $V$ restricted to $\{K=1\}$ is rank-one with null space containing this tangent, confirming that Class~B carries no restoring force and no heat.

Mixing the two classes conflates thermodynamic and kinematic degrees of freedom and yields a non-Boltzmann marginal $K$-distribution; this is an artifact of marginalising over the gauge direction.

\section{Spacetime thermodynamics}\label{sec:physical}

This section connects the geometric structures of \S\S2--6 to spacetime thermodynamics; every connection requires at least one external identification, marked explicitly.

For the Rindler metric $G=\mathrm{diag}(-(\kappa\ell)^{2},1)$, where $\kappa$ is the surface gravity and $\ell$ the proper distance from the horizon, eq.~\eqref{eq:dc} gives $d_{c}=\alpha/(\kappa\ell)=\alpha/\sqrt{|g_{00}|}$. With $T_{H}=\hbar\kappa/(2\pi)$~\cite{Hawking},
\begin{equation}
d_{c}\cdot T_{H} = \frac{\alpha}{\kappa\ell}\cdot\frac{\hbar\kappa}{2\pi} = \frac{\alpha\hbar}{2\pi\ell},\label{eq:nearhorizon}
\end{equation}
exhibiting exact $\kappa$-cancellation. Hence the Tolman temperature~\cite{Tolman} $T_{\mathrm{tol}}=d_{c}\cdot T_{H}=\alpha\hbar/(2\pi\ell)$.

\subsection*{Clausius relation as FDT consequence within Class~A}

Within Class~A (fixed inertial observer), the $K$-dynamics near $K=1$ is a one-dimensional Ornstein--Uhlenbeck process with stationary variance
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Var}(K)\approx\frac{\sigma^{2}}{2d_{c}}\label{eq:var}
\end{equation}
and effective temperature $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=\sigma^{2}/(2d_{c})$. The fluctuation-dissipation relation $\sigma_{K}^{2}=2\kappa_{K}T_{\mathrm{eff}}$ follows algebraically from Law~II and Theorem~6. The Fokker--Planck equation yields a Boltzmann stationary distribution $\rho\propto\exp(-V/T_{\mathrm{eff}})$; the Clausius relation $\delta Q=T_{\mathrm{eff}}\,dS$ then follows for the OU process, with
\[
\delta Q := dV + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma_{K}^{2}\,dt,\qquad \sigma_{K}=2\sigma\|Gx_{*}\|.
\]
The only external input is the identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}(x_{*})=T_{\mathrm{tol}}(x_{*})$; everything else is derived internally.

\begin{proposition}[Local Clausius Equivalence]
Under local thermal equilibrium and the identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$, the Clausius relation $\delta Q=T\,\delta S$ is equivalent to
\begin{equation}
\sigma^{2} = 2\,d_{c}\cdot T_{\mathrm{tol}} = 2\,d_{c}^{2}\cdot T_{H}.\label{eq:noise}
\end{equation}
Conversely, $\sigma^{2}=2d_{c}\cdot T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ implies $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Setting $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ gives $\sigma^{2}/(2d_{c})=d_{c}\cdot T_{H}$, hence $\sigma^{2}=2d_{c}^{2}\cdot T_{H}$.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Status of the approximation]
Three logically distinct inputs: (i)~the OU approximation, valid when $|K-1|\ll 1$ and Class~B diffusion is slow compared to Class~A relaxation ($\tau=1/(4d_{c})$); (ii)~the identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$, the sole true external axiom, connecting the stochastic model to Rindler thermodynamics; (iii)~the Tolman identity $T_{\mathrm{tol}}=d_{c}T_{H}$, which follows from~\eqref{eq:nearhorizon} and is independently verifiable.
\end{remark}

\subsection*{It\^{o} correction and local temperature}

Applying It\^{o}'s formula to $K=x^{\top}Gx$ gives an additional drift $\sigma^{2}\,\mathrm{tr}(G)\,dt$. This shifts the mean of $K$ but not its variance: at any $G$-eigenvector base point, the FDT ratio $\sigma_{K}^{2}/(2\kappa_{K})$ cancels to $\sigma^{2}/(2d_{c})$ regardless of $x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}$. Hence~\eqref{eq:var} and $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=\sigma^{2}/(2d_{c})$ hold for any Lorentzian $G$. For the Rindler metric, $\sigma^{2}\,\mathrm{tr}(G)=\sigma^{2}(1-(\kappa\ell)^{2})$; the OU approximation is exact at $\kappa\ell=1$ and accurate when $|(\kappa\ell)^{2}-1|\ll 1$.

The $K$-space noise amplitude $\sigma_{K}(x)=2\sigma\|Gx\|$ varies along $\{K=1\}$, yielding a position-dependent temperature:
\begin{equation}
T_{\mathrm{loc}}(x) := \frac{\sigma_{K}(x)^{2}}{2\kappa_{K}(x_{*})} = \frac{\sigma^{2}\,x^{\top}G^{2}x}{2d_{c}\,x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}}.\label{eq:Tloc}
\end{equation}
For $\kappa\ell=1$ and $x(t)=(\sinh t,\cosh t)$: $T_{\mathrm{loc}}(t)=\sigma^{2}\cosh(2t)/(2d_{c})$, which has the structure of Tolman's law.

\begin{remark}[Stability constraint]
For macroscopic black holes, $T_{\mathrm{tol}}\ll d_{c}$; setting $D=T_{\mathrm{tol}}I$ renders $K=1$ unstable. Stability requires $D\ge d_{c}I$; critical damping $D=d_{c}I$ is the unique marginally stable choice.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Gromov bound]\label{rem:gromov}
The Gromov bound~\cite{Gromov} $\sigma_{1}\ge\hbar/2$ gives $\sigma_{\mathrm{Gromov}}^{2}\sim\ell$, while the Clausius constraint requires $\sigma^{2}\sim 1/\ell^{2}$. These scale oppositely: Gromov provides a lower bound insufficient to produce thermal noise at $T_{\mathrm{tol}}$. The Clausius relation remains an independent input.
\end{remark}

\section{Discussion}

The $2\times 2$ equivalence theorem is the minimal case of a structure that may extend in stages.

\noindent\textit{Stage~0: Origin.} Realizability plus positive temporal cost forces $G$ indefinite (Theorem~4, \cite{Realizability}). Conversely, for definite $G$ no nontrivial stability boundary exists (Theorem~3): the framework is self-limiting, producing physical content only when the cost structure is genuinely Lorentzian.

\noindent\textit{Stage~1: Geometry.} $G$ defines a Lorentzian metric with light cones and causal structure. Whether a Lorentzian information geometry --- as opposed to the Riemannian Fisher geometry --- can be made precise remains open.

\noindent\textit{Stage~2: Physics.} At critical damping, the stochastic extension identifies Class~A and Class~B fluctuations. Within Class~A, the Clausius relation follows algebraically and the local noise temperature has the Tolman structure.

\noindent\textit{Stage~3: Quantum.} For $G=\mathrm{diag}(-(\kappa\ell)^{2},1)$, $d_{c}=\alpha/(\kappa\ell)$; defining $\sigma_{1}:=|\det G|^{1/2}/\alpha=\kappa\ell/\alpha$ gives $d_{c}\,\sigma_{1}=1$ identically. Combining with $\sigma_{1}\ge\hbar/2$ gives $d_{c}\le 2/\hbar$ --- an upper bound on~$d_{c}$, and hence on the Tolman temperature $T_{\mathrm{tol}}=d_{c}T_{H}$, from symplectic topology alone. However, the Gromov and Clausius scalings are incompatible (Remark~\ref{rem:gromov}); whether a refinement can close the gap, or is related to the Bekenstein bound~\cite{Bekenstein}, is the question to which the framework points.

\noindent\textit{Beyond GENERIC.} For Lorentzian $G$, $S\neq 0$ departs from GENERIC while $d_{c}$ is controlled by $\det G$ alone, suggesting a Lorentzian extension of non-equilibrium thermodynamics in the spirit of Wiener's programme~\cite{Wiener}.

\medskip

The $2\times 2$ framework is not a truncation of a $(1+3)$-dimensional theory but the natural dimensionality of a single local Rindler horizon: one temporal and one spatial direction normal to the horizon. The Jacobson argument~\cite{Jacobson} reconstructs the Einstein equations by requiring the Clausius relation on \emph{all} local Rindler horizons and invoking $S=A/4$ independently. The present work supplies one essential ingredient --- the algebraic derivation of the Clausius relation on each such horizon --- while the patching across all horizon directions and the entropy--area identification remain the content of the Jacobson argument. The $2\times 2$ setting captures signature, light cones, and symplectic structure; conformal and spinor structure require the $(1{+}3)$-dimensional extension.

The condition $K=1$ has a self-referential character: the metric $G$ defines the quadratic form $K$, and $K=1$ requires that this form reproduce~$G$'s own normalisation. The residual $K-1$ is the metric's own measure of departure from self-consistency; the leading-order restoring potential $V=\tfrac{1}{2}(K-1)^{2}$ is therefore fixed by smoothness alone.

General relativity prescribes Lorentzian signature; the present work derives it from a cost asymmetry and shows that the resulting metric enforces its own normalisation. What remains prescribed is the asymmetry itself: why one direction carries positive cost while others do not. This is Riemann's question in economic form.
