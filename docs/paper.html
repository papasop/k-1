Abstract

Background. Time is usually presupposed. Here it is modeled as information cost: dt_info = dΦ / H. The associated leading quadratic form G is constrained by the realizability axioms R, E, T and non-degeneracy.

Point-level result. In 2D, those conditions force Sig(G) = (1,1), equivalently det G < 0. Under isotropic linearization, Lorentzian signature, real spectrum of J_G, and a positive stability boundary d_c > 0 are equivalent. The unit-cost locus is K(x)=xᵀGx=1.

Dynamics. Within the symplectic–dissipative class GA = αJ − GD, the unique law is ẋ = (J_G − D)∇V; at critical damping and a normalized eigenvector base point, the local restoring rate is κ_K = 4 d_c.

Gravity. Two routes connect to Einstein’s equations: Route A via local stochastic thermodynamics and Jacobson’s argument, requiring external inputs T_eff = T_tol and S ∝ A; Route B gives, in static spherical symmetry, an algebraic equivalence between the field-level K=1 analogue and R_{μν}=0.

§1 · Law I

From information time to Lorentzian type

The first claim is algebraic and local: the cost structure fixes the type of the leading quadratic form. The figure below lets you drag the determinant across its sign change and watch the geometric, spectral, and dynamical reading move together.

Framework

Define information time as dt_info := dΦ/H, where Φ parametrizes structural variation and H > 0 is a local scalar cost per unit variation. The signed principal quadratic form associated with dt_info² is modeled as G ≡ Q, the leading form of the realizability paper.

Axioms R, E, T, together with non-degeneracy, exclude both definite signatures in two dimensions. The only remaining inertia is therefore (1,1), equivalently det G < 0. This does not determine the entries of G; it determines the Lorentzian algebraic type.

Once G is fixed, it defines K(x) = xᵀGx. The additional condition K = 1 is not inherited from the realizability axioms; it is the paper’s own self-consistency condition and becomes dynamically meaningful only after Laws II and III are added.

§2 · Laws II–III

Dynamics and the local restoring law

The theorem-level point is subtle: K=1 is not automatically an attractor. It becomes locally restoring only after the symplectic–dissipative law and critical damping are specified. The figure below keeps the formula, its assumptions, and its failure modes in one place.

Dynamic layer

Law II postulates the Helmholtz-style split GA = αJ − GD with D ≥ 0, giving the unique evolution law ẋ = (J_G − D)∇V. The paper then specializes to V = ½(K−1)² and the critical choice D = d_c I.

At a G-eigenvector base point x* on {K=1}, the skew term drops out and the linearized evolution of K becomes Ḱ = −4 d_c x*ᵀG²x* (K−1) + o(K−1). Under the normalized choice x*ᵀG²x* = 1, this yields κ_K = 4 d_c.

The interface below separates two things that are easy to conflate: the theorem coefficient itself, and the conditions under which one is allowed to interpret it as the genuine local restoring rate rather than a heuristic surrogate.

§3 · Two Routes

One thermodynamic bridge, one algebraic equivalence

The paper’s two routes are complementary, not redundant. Route A is broad but imports the usual thermodynamic input. Route B is sharper but presently limited to the spherical sector. The page below refuses to blur that distinction.

§4 · Proof Map

Theorem 5.2 as an executable equivalence diagram

The field-level theorem in the paper says: for static spherical metrics on intervals where f>0, the pair of scalar conditions K₁=K₂=1 is equivalent to the vacuum Einstein equations. The point of the proof map is not to replace the analytic argument; it is to keep every algebraic gate visible at once.

Theorem scope

The proof has two directions. Forward: from K₁=K₂=1 to vacuum closure. Reverse: from R_{μν}=0 to the two scalar conditions. In spherical symmetry these arrows can be written in a way that is both exact and executable.

But the theorem also has a domain: static spherical gauge, vacuum, and f > 0 so that σ₁ = r√f is real. The map below does not let the user forget those gates. If you switch on a cosmological term or step outside spherical symmetry, the theorem is not contradicted; the system simply leaves the theorem’s domain and moves to another panel or another open problem.

§6 · Audits

Auditing the assumption stack

The paper’s strongest claims are carefully layered. These compact audit cards act like warning labels: they keep higher-level conclusions from being used where only a lower-level statement has been established.

Audit 1
Law III package
The coefficient κ_K=4d_c is theorem-level only under the full local hypothesis package: Lorentzian G, critical damping, eigenvector base point, and local linearization.
Audit 3
H* dependence
Outside isotropic linearization, the zero-eigenvalue locus remains pinned but the full real-part threshold may shift. The page treats d_c as a signature invariant only where the paper says it is.
Audit 5
Field-level scope
Theorem 5.2 is vacuum and spherical. RN and SdS are displayed as disciplined side cases, not as unproved generalizations of the same theorem.
Audit 6
Route claims
Route A recovers Einstein’s equations only with the stated external inputs. Route B is an algebraic equivalence in its own sector, not a new thermodynamic derivation.
§7 · Λ extension

The Schwarzschild–de Sitter extension

The paper extends the vacuum theorem by a separate corollary: K₁ = 1 − 2Λr² and K₂ = 1 − Λr² are equivalent to R_{μν} = Λ g_{μν} in the same static spherical gauge. This is not the vacuum theorem itself; it is a controlled extension.

§8 · RN near-horizon

Reissner–Nordström as a one-leg example

This page deliberately does not present RN as a vacuum branch of the theorem. Instead it is displayed as the canonical example of a profile that keeps K₁=1 but breaks K₂=1, exactly as the paper’s revised asymmetry story says it should.

§9 · Leg asymmetry

The asymmetric rigidity lemma in motion

The updated paper no longer treats the two legs symmetrically. K₂=1 is rigid in the static spherical family: it forces f = 1 + C/r and therefore also K₁=1. By contrast, K₁=1 still leaves a one-parameter non-vacuum freedom.

§10 · Birkhoff ladder

From ODE structure to the Schwarzschild family

The paper’s Birkhoff discussion is not the full GR theorem. What it does show is the ODE ladder inside already static spherical gauge: one scalar condition leaves a two-parameter family, the second kills the extra constant, and the remaining parameter reads naturally as mass.

§11 · Closing

What this page does and does not claim

The page mirrors the paper’s structure and keeps its boundaries visible. It demonstrates algebraic coherence and logical dependencies; it does not replace the analytic proofs or upgrade a limited theorem into a general one by interface design.

Scope statement

This interactive document validates that the formulas displayed in the paper can be kept internally synchronized as a live system. When a parameter leaves the vacuum theorem, the relevant panels say so. When a quantity depends on external input, the page labels it. When a leg is rigid and another is not, the interface separates them rather than averaging them into a slogan.

But no slider proves a theorem. The proofs remain in the paper, and the limits remain real: Route B is still spherical, Route A still imports its thermodynamic identifications, and global questions — especially beyond the local and spherical settings — remain open.
