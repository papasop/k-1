§0 · Abstract

Time as a derived concept. In standard physics, time is an unexplained input. Here it is recast as the cost of change: a scalar Φ paired with a local impedance H gives an information time dtinfo:=dΦ/H.

The symbolic principal quadratic form G associated with dtinfo2, together with realizability axioms R · E · T and non‑degeneracy, forces Sig(G)=(1,1) — Lorentz signature — as an algebraic consequence, not an input.

K(x)=x⊤Gx=1 is then the locus of unit time cost: a self‑consistent trajectory that the induced symplectic‑dissipative dynamics restores toward. From this point, two bridges reach the Einstein equations — one thermodynamic, one algebraic.

§1 · Layer 1

Three axioms, one signature

G is the symbolic principal quadratic form associated with dtinfo2. Three realizability axioms pin down its algebraic type — and exclude every alternative to Lorentz signature.

Axioms

The construction runs in three steps. A smooth scalar Φ on a 2‑dim configuration space is paired with a local impedance H>0. Their ratio dtinfo=dΦ/H has the dimension of time by choice of units. Its symbolic principal quadratic part is what G denotes.

Non‑degeneracy leaves three possible signatures in two dimensions: (2,0), (0,2), or (1,1). The axioms kill the first two.

R.
Realizability
A sequence of forward displacements exists with non‑zero spatial component whose cost‑to‑size ratio vanishes. This is the cost content of the light cone — directions along which motion becomes asymptotically free relative to path length. Excludes positive‑definite G.
E.
Expansion
Displacement cost admits a quadratic leading order. The smoothness of Φ,H motivates this expansion; the form G is the symbolic coefficient of that leading quadratic.
T.
Time‑cost
Cost along the distinguished time direction has a strictly positive lower bound: d(x;(Δt,0))≥ctΔt with ct>0. Excludes negative‑definite G.
“Non‑degenerate G minus positive‑definite minus negative‑definite leaves exactly one inertia class: (1,1). Signature is the residue of axioms R and T.”

§3 · Layer 3

The K=1 locus is locally self‑restoring

A symplectic‑dissipative drift on $\mathbb{R}^{2}$ with potential $V = \tfrac{1}{2}(K-1)^{2}$. Near a normalized $G$-eigenvector base point, deviations from unit time cost decay locally at rate $\kappa_K = 4 d_c$.

Law II

Symplectic–dissipative split. The evolution generator obeys GA=αJ−GD with D≥0 — a Helmholtz/GENERIC‑style decomposition into a reversible symplectic piece JG and an irreversible dissipative piece D. Under this axiomatic split, any smooth potential V yields a uniquely reduced evolution x˙=(JG−D)∇V. Here \($\nabla\$) is the Euclidean gradient on \($\mathbb{R}^{2}\$); it acts on \($V\$), and the metric \($G\$) is encoded in the potential itself, so no metric-compatible gradient is required.

For Lorentz G the symmetric part of JG is non‑zero in the Euclidean sense; the framework departs from standard GENERIC, which assumes Euclidean antisymmetry.

Law III · restoring rate

At critical damping D=dcI, on any G‑eigenvector of {K=1} normalized by x∗⊤G2x∗=1:  K˙=−κK(K−1)+o(K−1), κK=4dc. The factor 4 is 2×2: once from K being quadratic, once from V=12(K−1)2.

§4 · Two bridges

Two routes to the Einstein equations

From K=1 to gravity, via two complementary paths. Route A supplies the thermodynamic input for the Jacobson route once $T_{\mathrm{eff}} = T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ and $S \propto A$ are given. Route B proceeds by direct algebra in the spherical vacuum sector.

A Thermodynamic bridge

external inputs · Teff=Ttol and S∝A
  1. Near {K=1}, the Class‑A fluctuations linearize to a 1‑D Ornstein–Uhlenbeck process.
  2. FDT fixes an effective temperature Teff=σ2/(2dc).
  3. Local Clausius: δQ=TeffdS by OU detailed balance.
  4. Identifying Teff with the Tolman temperature Ttol=ℏ/(2πℓ) and using S∝A, Jacobson's argument returns Gμν=8πTμν.

Reading

Three things fall out when you paint matter into the prototype:

  1. Where ρ=0, K2=1 identically. Vacuum is the trace‑self‑consistent locus; outside any source, the metric slides back onto the Schwarzschild branch by Birkhoff — the exterior is insensitive to how you paint the interior as long as the integral is the same.
  2. K2<1 tracks energy density directly through the exact corollary ρ=(1−K2)/(8πr2). No integration required: the tt‑Einstein equation is a pointwise statement in K‑language.
  3. The cosmological preset gives K2−1=−Λr2, reproducing Schwarzschild–de Sitter with ρΛ=Λ/(8π). The cosmological constant is K2's constant angular deficit.
§6 · Route B

Eigenvalue self‑consistency equals vacuum Einstein

In the spherical vacuum sector, K1=σ22◻ln⁡σ1 and K2=σ22◻ln⁡σ2 give simple ODE content for f(r). When both equal 1, you are looking at Schwarzschild.

Domain

Axiom T forces σ1>0: the Schwarzschild singularity at r=0 sits outside the theory's domain, not inside it as a failure. The interior region r<2M — where f<0 — likewise falls off the real branch.

Under the Tolman identification, the OU variance bound Var(K)<1 yields a soft self‑consistency cutoff at σ1≳ℓP/(4π). This is a semiclassical self‑consistency condition, not an independent hard cutoff.

§7 · Closing

What remains prescribed, and what is open

Four limitations, five open questions. The framework fixes its internal claims sharply — and cleanly exposes what remains assumed.

Limitations
i

2×2 only. Extension to (1+3) dimensions needs an additional symplectic‑pairing choice; dc>0⇔ Lorentz weakens beyond 2D.

ii

Two external inputs. Route A still depends on Teff=Ttol and S∝A.

iii

Spherical vacuum only. Route B's strict algebraic equivalence with Rμν=0 is established only in the spherical vacuum sector. A general lift needs null Gauss–Codazzi.

iv

Two cost layers. The scalar H and the matrix weight H∗ are distinct objects. For general $H_*$, the zero-eigenvalue locus in $d$ is invariant under invertible right multiplication, but the full real-part stability threshold may shift. A common deeper cost principle is left open.

Open questions
  1. Can Teff=Ttol be derived from the KMS condition?
  2. Does quantization give a hard lower bound on σ1, connecting to the Bekenstein bound?
  3. Does Route B extend beyond spherical symmetry?
  4. Is there a canonical symplectic‑pairing rule in (1+3) dimensions?
  5. Is {K=1} a global attractor, not merely local?
“What remains prescribed is the cost asymmetry itself — why one direction carries positive cost while others do not. That is the economical form of Riemann's question.”
