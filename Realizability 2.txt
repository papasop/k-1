\title{\textbf{Realizability and the Origin of Causality}}

\begin{abstract}
Zeno's paradox implicitly assumes that spatial displacements carry irreducible positive
cost at every scale. We negate this: if spatial displacement sequences exist whose cost-to-magnitude ratio vanishes (Assumption~R), while pure temporal displacements retain positive
cost (Assumption~T), then in $(1{+}1)$ dimensions any quadratic leading-order expansion of the
cost function is indefinite---provided the quadratic form is nondegenerate, an explicit
condition that is separately required. After normalisation,
\[
Q = dt^{2} - c_{\max}^{-2}\,dr^{2}.
\]
Here the cost function~$d$ is the primitive object and~$Q$ is derived from the leading-order
behaviour of~$d^{2}$; Lorentzian signature therefore emerges from the asymmetry between
temporal and spatial displacement costs rather than from any geometric postulate. Among
nondegenerate quadratic forms, every non-Lorentzian alternative is excluded: positive-definite by
Assumption~R together with the Lemma, and negative-definite by Assumption~T.
Degenerate forms lie outside this class by hypothesis. In $(1{+}3)$~dimensions, under
Assumptions~R4, E4, T, and nondegeneracy of~$Q$, the only possible signature is
$\mathrm{Sig}(Q)=(1,3)$.

The present derivation differs from the EPS axiomatisation~\cite{EPS} in its starting point: no
particles, light signals, or kinematic objects are presupposed---only a scalar cost function.
This provides a more primitive geometric foundation for the thermodynamic derivation
of Einstein's equations~\cite{Jacobson}, at the logical level of the metric signature itself.
\end{abstract}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Lorentzian signature $\cdot$ Causality $\cdot$ Cost function $\cdot$ Quadratic form $\cdot$ Constructive axiomatics

\section{Introduction}

We show that a Lorentzian causal structure follows from a minimal asymmetry between temporal and spatial displacement costs together with a quadratic leading-order expansion. Together with nondegeneracy of the leading quadratic form~$Q$, this asymmetry forces $Q$ to be indefinite, yielding Lorentzian causal
structure without assuming any metric or \textit{a priori} causal cone. Throughout, the
cost function~$d$ is the primitive object, while the quadratic form~$Q$ is introduced only as the
leading second-order structure extracted from~$d^{2}$. This logical ordering is essential:
Lorentzian signature is a conclusion, not an assumption. The closest precursor is the EPS
axiomatisation~\cite{EPS}; unlike EPS, no particles, light signals, or worldlines appear as primitives ---
only a scalar cost function. The result provides a foundation one step earlier than the Jacobson
derivation~\cite{Jacobson}: Lorentzian signature itself is derived rather than assumed.

The title warrants a brief clarification. By \textit{origin of causality} we mean a logical rather than
a physical origin: a minimal set of conditions on a cost function from which Lorentzian causal
structure follows as the unique algebraic consequence, with no other signature possible. In $(1{+}1)$ dimensions, the
present paper identifies this set as Assumptions~R, E, T, together with nondegeneracy of~$Q$. The physical origin of the cost
asymmetry itself lies outside the scope of the present paper.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Main Theorem (informal).}
Under Assumptions~R, E, T, and nondegeneracy of~$Q$, the leading
quadratic form~$Q$ is Lorentzian in $(1{+}1)$ dimensions:
\[
Q = dt^{2} - c_{\max}^{-2}\,dr^{2}, \qquad c_{\max}>0,
\]
up to normalisation and an invertible linear change of variables. In $(1{+}3)$ dimensions, under
R4, E4, T, and nondegeneracy of~$Q$, the only possible signature is $\mathrm{Sig}(Q)=(1,3)$.

The precise setup, statements, and proofs are given in Sections~2--4.

\section{Setup}

Let $U\subset\mathbb{R}^{2}$ be open with chart $(t,r)$, and fix $x\in U$. For small $\delta x=(\delta t,\delta r)$ with $\delta t>0$, let
$d(x;\delta x)\ge 0$ be a displacement cost at~$x$. The norm $\|\cdot\|$ denotes any norm on~$\mathbb{R}^{2}$; all conclusions
are independent of the choice. Write $S^{1}=\{u\in\mathbb{R}^{2}:\|u\|=1\}$. The chart variables $t$ and~$r$ label a distinguished direction (carrying
positive cost, Assumption~T) and its complement; no metric or causal structure on~$U$ is assumed
at this stage.

\begin{definition}[R --- Zero-threshold realizability]\label{def:R}
There exists a sequence $\delta x_{n}=(\delta t_{n},\delta r_{n})\to 0$
with $\delta t_{n}>0$, $\delta r_{n}\neq 0$ such that
\[
\frac{d(x;\delta x_{n})}{\|\delta x_{n}\|}\longrightarrow 0,
\]
and there exists a subsequence $v_{n_{k}}\to v=(v_{t},v_{r})$ with $v_{t}>0$ and $v_{r}\neq 0$, where $v_{n_{k}}:=\delta x_{n_{k}}/\|\delta x_{n_{k}}\|$.
\end{definition}

The condition $\delta t_{n}>0$ restricts attention to forward-in-time displacements.
Requiring the same condition for $\delta t_{n}<0$ would strengthen the assumption without
changing the conclusion: the proof uses only one null direction, which the
single-sided condition already provides.

Assumption~E formalises the leading-order structure of the cost function within which
Assumption~R operates. Crucially, $Q$ is not introduced as an independent geometric object,
but as the quadratic coefficient structure extracted from~$d^{2}$.

\begin{definition}[E --- Quadratic expansion]\label{def:E}
Suppose $d^{2}$ admits a quadratic leading-order expansion at~$x$: there exists a quadratic
form $Q(\delta t,\delta r)=a\,\delta t^{2}+2\kappa\,\delta t\,\delta r+b\,\delta r^{2}$, determined by
the second-order behaviour of~$d^{2}$, such that
\[
d(x;\delta x)^{2} = \bigl(Q(\delta x)\bigr)_{+} + o\!\left(\|\delta x\|^{2}\right)
\quad\text{as } \delta x\to 0 \text{ with } \delta t>0,
\]
where $(u)_{+}:=\max\{u,0\}$.
\end{definition}

The notation $\mathrm{Sig}(Q)=(p,q)$ denotes the inertia of~$Q$: $p$ positive and $q$ negative eigenvalues.

\begin{definition}[T --- Temporal cost]\label{def:T}
There exists $c_{t}>0$ such that for all sufficiently small $\Delta t>0$,
\[
d\bigl(x;(\Delta t,0)\bigr)\ge c_{t}\,\Delta t.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Primitive status of $d$ and meaning of $(Q)_{+}$]
The logical structure of the paper is that $d$ is primitive and $Q$ is derived. Assumptions~R and~T constrain~$d$
directly, while Assumption~E extracts from~$d^{2}$ a quadratic leading-order form~$Q$.
This is what makes the Lorentzian signature theorem nontrivial: the geometry is not assumed in advance.

The truncation $(Q)_{+}$ is not a repair inserted merely because $d^{2}\ge 0$. Rather, it records the fact that
only positive quadratic contributions of~$Q$ appear in the leading behaviour of~$d^{2}$.
For a sequence $\delta x_{n}\to 0$ whose normalized directions converge to a vector~$v$ with $Q(v)\le 0$,
we have $d(x;\delta x_{n})/\|\delta x_{n}\|\to 0$ (by Lemma~\ref{lem:null} below), so $d^{2}$ is of lower than quadratic order along such sequences.

The physical identification of~$d$---whether as an information-theoretic cost, a thermodynamic work function, or otherwise---lies outside the present scope; only the three structural properties R, E, and~T enter the derivation.
\end{remark}

\section{Main result in $(1{+}1)$ dimensions}

\begin{lemma}[Null--Sign equivalence]\label{lem:null}
Assume~E. If $\delta x_{n}\to 0$ with $\delta t_{n}>0$, $d(x;\delta x_{n})/\|\delta x_{n}\|\to 0$,
and a subsequence of $v_{n}:=\delta x_{n}/\|\delta x_{n}\|$ converges to $v\in S^{1}$, then $Q(v)\le 0$. Conversely, if
$Q(v)\le 0$ and $\delta x_{n}\to 0$ with $\delta x_{n}/\|\delta x_{n}\|\to v$, then $d(x;\delta x_{n})/\|\delta x_{n}\|\to 0$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
\textit{Forward:} By~E, $(d(x;\delta x_{n_{k}})/\|\delta x_{n_{k}}\|)^{2}=(Q(v_{n_{k}}))_{+}+o(1)\to 0$, so $(Q(v))_{+}=0$.

\textit{Converse:} Let $v_{n}:=\delta x_{n}/\|\delta x_{n}\|$, so $v_{n}\to v$. Since $Q$ is continuous and $Q(v)\le 0$, we have
$(Q(v_{n}))_{+}\to 0$. By homogeneity of~$Q$,
\[
\bigl(Q(\delta x_{n})\bigr)_{+} = \|\delta x_{n}\|^{2}\bigl(Q(v_{n})\bigr)_{+} = o\!\left(\|\delta x_{n}\|^{2}\right).
\]
Hence Assumption~E gives $d(x;\delta x_{n})^{2}=o(\|\delta x_{n}\|^{2})$, so $d(x;\delta x_{n})/\|\delta x_{n}\|\to 0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Lorentzian signature from realizability]\label{thm:lorentz}
Assume R, E, T, and that $Q$ is nondegenerate. Then $Q$ is indefinite, and after
rescaling~$t$ so that $Q(1,0)=1$ and applying an invertible
linear change of variables,
\[
Q = dt^{2} - c_{\max}^{-2}\,dr^{2}
\]
for some $c_{\max}>0$. The null set consists of exactly two directions $dr/dt=\pm c_{\max}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\textbf{Step~1 (Null direction from~R).}
By~R, there exists a subsequence $v_{n_{k}}\to v\in S^{1}$ with
$v_{t}>0$, $v_{r}\neq 0$. By Lemma~\ref{lem:null}, $Q(v)\le 0$.

\textbf{Step~2 (Temporal direction positive).}
If $Q(1,0)\le 0$, then by homogeneity
\[
Q(\Delta t,0)=\Delta t^{2}Q(1,0),
\]
hence
\[
\bigl(Q(\Delta t,0)\bigr)_{+}=\Delta t^{2}\bigl(Q(1,0)\bigr)_{+}=0.
\]
Assumption~E therefore gives
\[
d(x;(\Delta t,0))^{2}=o(\Delta t^{2}),
\]
so $d(x;(\Delta t,0))=o(\Delta t)$, contradicting~T. Therefore $Q(1,0)>0$.

\textbf{Step~3 (Null vector by IVT).}
Define
\[
\gamma(s):=\frac{(1-s)(1,0)+s\,v}{\|(1-s)(1,0)+s\,v\|}, \qquad s\in[0,1].
\]
Since $v_{r}\neq 0$ (Assumption~R), the denominator is nonzero for all $s\in[0,1]$, so $\gamma$ is well-defined.
Since $v_{t}>0$, the numerator has strictly positive $t$-component throughout, so $\gamma$ is a continuous
path in $S^{1}\cap\{u_{t}>0\}$. Then $Q(\gamma(0))=Q(1,0)>0$ and $Q(\gamma(1))=Q(v)\le 0$, so by the
intermediate value theorem there exists~$s^{*}$ with $Q(\gamma(s^{*}))=0$. Set $w:=\gamma(s^{*})$; then $w_{t}>0$. Since $Q(w)=0$ but $Q(1,0)>0$, $w\neq(1,0)$, so $w_{r}\neq 0$.

\textbf{Step~4 (Signature and normalisation).}
By Step~2, $Q$ takes a positive value; by Step~3,
$Q$ vanishes on a nonzero vector. Since $Q$ is nondegenerate,
a nondegenerate quadratic form on~$\mathbb{R}^{2}$ with a nonzero null
vector cannot be definite; hence it is indefinite, and in two dimensions the signature is $(1,1)$.
Rescaling~$t$ so that $Q(1,0)=1$ and applying an invertible linear change of variables gives
\[
Q = dt^{2} - c_{\max}^{-2}\,dr^{2}.\qedhere
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[The speed $c_{\max}$]
The constant $c_{\max}$ is not an independent postulate but arises from the cost structure. It represents the maximal propagation speed, determined by the relative
coefficients of~$Q$. $c_{\max}$ may vary from point to point; the present result is local. A
global constant $c_{\max}$ would follow from additional homogeneity assumptions on the cost function,
which are not required here.
\end{remark}

\section{Extension to $(1{+}3)$ dimensions}

The two-dimensional case of Theorem~\ref{thm:lorentz} is the minimal realisation---a single spatial direction suffices. Extending to physical spacetime (three spatial dimensions) requires two additional scope conditions stated explicitly before the four-dimensional extension.
First, the paper takes the existence of three spatial directions as given; it does not explain why physical spacetime has exactly three spatial dimensions.
Second, the three realizable limiting directions are required not merely to exist individually, but to span a three-dimensional subspace on which $Q$ is negative semidefinite. This is an additional coherence condition on the spatial sector.

With this understood, let $U\subset\mathbb{R}^{4}$ be open, fix $x\in U$,
and consider the chart $(t,x^{1},x^{2},x^{3})$; write $e_{t}=(1,0,0,0)$ for the temporal
basis vector.

\begin{definition}[R4 --- Three-directional zero-threshold realizability]\label{def:R4}
For each $i\in\{1,2,3\}$ there
exists a sequence $\delta x_{n}^{(i)}\to 0$ with $\delta t_{n}^{(i)}>0$ and $\delta x_{n}^{(i),i}\neq 0$, such that $d(x;\delta x_{n}^{(i)})/\|\delta x_{n}^{(i)}\|\to 0$, and
a subsequence of normalised directions converges to $v^{(i)}$ with $v_{t}^{(i)}>0$ and $v_{i}^{(i)}\neq 0$. The three
limiting directions $\{v^{(1)},v^{(2)},v^{(3)}\}$ are required to span a three-dimensional subspace~$W$, and
the restriction $Q|_{W}$ is required to be negative semidefinite:
\[
Q(w)\le 0 \quad\text{for all } w\in W.
\]
\end{definition}

Here $\delta x_{n}^{(i),i}$ denotes the $i$-th spatial component of the
displacement~$\delta x_{n}^{(i)}$.

\begin{definition}[E4 --- Quadratic expansion in 4D]\label{def:E4}
$d^{2}$ admits a quadratic leading-order expansion at~$x$: there exists a quadratic
form $Q$ on~$\mathbb{R}^{4}$, determined by the second-order behaviour of~$d^{2}$, such that
\[
d(x;\delta x)^{2} = \bigl(Q(\delta x)\bigr)_{+} + o\!\left(\|\delta x\|^{2}\right)
\quad\text{as } \delta x\to 0 \text{ with } \delta t>0.
\]
\end{definition}

Assumption~T carries over unchanged.

\begin{theorem}[Lorentzian signature --- $(1{+}3)$D]\label{thm:4d}
Assume R4, E4, and~T, and suppose that $Q$ is
nondegenerate. Then $\mathrm{Sig}(Q)=(1,3)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\textbf{Step~1 (Temporal direction positive).}
If $Q(e_{t})\le 0$, then by homogeneity $Q(\Delta t,0,0,0)=\Delta t^{2}Q(e_{t})\le 0$,
hence $(Q(\Delta t,0,0,0))_{+}=0$. Assumption~E4 therefore gives
$d(x;(\Delta t,0,0,0))^{2}=o(\Delta t^{2})$, so $d(x;(\Delta t,0,0,0))=o(\Delta t)$,
contradicting~T. Therefore $Q(e_{t})>0$, and the positive inertia index of~$Q$
is at least~1.

\textbf{Step~2 (Negative-semidefinite spatial subspace).}
By Definition~\ref{def:R4}, the limiting directions
$\{v^{(1)},v^{(2)},v^{(3)}\}$ span a three-dimensional subspace $W\subset\mathbb{R}^{4}$ on which
\[
Q(w)\le 0 \quad\text{for all } w\in W.
\]
Let $V_{+}\subset\mathbb{R}^{4}$ be a maximal positive-definite subspace of~$Q$, so
$\dim V_{+}=p$. For $w\in W\cap V_{+}$, $Q(w)\le 0$ since $w\in W$, while
$Q(w)>0$ for every nonzero $w\in V_{+}$ because $V_{+}$ is positive definite.
Hence no nonzero vector can lie in $W\cap V_{+}$, so $W\cap V_{+}=\{0\}$.
Therefore
\[
\dim W+\dim V_{+}=\dim(W+V_{+})+\dim(W\cap V_{+})\le 4,
\]
so $3+p\le 4$, hence $p\le 1$. Therefore the negative inertia index satisfies $q=4-p\ge 3$.

\textbf{Step~3 (Inertia count).}
Since $Q$ is nondegenerate on~$\mathbb{R}^{4}$, the positive and negative inertia
indices sum to~4. The positive index is at least~1 (Step~1) and the negative index is at least~3
(Step~2); hence the only possibility is $\mathrm{Sig}(Q)=(1,3)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Nondegeneracy]
Both Theorem~\ref{thm:lorentz} and Theorem~\ref{thm:4d} take nondegeneracy
of~$Q$ as an explicit hypothesis. In $(1{+}1)$ dimensions, ruling out degeneracy
from Assumptions~R, E, T alone is not possible. Consider
$Q=(dt-\lambda\,dr)^{2}$ with $\lambda\neq 0$, realised by the concrete cost
function $d(x;\delta x):=\sqrt{(Q(\delta x))_{+}}$. Assumption~E holds with
this $Q$ as an exact identity (no $o$-term needed). Assumption~T holds
because $Q(e_{t})=1$ gives $d(x;(\Delta t,0))=\Delta t$. Assumption~R
holds because the kernel of $Q$ is spanned by $(\lambda,1)$. Choose the
sign so that the $t$-component is positive, call this kernel vector $u$,
and set $\delta x_{n}:=\varepsilon_{n}u$ with $\varepsilon_{n}\downarrow 0$.
Then $\delta x_{n}\to 0$, $\delta x_{n}/\|\delta x_{n}\|\to u/\|u\|$ (a
direction with positive $t$-component), and $Q(\delta x_{n})=\varepsilon_{n}^{2}Q(u)=0$,
so by the construction of $d$,
\[
d(x;\delta x_{n})=\sqrt{(Q(\delta x_{n}))_{+}}=0
\]
for every $n$. Hence $d(x;\delta x_{n})/\|\delta x_{n}\|=0\to 0$, and the
sequence $(\delta x_{n})$ realises R.

A tempting argument would be that degeneracy together with $Q(e_{t})>0$
should imply, in suitable coordinates, that $Q=a\,dt^{2}$, forcing any
null vector to have vanishing $t$-component. This is not valid, however,
because the reduction $Q=a\,dt^{2}$ holds only when the kernel vector has
vanishing $t$-component; the example above shows that the kernel $(\lambda,1)$
has $t$-component $\lambda\neq 0$. Nondegeneracy is therefore a genuinely
independent condition, not derivable from R, E, T.

In four dimensions, ruling out degeneracy from Assumptions~R4, E4, T alone
requires additional input (for example, realizability along a fourth
independent direction). The nondegeneracy hypothesis is therefore an explicit
scope condition in both theorems, not a hidden assumption.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Exclusion of non-Lorentzian signatures]
Under the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:lorentz} in $(1{+}1)$ dimensions, and of Theorem~\ref{thm:4d} in $(1{+}3)$ dimensions, every non-Lorentzian signature is excluded. In $(1{+}1)$ dimensions:
\begin{itemize}
\item[(i)] Positive-definite signature $\mathrm{Sig}(Q)=(2,0)$ is excluded by Assumption~R together with
Lemma~\ref{lem:null}: it yields a nonzero limiting direction~$v$ with $v_{t}>0$ and $Q(v)\le 0$, which is
impossible for a positive-definite form.
\item[(ii)] Negative-definite signature $\mathrm{Sig}(Q)=(0,2)$ is excluded by Assumption~T: negative definiteness gives $Q(1,0)<0$, hence $(Q(\Delta t,0))_{+}=0$ for all sufficiently small $\Delta t>0$; Assumption~E therefore yields $d(x;(\Delta t,0))^{2}=o(\Delta t^{2})$, so $d(x;(\Delta t,0))=o(\Delta t)$, contradicting~T.
\item[(iii)] Degenerate forms are excluded by the nondegeneracy hypothesis of Theorem~\ref{thm:lorentz}: under that hypothesis,
the existence of a nonzero null vector rules out definiteness; hence the form must be indefinite.
\end{itemize}
In $(1{+}3)$ dimensions:
\begin{itemize}
\item[(iv)] The split signature $(2,2)$ is excluded by the negative-semidefinite subspace condition of~R4, which forces the negative inertia index of~$Q$ to be at least~3.
\end{itemize}
The remaining non-Lorentzian signatures are excluded by the inertia count in the proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:4d}. Lorentzian signature is therefore not merely compatible with the cost asymmetry; it is the unique outcome.
\end{remark}
