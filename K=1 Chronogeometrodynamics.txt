\begin{abstract}
\textit{Background.} Time has invariably entered physics as a primitive --- an underived input.

\textit{Framework.} Redefining time as the cost of change, and identifying the signed leading quadratic form $G$ associated with $dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2}$ with that of~\cite{Realizability}, the three realizability conditions (Axioms~R, E, T) together with nondegeneracy force $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$, equivalently $\det G<0$. Under isotropic linearisation, the sign $\det G<0$ has three equivalent expressions: Lorentzian signature, real symplectic eigenvalues, and $d_{c}>0$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:equivalence}); $K(x)=x^{\top}Gx=1$ is the unit-cost locus introduced as the additional self-consistency condition of the present paper. The evolution law, subject to the $G$-induced symplectic structure and $D\ge 0$, is unique (Theorem~\ref{thm:lawII}); at critical damping, the restoring rate is $\kappa_{K}=4d_{c}$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:restoring}).

\textit{Thermodynamic content.} In the stationary regime near $K=1$, the Clausius relation uniquely fixes the noise amplitude in terms of the Tolman temperature and the stability boundary $d_{c}$ (Proposition~\ref{prop:clausius}, eq.~\eqref{eq:noise}). Geometrically, the $K=1$ manifold carries two classes of fluctuation: thermodynamic (Class~A, normal to $K=1$) and kinematic (Class~B, tangential, along Lorentz boost orbits). Within the stochastic-to-Clausius step, the sole external identification is $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$; Route~B (\S~\ref{sec:field}) requires none.

\textit{Gravitational content.} For static, spherically symmetric metrics with symplectic eigenvalues $\sigma_1:=r\sqrt{f}$ and $\sigma_2:=r$, the conjectured field-level analogue $\sigma_2^2\,\Box\ln\sigma_i=1$ ($i=1,2$) is algebraically equivalent to $R_{\mu\nu}=0$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:field}). Axiom~T (positive temporal cost) forces $\sigma_1>0$ (Proposition~\ref{prop:sigma1}), excluding curvature singularities (where $\sigma_1$ degenerates) from the theory's domain; the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck variance bound $\mathrm{Var}(K)<1$ yields an intrinsic resolution scale $\sigma_1\gtrsim\ell_P$ (Proposition~\ref{prop:domain}).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction: time as consequence}

\textit{The problem.} Every standard formulation of spacetime physics assumes a metric or a causal structure as input. The Jacobson argument~\cite{Jacobson} obtains the Einstein equations from thermodynamics on local Rindler horizons, but Lorentzian signature is universally assumed --- as in the approaches of Padmanabhan~\cite{Padmanabhan} and Verlinde~\cite{Verlinde}. The EPS axiomatisation~\cite{EPS} derives causal structure from free-fall and light propagation, but uses kinematic primitives. In every approach to Riemann's open problem --- what determines the metric? --- either the signature is prescribed or the derivation presupposes kinematic primitives.

\textit{The gap.} No existing cost-based approach appears to derive Lorentzian signature itself in this sense.\footnote{Related but distinct questions include Born--Infeld signature issues, signature change in quantum cosmology, and the problem of time~\cite{Gibbons1995,Kiefer2012,Isham1993}. Entropic gravity approaches~\cite{Verlinde,Padmanabhan} presuppose Lorentzian spacetime and derive gravitational dynamics; the present work addresses the prior cost-based signature question.}

\textit{The solution.} Here time is neither coordinate nor metric primitive: it is the cost of change, so the metric --- including its Lorentzian signature --- is a consequence. This resolves the question in the $2\times 2$ setting dictated by a single local Rindler horizon. Information time, defined as $dt_{\mathrm{info}}:=d\Phi/H$, yields a signed leading quadratic form $G$ associated with $dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2}$ (denoted heuristically by $G=\nabla^{2}(dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2})$, identified with the leading quadratic form of~\cite{Realizability}); the three realizability axioms~R, E, T together with nondegeneracy force $\det G<0$ (\S\ref{sec:lawI}). Lorentzian signature, real symplectic eigenvalues, and $d_{c}>0$ are then algebraic consequences of a single sign. The quadratic form $K(x)=x^{\top}Gx$ quantifies local temporal cost; departures from $K{=}1$ generate a local restoring drift near suitable base points (Law~III), making $K=1$ a locally self-consistent locus.

\textit{Two routes to Einstein.}
\begin{itemize}
\item[\textbf{A.}] \emph{Thermodynamic bridge} (\S\S2--7): $K{=}1\xrightarrow{\mathrm{lin.}}\mathrm{OU}\to\mathrm{Clausius}$; combined with $S\propto A$ and the Jacobson argument~\cite{Jacobson}, this yields $G_{\mu\nu}=8\pi T_{\mu\nu}$ for general metrics.
\item[\textbf{B.}] \emph{Equivalence theorem} (\S~\ref{sec:field}): in the spherically symmetric sector, the eigenvalue self-consistency condition $\sigma_2^2\Box\ln\sigma_i=1$ --- a motivated field-level analogue of $K{=}1$, introduced as a conjecture --- is algebraically equivalent to $R_{\mu\nu}=0$, with \emph{no thermodynamic input}. This is not a derivation of the Einstein equations from the framework axioms, but a new equivalent formulation of the vacuum equations in the language of the $K{=}1$ framework.
\end{itemize}
Route~A is general but requires $S\propto A$; Route~B is thermodynamically independent but currently limited to spherical symmetry. \textit{Main results:} under isotropic linearisation, $d_{c}>0\Leftrightarrow\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:equivalence}) and $\sigma_2^2\,\Box\ln\sigma_i=1\Leftrightarrow R_{\mu\nu}=0$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:field}); full statements follow in \S\S\ref{sec:lawII}--\ref{sec:singularity}.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Notation.} $\mathrm{spec}(A)$: spectrum of $A$. $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(p,q)$: inertia ($p$ positive, $q$ negative eigenvalues). $J=\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}0&1\\-1&0\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$: canonical symplectic matrix. $\|\cdot\|$: Euclidean norm on~$\mathbb{R}^{2}$. $\nabla$: Euclidean gradient on~$\mathbb{R}^{2}$. $\alpha>0$: normalisation constant ($\alpha=1$ when compared with thermal quantities). $\kappa$: surface gravity of the horizon (distinct from the restoring rate $\kappa_{K}$). $\ell$: proper distance from the horizon. $\sigma>0$: noise amplitude in the stochastic dynamics; $\sigma_{1}:=r\sqrt{f}$, $\sigma_{2}:=r$: symplectic eigenvalues of the metric. $D\ge 0$: $2\times 2$ positive-semidefinite dissipation matrix. $\Box\phi := r^{-2}\partial_{r}(r^{2}f\,\partial_{r}\phi)$: \emph{radial} d'Alembertian, exact for $r$-dependent $\phi$ (not the full $4$-dimensional wave operator).

\section{Metric from information time (Law~I)}\label{sec:lawI}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Law~I.} \textit{The metric $G$ is a non-degenerate symmetric bilinear form measuring local displacement cost \textup{(in the sense of~\cite{Realizability})}, where the temporal direction is that of positive cost \textup{(Axiom~T of~\cite{Realizability})}.}

\medskip

The information time $dt_{\mathrm{info}}:=d\Phi/H$, where $\Phi$ parametrises structural variation and $H>0$ is the local cost per unit variation of $\Phi$ (an entropic resistance), provides a signed leading quadratic form $G$ associated with $dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2}$ (denoted heuristically by $G:=\nabla^{2}(dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2})$): the local quadratic approximation $dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2}\approx \delta x^{\top}G\,\delta x$. The quadratic form $K(x):=x^{\top}Gx$ quantifies local temporal cost, and $K=1$ identifies the self-consistent locus. This redefinition of time enters the theorems through $G$ alone: $dt_{\mathrm{info}}$ serves as the motivating construction for~$G$ (the leading-order form $Q$ of~\cite{Realizability}).

\medskip\noindent\textit{Bridge to the Realizability framework.} The notation $G:=\nabla^{2}(dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2})$ is used as a shorthand: strictly, $G$ denotes the \emph{signed leading quadratic coefficient form} associated with $dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2}$, identified with the leading quadratic form $Q$ of~\cite{Realizability}. The nonnegative information-time cost itself is represented to leading order by the positive part $(x^{\top}Gx)_{+}$, as in~\cite{Realizability}; $G$ is the underlying signed coefficient form, which may take both signs even though $dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2}\ge 0$. Hence the main theorem of~\cite{Realizability} applies directly: Axioms~R, E, T together with nondegeneracy imply $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$, equivalently $\det G<0$. The condition $K=x^{\top}Gx=1$ is not a conclusion of~\cite{Realizability}, but the additional self-consistency condition introduced in the present paper.

\medskip

The specific form of~$\Phi$ does not affect the algebraic theorems once the induced cost structure satisfies Axioms~R, E, T and nondegeneracy: what matters is that the cost structure satisfies the three axioms, which forces $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$ regardless of~$\Phi$. This is an \emph{argument by type} --- the theorems constrain the algebraic type of~$G$ (its signature), not its specific values.

The cost structure associated with $dt_{\mathrm{info}}$ is required to satisfy Axioms~R, E, T as follows. Axiom~E (quadratic expansion in $\delta x$) is imposed by requiring that the information-time cost admit the quadratic leading-order expansion of~\cite{Realizability}; smoothness of $\Phi$ and $H$ motivates the existence of such a leading quadratic coefficient form. Axiom~T (positive temporal cost) is imposed as the requirement that the displacement cost (denoted $d$, in the sense of~\cite{Realizability}) has a positive linear lower bound along the distinguished temporal direction: $d(x;(\Delta t,0))\ge c_{t}\,\Delta t$ for some $c_{t}>0$. Axiom~R requires forward displacement sequences with nonzero spatial component whose cost-to-magnitude ratio tends to zero. A complete derivation of $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$ from Axioms~R, E, T together with nondegeneracy is given in~\cite{Realizability}.

Lorentzian signature therefore emerges from the cost structure: $G$ is indefinite, with light cones (the null set $\{x:x^{\top}Gx=0\}$) and a causal structure determined by the sign of~$K$.

In general relativity, the Lorentzian metric is prescribed on a pre-existing differentiable manifold; here $G$ and its causal structure are derived.

\section{Symplectic structure from $G$}\label{sec:symplectic}

The requirement that the Hamiltonian flow preserve the cost structure, together with a Euclidean normalisation condition, singles out a unique symplectic generator.

\begin{theorem}[Structural Uniqueness]\label{thm:unique}
Let $G$ be a non-degenerate symmetric $2\times 2$ real matrix. Among all matrices $M$ such that
\textup{(i)}~$GM$ is skew-symmetric and \textup{(ii)}~$M=\alpha J$ when $G=I$ \textup{(Euclidean normalisation)},
the unique solution is $J_{G}=\alpha G^{-1}J$.
\end{theorem}

Here $\alpha>0$ is a normalisation constant fixed by condition~(ii); all results scale with $\alpha$, and $\alpha=1$ is adopted wherever $d_{c}$ is compared with thermal quantities. Condition~(ii) is the Euclidean normalisation: when $G=I$, the framework reduces to standard Hamiltonian mechanics.

\begin{proof}
Since $\dim(\mathrm{Skew}_{2})=1$ and $J$ spans $\mathrm{Skew}_{2}$, condition~(i) forces $GM=\beta J$ for some $\beta\in\mathbb{R}$, hence $M=\beta G^{-1}J$; condition~(ii) fixes $\beta=\alpha$.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Non-degeneracy vs.\ signature]
Theorem~\ref{thm:unique} requires only $\det G\neq 0$; it holds for any signature. Theorem~\ref{thm:spectral} provides the spectral computation; the Lorentzian condition becomes a stability criterion in Theorem~\ref{thm:equivalence}, under isotropic linearisation. Since $GJ_{G}=\alpha J$ is skew-symmetric, $J_{G}$ is $G$-antisymmetric by construction; the Lorentzian condition forces $J_{G}$ to be a hyperbolic element (with real eigenvalues), whose real eigenvalues yield $d_{c}>0$.
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}[Spectral Reality]\label{thm:spectral}
Let $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$. The characteristic polynomial of $G^{-1}J$ is $\lambda^{2}+1/\det G=0$. Since $\det G<0$, the roots are real: $\lambda=\pm\sqrt{-1/\det G}$. The stability boundary is
\begin{equation}
d_{c}(\alpha)=\alpha\sqrt{-1/\det G}>0.\label{eq:dc}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$\mathrm{tr}(G^{-1}J)=0$ and $\det(G^{-1}J)=1/\det G$; the characteristic polynomial $\lambda^{2}+1/\det G=0$ then gives $\mathrm{spec}(J_{G})=\{\pm d_{c}\}$. For the isotropic case $H_{*}=I$ (the linearisation of $H$ at the base point; see \S\ref{sec:stability}), $\max\mathrm{Re}\,\mathrm{spec}(J_{G}-dI)=d_{c}-d$, which vanishes at $d=d_{c}$.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Necessity of Lorentzian Signature]\label{thm:necessity}
If $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(2,0)$ or $(0,2)$, then $\det G>0$, so $\lambda^{2}+1/\det G=0$ has purely imaginary roots and $d_{c}=0$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$\det G>0$ gives $1/\det G>0$, so $\lambda^{2}=-1/\det G<0$ and both roots are purely imaginary.\qed
\end{proof}

\section{The stability boundary}\label{sec:stability}

\begin{theorem}[Signature-Stability Equivalence]\label{thm:equivalence}
For non-degenerate symmetric $2\times 2$ real $G$, under the isotropic linearisation $H_{*}=I$, the following are equivalent:
\begin{itemize}
\item[\textup{(a)}] The metric admits a stability boundary $d_{c}>0$.
\item[\textup{(b)}] $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$ \textup{(Lorentzian)}, equivalently $\det G<0$.
\end{itemize}
The equivalence fails for general $H_{*}$ \textup{(Remark~\ref{rem:Hstar_dep})}: $d_{c}$ is a signature invariant only within the isotropic class.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$d_{c}>0 \Leftrightarrow -1/\det G>0$ (eq.~\eqref{eq:dc}) $\Leftrightarrow \det G<0 \Leftrightarrow \mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$, via Theorems~\ref{thm:spectral} and~\ref{thm:necessity}.\qed
\end{proof}

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Scope.} The closed-form $d_{c}=\alpha\sqrt{-1/\det G}$ relies on $\dim(\mathrm{Skew}_{2})=1$; higher-dimensional extensions require additional structure and are not fully developed in the present paper.

\begin{remark}[Isotropic scaling]
$H_{*}$ denotes the cost-function weight matrix in $d(\delta x)/dt=(J_{G}-dI)H_{*}\,\delta x$ (see \S\ref{sec:lawII}); $H_{*}=I$ is the default. For $H_{*}=cI$ ($c>0$), $d_{c}$ is invariant. For general $H_{*}>0$, the threshold may shift.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Dependence on $H_{*}$]\label{rem:Hstar_dep}
With $G=\mathrm{diag}(0.1,-10)$ and $H_{*}=\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}1&0.9\\0.9&1\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$, $d_{c}^{\mathrm{iso}}=1$ while $d_{c}(H_{*})=4.55$, confirming that $d_{c}$ may depend on $H_{*}$ outside the isotropic class.
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}[Diagonal $H_{*}$ invariance]\label{prop:diag_inv}
For any non-degenerate diagonal $G$ and any diagonal $H_{*}$,
$\mathrm{tr}(J_{G}H_{*})=0$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$(G^{-1}J)_{ii}=(G^{-1})_{ii}J_{ii}=0$, so
$\mathrm{tr}(J_{G}H_{*})=\alpha\sum_{i}(G^{-1}J)_{ii}(H_{*})_{ii}=0$.\qed
\end{proof}
\begin{remark}
For the Rindler metric with diagonal $H_{*}>0$, the zero-eigenvalue threshold of $(J_{G}-dI)H_{*}$ remains $d_{c}=\alpha/(\kappa\ell)$. The thermodynamic applications of \S~\ref{sec:thermo} use this threshold directly, without requiring the full isotropic equivalence of Theorem~\ref{thm:equivalence}.
\end{remark}

\section{Unique dynamics (Law~II)}\label{sec:lawII}

\noindent\textbf{Law~II.} \textit{The generator of the dynamics splits as $GA = \alpha J - GD$ with $D\ge 0$ (positive-semidefinite dissipation). This symplectic-dissipative decomposition is the content of Law~II; it is a structural axiom, not derived from Laws~I or~III, motivated by the universal Helmholtz-type splitting of physical evolution into reversible (symplectic) and irreversible (dissipative) parts~\cite{GENERIC}. Under this axiom, the unique evolution law for any smooth potential $V$ is $\dot{x}=(J_{G}-D)\nabla V$. The local restoring role of $\{K=1\}$ is specified separately by Law~III.}

The splitting $GA=\alpha J-GD$ is the axiomatic content of Law~II; Theorem~\ref{thm:lawII} derives the explicit evolution law from it.

\begin{theorem}[Canonical Form of Law~II]\label{thm:lawII}
Let $G$ be non-degenerate and let $J_{G}=\alpha G^{-1}J$ \textup{(Theorem~\ref{thm:unique})}. Within the class of generators satisfying the Law~II constraint $GA = \alpha J - GD$ with $D \ge 0$, the evolution law for any smooth potential $V$ is uniquely
$\dot{x} = (J_{G}-D)\,\nabla V$. This class defines the physically admissible evolution laws in the present framework, in the spirit of the Helmholtz/GENERIC splitting~\cite{GENERIC}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$A=G^{-1}(\alpha J-GD)=J_{G}-D$.\qed
\end{proof}

For Lorentzian $G$, $J_G$ has symmetric part $S=(J_{G}+J_{G}^{\top})/2\neq 0$, departing from the GENERIC requirement of Euclidean antisymmetry; see \S~\ref{sec:discussion} (\textit{Departure from GENERIC}).

\section{Critical damping and fluctuation structure (Law~III)}\label{sec:lawIII}

\noindent\textbf{Law~III.} \textit{Near any $G$-eigenvector on $\{K=1\}$, the stochastic dynamics generates a restoring drift towards $K=1$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:restoring}): $K=1$ is a \emph{locally} self-consistent locus. Whether global attractivity holds remains open (Open Problem~5 in \S\ref{sec:discussion}).}

\medskip

The auxiliary linear system $\dot{x}=(J_{G}-d_{c}I)x$ has eigenvalues $0$ and $-2d_{c}$ at critical damping (from $\mathrm{spec}(J_{G})=\{\pm d_{c}\}$ at $d=d_{c}$). Theorem~\ref{thm:restoring} concerns the nonlinear system $\dot{x}=(J_{G}-d_{c}I)\nabla V$, $V=\tfrac{1}{2}(K{-}1)^2$, where departure from $K{=}1$ generates a penalty~$V$. The choice $D=d_{c}I$ is the unique marginally stable isotropic damping: the minimum isotropic dissipation stabilising $K{=}1$, and yielding the most explicit restoring-rate formula (eq.~\eqref{eq:kappa}).

\begin{theorem}[Local Effective Restoring Rate]\label{thm:restoring}
Let $G$ be a $2\times 2$ Lorentzian metric, $D=d_{c}I$, and $J_{G}=\alpha G^{-1}J$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:unique}). Define $K(x)=x^{\top}Gx$, $V=\tfrac{1}{2}(K-1)^{2}$, with drift $\dot{x}=(J_{G}-d_{c}I)\nabla V$. Then for all $x\in\mathbb{R}^{2}$:
\begin{equation}
\dot{K} = 4(K-1)\bigl[\alpha\,x^{\top}(JG)\,x - d_{c}\,x^{\top}G^{2}x\bigr].\label{eq:Kdot}
\end{equation}
At any $x_{*}\in\{K=1\}$ with $Gx_{*}=\lambda x_{*}$, the term $x_{*}^{\top}JGx_{*}=0$, so
\begin{equation}
\dot{K}\big|_{x=x_{*}} = -4\,d_{c}\,x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}\cdot(K-1).\label{eq:Kdot_eigen}
\end{equation}
If, in addition, the base point is normalised by $x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}=1$ (i.e., $x_*$ is a unit-eigenvalue $G$-eigenvector), then
\begin{equation}
\dot{K} = -\kappa_{K}(K-1)+o(K-1),\qquad \kappa_{K}=4\,d_{c}.\label{eq:kappa}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$\dot{K}=2x^{\top}G\dot{x}=4(K-1)\,x^{\top}G(J_{G}-d_{c}I)Gx$. Since $GJ_{G}=\alpha J$, right-multiplying by $G$ gives $GJ_{G}G=\alpha(JG)$, yielding~\eqref{eq:Kdot}. At a $G$-eigenvector, $x_{*}^{\top}JGx_{*}=\lambda\,x_{*}^{\top}Jx_{*}=0$ by skew-symmetry.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Base-point dependence]
The local effective restoring rate is $\kappa_{K}(x_{*})=4d_{c}\cdot x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}$; it depends on the base point. For the Rindler metric, $x_{*}=(0,1)$ is the unique $G$-eigenvector on $\{K{=}1\}$ with $x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}=1$. The factor~$4=2\times 2$ reflects two sources: the quadratic form~$K$ and the quadratic penalty~$V$; each contributes a factor of~$2$ upon differentiation.
\end{remark}

\subsection*{Two classes of fluctuation on $\{K=1\}$}

Adding isotropic additive white noise (It\^{o} convention) to Law~II gives $dx=(J_{G}-D)\nabla V\,dt+\sigma\,dW_{t}$, where $W_{t}$ is a standard $\mathbb{R}^{2}$-valued Wiener process and $\sigma>0$ is constant. At $D=d_{c}I$ (critical damping), two geometrically distinct classes arise:

\medskip\noindent
\textit{Class~A} (thermodynamic): fluctuations of $K$ perpendicular to $\{K=1\}$ --- departures from metric self-consistency --- governed by restoring rate $\kappa_{K}(x_{*})=4d_{c}\,x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}$; the conditional distribution $P(K|x)$ at a fixed base point is Gaussian with variance $\sigma^{2}/(2d_{c})$, as follows from the standard stationary distribution of the one-dimensional OU process~\cite{Gardiner1985}. Although both the restoring rate and the $K$-space noise amplitude depend on $x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}$, this factor cancels in the OU variance.

\medskip\noindent
\textit{Class~B} (kinematic; thermodynamically inert here): free tangential diffusion along $\{K=1\}$ in the boost direction. For the Rindler metric $G=\mathrm{diag}(-(\kappa\ell)^{2},1)$, $\{K=1\}$ is the hyperbola $-(\kappa\ell)^{2}x_{1}^{2}+x_{2}^{2}=1$; motion along it generates Lorentz boosts, changing the inertial observer but not the thermodynamic state. The Hessian of $V$ restricted to $\{K=1\}$ is rank-one with null space containing this tangent, confirming that Class~B carries no restoring force and no heat.


\section{Spacetime thermodynamics (Route~A)}\label{sec:thermo}

Every external identification is marked explicitly.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Sign convention.} In Route~A we use the spacetime-sign convention $G=\mathrm{diag}(-(\kappa\ell)^{2},1)$ for direct comparison with Rindler geometry. Statements depending only on $\det G$, $d_{c}$, or the hyperbolic spectrum are invariant under the overall sign change. Statements invoking Axiom~T as positive temporal cost are to be read in the cost-sign convention $G_{\mathrm{cost}}=-G=\mathrm{diag}((\kappa\ell)^{2},-1)$, used explicitly in \S~\ref{sec:singularity}. Both conventions have $\det G=-(\kappa\ell)^{2}$ and give the same $d_{c}$.

As a concrete realisation, we apply the framework to the Rindler metric
$G=\mathrm{diag}(-(\kappa\ell)^{2},1)$
\textup{[external: $G$ is the standard Rindler metric from GR, not derived from Law~I]},
where $\kappa$ is the surface gravity and $\ell$ the proper distance
from the horizon. Equation~\eqref{eq:dc} gives $d_{c}=\alpha/(\kappa\ell)$ (i.e., $\alpha/\sqrt{|g_{00}|}$ with $g_{00}=-(\kappa\ell)^{2}$ the temporal component of the Rindler metric). The expression for $d_{c}$ uses the diagonal/isotropic threshold described in \S~\ref{sec:stability}; for general $H_{*}$, the threshold may shift. With $T_{H}=\hbar\kappa/(2\pi)$~\cite{Hawking},
\begin{equation}
d_{c}\cdot T_{H} = \frac{\alpha}{\kappa\ell}\cdot\frac{\hbar\kappa}{2\pi} = \frac{\alpha\hbar}{2\pi\ell},\label{eq:nearhorizon}
\end{equation}
exhibiting exact $\kappa$-cancellation. Hence the result $d_{c}\cdot T_{H}=\alpha\hbar/(2\pi\ell)$ recovers the Tolman temperature~\cite{Tolman} $T_{\mathrm{tol}}=\alpha\hbar/(2\pi\ell)$, depending only on the proper distance $\ell$ from the horizon, not on the surface gravity $\kappa$.

\subsection*{Clausius relation (from OU dynamics) within Class~A}

In this subsection we work within the Sekimoto--Seifert stochastic thermodynamics framework~\cite{Sekimoto}; within that external framework, the only additional external identification is $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$.

Within Class~A (fixed inertial observer), the $K$-dynamics near $K=1$ is a one-dimensional Ornstein--Uhlenbeck process with stationary variance
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Var}(K)\approx\frac{\sigma^{2}}{2d_{c}}.\label{eq:var}
\end{equation}
With this stationary variance, the FDT form $\sigma_{K}^{2}=2\kappa_{K}T_{\mathrm{eff}}$ defines the effective temperature $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=\sigma^{2}/(2d_{c})$, consistent with Law~II and Theorem~\ref{thm:restoring}. The Fokker--Planck equation yields a Boltzmann stationary distribution $\rho\propto\exp(-V/T_{\mathrm{eff}})$; the Clausius relation $\delta Q=T_{\mathrm{eff}}\,dS$ then follows from detailed balance for the OU process, with
\[
\delta Q := dV + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma_{K}^{2}\,dt,\qquad \sigma_{K}=2\sigma\|Gx_{*}\|.
\]
(The heat definition follows the Sekimoto--Seifert stochastic thermodynamics convention~\cite{Sekimoto}.)
A derivation of the identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$, possibly from a KMS condition~\cite{Haag,Connes1994}, remains open.

\begin{proposition}[Local Clausius Equivalence]\label{prop:clausius}
Under local thermal equilibrium (in the KMS sense of the preceding remark) and the identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$, the Clausius relation $\delta Q=T_{\mathrm{tol}}\,dS$ is equivalent to
\begin{equation}
\sigma^{2} = 2\,d_{c}\cdot T_{\mathrm{tol}} = 2\,d_{c}^{2}\cdot T_{H}.\label{eq:noise}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
$(\Rightarrow)$: Setting $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ gives $\sigma^{2}/(2d_{c})=d_{c}\cdot T_{H}$, hence $\sigma^{2}=2d_{c}^{2}\cdot T_{H}$.
$(\Leftarrow)$: $\sigma^{2}=2d_{c}\cdot T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ gives $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=\sigma^{2}/(2d_{c})=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$, hence Clausius holds.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Assumptions of Proposition~\ref{prop:clausius}]
The assumptions entering Proposition~\ref{prop:clausius} are:
(i)~the OU approximation near $K=1$ ($|K-1|\ll 1$, equivalently $T_{\mathrm{eff}}\ll 1$);
(ii)~the Sekimoto--Seifert heat convention $\delta Q := dV + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma_{K}^{2}\,dt$;
(iii)~local detailed balance for the OU process;
(iv)~the external identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$;
together with the algebraic Tolman identity $T_{\mathrm{tol}}=d_{c}T_{H}$ ($\kappa$-factors cancel in eq.~\eqref{eq:nearhorizon}).
\end{remark}

\subsection*{Position-dependent temperature and It\^{o} corrections}

Applying It\^{o}'s formula to $K=x^{\top}Gx$ gives an additional drift $\sigma^{2}\,\mathrm{tr}(G)\,dt$. This shifts the mean of $K$ but not its variance: at any $G$-eigenvector base point, the FDT ratio $\sigma_{K}^{2}/(2\kappa_{K})$ reduces to $\sigma^{2}/(2d_{c})$ regardless of $x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}$. Hence~\eqref{eq:var} and $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=\sigma^{2}/(2d_{c})$ hold for any Lorentzian $G$.

The $K$-space noise amplitude $\sigma_{K}(x)=2\sigma\|Gx\|$ varies along $\{K=1\}$, yielding a position-dependent temperature (eq.~\eqref{eq:Tloc}):
\begin{equation}
T_{\mathrm{loc}}(x) := \frac{\sigma_{K}(x)^{2}}{2\kappa_{K}(x_{*})} = \frac{\sigma^{2}\,x^{\top}G^{2}x}{2d_{c}\,x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}}.\label{eq:Tloc}
\end{equation}
This position dependence should not be read as a Class~B heat fluctuation: variation along the boost orbit changes the observer/position assignment, while Class~B remains thermodynamically inert and carries no heat.

\begin{remark}[Stability constraint]
For macroscopic black holes, $T_{\mathrm{tol}}\ll d_{c}$; setting $D=T_{\mathrm{tol}}I$ renders $K=1$ unstable. Stability requires $D\ge d_{c}I$; critical damping $D=d_{c}I$ is the unique marginally stable choice.
\end{remark}

Since every local Rindler horizon admits the standard Rindler approximation~\cite{Jacobson} with $G=\mathrm{diag}(-(\kappa\ell)^{2},1)$ (where $\kappa$ and $\ell$ vary by location), Proposition~\ref{prop:clausius} applies to each such horizon, yielding the Clausius relation locally.
Together with Jacobson's argument~\cite{Jacobson}, this supplies the local Clausius input needed for the Einstein equations. The remaining thermodynamic input needed to invoke Jacobson's argument is $S\propto A$.

\section{Eigenvalue self-consistency (Route~B: direct algebraic)}\label{sec:field}

The eigenvalue self-consistency condition $\sigma_2^2\,\Box\ln\sigma_i=1$ is a motivated field-level analogue of $K{=}1$, introduced as a conjecture rather than derived from the framework's axioms; Theorem~\ref{thm:field} establishes its algebraic equivalence to $R_{\mu\nu}=0$ in the spherically symmetric sector, with no thermodynamic input.

Consider the static spherically symmetric metric
\begin{equation}\label{eq:metric}
  ds^2=-f(r)\,dt^2+f(r)^{-1}dr^2+r^2\,d\Omega^2,
\end{equation}
with symplectic eigenvalues $\sigma_1:=r\sqrt{f}$, $\sigma_2:=r$
(on each local Rindler wedge, $d_{c}\sigma_1=\alpha$, connecting $\sigma_1$ to the $2\times 2$ symplectic eigenvalue of~\S3),
and d'Alembertian
$\Box\phi=r^{-2}\partial_r(r^2f\,\partial_r\phi)$ (the radial d'Alembertian, exact for $r$-dependent $\phi$; primes denote $d/dr$).

\begin{definition}[Eigenvalue self-consistency functionals]\label{def:esc}
\begin{align}
  K_1 &:= \sigma_2^2\,\Box\ln\sigma_1,\label{eq:K1def}\\
  K_2 &:= \sigma_2^2\,\Box\ln\sigma_2.\label{eq:K2def}
\end{align}
\end{definition}

\noindent
The eigenvalue self-consistency condition $\sigma_2^2\,\Box\ln\sigma=1$, imposed on each eigenvalue ($K_1=1$, eq.~\eqref{eq:K1def}, and $K_2=1$, eq.~\eqref{eq:K2def}), states that the d'Alembertian of each log-eigenvalue equals the angular curvature cost $1/\sigma_2^2$. This is a motivated field-level analogue of the point-level balance $K{=}1$: each eigenvalue must propagate at the rate set by the angular sector, in the same way that $K=1$ requires the quadratic cost form $K$ to reproduce its own normalisation.

Expanding (direct computation using $\Box\ln\sigma_1=\Box\ln r+\tfrac{1}{2}\Box\ln f$):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:K1exp}
  K_1 = f+2rf'+\tfrac{r^2}{2}f'',
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:K2exp}
  K_2 = rf'+f = \frac{d(rf)}{dr}.
\end{equation}
(For $K_2$: $\Box\ln r = (1/r^2)d/dr[r^2f/r]=(rf'+f)/r^2$,
so $r^2\Box\ln r = rf'+f$.)

\begin{lemma}[Scalar Curvature Formula]\label{lem:R}
$R=-2\,\Box\ln\sigma_1+2/r^2$ for~\eqref{eq:metric}.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
$R=-f''-4f'/r+2(1{-}f)/r^2$.
$\Box\ln\sigma_1=f/r^2+2f'/r+f''/2$ (from $K_1=r^2\Box\ln\sigma_1$ and eq.~\eqref{eq:K1exp}).
Then $-2\Box\ln\sigma_1+2/r^2=R$.\qed
\end{proof}

\noindent
An immediate computation gives $R_{tt}=\tfrac{f}{r^2}(K_1-K_2)$, so $K_1=K_2$ implies $R_{tt}=0$. Substituting $K_1=r^2\Box\ln\sigma_1$ into Lemma~\ref{lem:R} gives $R=2(1-K_1)/r^2$; hence $K_1{=}1\Leftrightarrow R{=}0$ and
$K_2{=}1\Leftrightarrow R_{\theta\theta}{=}0$
(since $R_{\theta\theta}=1{-}f{-}rf'=1{-}K_2$).
Equivalently, $K_2{=}1$ states that the Misner--Sharp mass
$m=\frac{r}{2}(1{-}f)$ is constant: $dm/dr=(1{-}K_2)/2=0$.

\begin{theorem}[Field-Level Equivalence]\label{thm:field}
For~\eqref{eq:metric} with $f\in C^2(0,\infty)$ and $K_1,K_2$ as in Definition~\ref{def:esc}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:equiv}
  \sigma_2^2\,\Box\ln\sigma_i=1\;\;\text{for }i=1,2
  \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad R_{\mu\nu}=0,
\end{equation}
where $R_{\mu\nu}=0$ are the vacuum Einstein field equations~\cite{Einstein1915,Hilbert1915}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$(\Rightarrow)$\;
$K_2{=}1$ gives $R_{\theta\theta}{=}0$.
$K_1{-}K_2=rf'+\frac{r^2}{2}f''=0$ (from eqs.~\eqref{eq:K1exp}--\eqref{eq:K2exp}) gives $f''=-2f'/r$; hence $R_{tt}{=}0$ (by the observation $R_{tt}=\tfrac{f}{r^2}(K_1-K_2)$ above). (For this metric $R_{tt}=-f^{2}R_{rr}$~\cite{Wald1984}, so $R_{tt}=0\Leftrightarrow R_{rr}=0$.)

$(\Leftarrow)$\;
$R_{\theta\theta}{=}0$ gives $K_2{=}1$.
$R_{tt}{=}0$ gives $f''=-2f'/r$; substituting:
$K_1=f+2rf'-rf'=f+rf'=K_2=1$.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
The condition $K_1{=}1$ is the second-order linear ODE
$f+2rf'+\frac{r^2}{2}f''=1$, equivalent to $R{=}0$, with general solution
$f=1-C_1/r-C_2/r^2$; $K_2{=}1$ forces $C_2{=}0$,
recovering the Schwarzschild solution uniquely, in agreement with Birkhoff's theorem.
In the weak field ($f=1+\varepsilon\varphi$),
$K_2{=}1$ gives $\varphi=C/r$ (Newton) in one step;
$K_1{=}1$ is automatically satisfied at linear order,
so its independent content is purely nonlinear (strong-field).
Any future observational test distinguishing the eigenvalue principle from standard GR would therefore require strong-gravity data.
\end{corollary}

\begin{remark}[Non-vacuum and $\Lambda$]
From the Einstein $G_{tt}$ component,
$\rho = (1{-}K_2)/(8\pi r^2) = (1-\sigma_2^2\,\Box\ln\sigma_2)/(8\pi\sigma_2^2)$:
matter is the angular eigenvalue's failure to self-consistently
propagate, measured per unit area.
For Schwarzschild--de~Sitter, $K_2=1{-}\Lambda r^2$ and
$K_1=1{-}2\Lambda r^2$.
Both relations \emph{use} the Einstein equations;
they are cost rewritings, not independent derivations
(in particular, $8\pi$ is inherited from GR).
The cosmological constant shifts both eigenvalue conditions away from unity; its value is not predicted.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[General metrics and polarization]
The extension to general metrics is not yet complete: the step connecting the eigenvalue condition to $R_{\mu\nu}v^\mu v^\nu=0$ on each null plane requires the Gauss--Codazzi relations for null hypersurfaces, and remains open.
For a general metric, one applies $\sigma_2^2\Box\ln\sigma_i=1$ on
each null direction.
The \emph{polarization identity} guarantees that if
$R_{\mu\nu}v^\mu v^\nu=0$ for every null~$v^\mu$ and $R=0$,
then $R_{\mu\nu}=0$.
(Proof: in an orthonormal frame, taking $v=e_0\pm e_k$ gives
$R_{0k}=0$, $R_{00}+R_{kk}=0$; mixed spatial components vanish
by comparing $v=(e_0\pm\frac{e_i+e_j}{\sqrt{2}})$ and $v=(e_0\pm\frac{e_i-e_j}{\sqrt{2}})$, giving $R_{ij}=0$ for $i\neq j$; the trace $R=0$ then
forces $R_{00}=0$.)
\end{remark}

\section{Singularity exclusion and resolution scale}\label{sec:singularity}

The following results concern the \emph{domain} of the framework rather than the physical existence of singularities: where $\sigma_1=0$, the $K{=}1$ locus is empty and the framework is undefined, not incorrect.

\begin{proposition}[Positivity from Axiom~T]\label{prop:sigma1}
Among the three axioms, only Axiom~T is used here;
Axioms~R and~E are needed only in \S~\ref{sec:lawI} to establish $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$.
Axiom~T implies $\sigma_1>0$ wherever $K{=}1$ holds.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For the $(t,r)$ wedge (and by the same argument for any $2\times 2$ Lorentzian $G$), $G=\mathrm{diag}(\sigma_1^2,-1)$: Axiom~T gives $G_{00}=\sigma_1^2>0$; contrast: $g_{00}=-f<0$ for the spacetime metric; the sign convention here differs from \S\ref{sec:thermo} but gives the same $d_{c}$, since $\det G=-\sigma_1^2$ in both cases (only $|\det G|$ and the positive-cost temporal direction enter the argument). If $\sigma_1=0$ then $G_{00}=0$, violating Axiom~T.\qed
\end{proof}

\noindent
A direct consequence: when $\sigma_1=0$, $K=-x_2^2\le 0$ and $K{=}1$ has no solutions. The Schwarzschild singularity ($\sigma_1\to 0$ as $r\to 0$) therefore lies outside the domain of the theory --- the framework is undefined there, not incorrect.

\begin{proposition}[Intrinsic resolution scale]\label{prop:domain}
In Planck units ($\hbar=\ell_P=1$), the $K{=}1$ framework is self-consistent only for
$\sigma_1>\sigma_{1,c}:=\hbar/(4\pi)=\ell_P/(4\pi)$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
\textup{[External identification: $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$,
as in \S~\ref{sec:thermo}; all other steps are internal.]}
Near the horizon, $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}=\hbar\kappa/(2\pi\sqrt{f})$
(Tolman redshift factor; cf.~\eqref{eq:nearhorizon} with $\alpha=1$). With $\sigma_1=r\sqrt{f}$ and $r\approx 2M$, $\kappa=1/(4M)$ near the Schwarzschild horizon:
\begin{equation*}
T_{\mathrm{eff}}\approx\frac{\hbar\cdot(1/(4M))}{2\pi\cdot\sigma_1/(2M)}=\frac{\hbar}{4\pi\sigma_1}.
\end{equation*}
$\mathrm{Var}(K)\approx T_{\mathrm{eff}}$ (from eq.~\eqref{eq:var} and $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$, in the OU approximation); for $K{=}1$ to be a
well-defined equilibrium, $\mathrm{Var}(K)<1$, hence
$\sigma_1>\hbar/(4\pi)$. This is a self-consistent bound: the OU approximation must be valid, i.e., $\mathrm{Var}(K)<1$.\qed
\end{proof}

\noindent
Below $\sigma_{1,c}$, thermal fluctuations of $K$ exceed its
mean value and the semiclassical metric loses its classical meaning.
This is a \emph{soft} bound---analogous to classical mechanics at action~$\sim\hbar$: the OU approximation requires $\mathrm{Var}(K)<1$, which forces $\sigma_1>\sigma_{1,c}$; in that regime $\mathrm{Var}(K)<1$ is satisfied. Thus the bound is a self-consistency condition for the OU/semiclassical regime, not an independent derivation of a hard cutoff.

Both the self-consistency bound (Proposition~\ref{prop:domain}) and the canonical conjugacy argument of Open Problem~2 give $\sigma_{1,\min}\sim O(\ell_P)$, confirming the Planck scale as the natural resolution limit of the framework.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection*{Two routes: one derivation, one equivalence theorem}

Route~A reaches $G_{\mu\nu}=8\pi T_{\mu\nu}$ via stochastic dynamics and the Jacobson argument (\S\ref{sec:thermo}); Route~B establishes $R_{\mu\nu}=0$ algebraically, with no thermodynamic input, in the spherically symmetric sector (\S\ref{sec:field}).

The Jacobson argument~\cite{Jacobson} reconstructs the Einstein equations from three inputs: Lorentzian signature, the Clausius relation on every local Rindler horizon, and $S=A/4$. Route~A supplies the first two: signature is forced by realizability after identifying $G$ with the signed leading quadratic form, and its equivalence with $d_{c}>0$ under isotropic linearisation is given by Theorem~\ref{thm:equivalence}; Clausius follows on each horizon (Proposition~\ref{prop:clausius}, given $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$). The sole remaining external input is $S\propto A$. Patching across horizon directions requires no additional assumptions (see $(1{+}3)$-dimensional extension below).

The two routes meet in the vacuum sector: Route~B establishes $R_{\mu\nu}=0$ directly, while Route~A recovers the same conclusion via $G_{\mu\nu}=8\pi T_{\mu\nu}$ with $T_{\mu\nu}=0$.

\subsection*{Physical content of Axiom~R}

Physically, Axiom~R expresses the persistence of configurations through arbitrarily low-cost spatially nontrivial displacements: configurations can drift in spatial directions at vanishing cost-to-magnitude ratio, while the temporal direction (Axiom~T) remains expensive. This asymmetry --- cheap spatial drift, costly temporal drift --- is the cost-theoretic content of Lorentzian causal structure. The technical derivation that Axioms~R, E, T together with nondegeneracy force $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$ is carried out in~\cite{Realizability}.

\subsection*{The $K{=}1$ self-referential structure}

The condition $K{=}1$ is self-referential: $G$ defines $K$, while $K{=}1$ fixes the normalisation against which deviations are measured. The restoring potential $V=\frac{1}{2}(K-1)^2$ is the leading smooth penalty for departures from this locus. At the field level, the conjectural analogue is $\sigma_2^2\Box\ln\sigma_i=1$.

\subsection*{Stages}

The framework proceeds in four steps: the Realizability axioms fix the Lorentzian type of $G$; $K{=}1$ supplies a local self-consistency condition; Route~A connects the resulting OU dynamics to Clausius under $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$; and Route~B gives an algebraic spherical equivalence to $R_{\mu\nu}=0$. The quantum and global-attractor questions remain open.

\subsection*{Extension to $(1{+}3)$ dimensions}

In $(1{+}3)$ dimensions, $\{K{=}1\}\cong H^3$ carries $S^2$ cross-sections with area $4\pi\sigma_1^2$ when $\alpha=1$ and $\sigma_1=\kappa\ell$. This relates the same symplectic invariant to the Bekenstein--Hawking area variable~\cite{Bekenstein}, while the coefficient $1/4$ still relies on the usual Hawking temperature~\cite{Hawking}/first-law input. Two structural changes remain: $d_{c}>0\Leftrightarrow\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$ weakens beyond $2\times 2$ (Lorentzian signature becomes necessary but not sufficient), and $J_G$ requires a symplectic pairing choice when $\dim(\mathrm{Skew}_4)=6$.

\subsection*{Departure from GENERIC}

For Lorentzian~$G$, $J_G$ is $G$-antisymmetric but not Euclidean antisymmetric, so the framework departs from the standard GENERIC setting~\cite{GENERIC}.

\subsection*{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
\item Route~B is proved for spherical symmetry only; general extension requires null-surface Gauss--Codazzi. \textit{(scope)}
\item Route~A requires $S\propto A$. \textit{(external input)}
\item No deviation from GR is predicted in vacuum. \textit{(predictive gap)}
\item The $\sigma_1$ bound is soft, not hard. \textit{(strength)}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Open problems}

\begin{enumerate}
\item Complete Route~B for general metrics (Gauss--Codazzi).
\item Prove $\sigma_{1,\min}\geq\hbar/(4\pi)$ (hard bound) from $K$--$\varphi$ canonical conjugacy, where $\varphi$ is the boost angle along $\{K{=}1\}$.
\item Derive the $8\pi$ factor from the cost framework (non-vacuum Route~B).
\item Identify testable strong-field predictions.
\item Prove global attractivity of $K=1$: show that for any $x_0\in\mathbb{R}^{2}$,
the trajectory of $\dot{x}=(J_G-d_{c}I)\nabla V$ with $V=\tfrac{1}{2}(K-1)^2$
satisfies $K(x(t))\to 1$ as $t\to\infty$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Conclusion}

General relativity prescribes Lorentzian signature; the present work derives it from a cost asymmetry and shows that the resulting metric locally enforces its own normalisation. At the point level, within the stochastic thermodynamics framework, the Clausius relation follows once the local effective temperature is identified with the Tolman temperature. In the spherically symmetric sector, the eigenvalue self-consistency principle is algebraically equivalent to the vacuum Einstein equations, connecting the $K{=}1$ language to the field level. Axiom~T leaves curvature singularities (where $\sigma_1$ degenerates) outside the theory's domain, where the framework is undefined rather than incorrect, and the OU variance sets a soft Planck-scale resolution limit. What remains prescribed is the cost asymmetry itself: why one direction carries positive cost while others do not. This is Riemann's question in economic form.
